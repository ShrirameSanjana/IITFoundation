## 7. Biotechnology
Foundation
## (CONCEPTMAP)
Biotechnology allows the manipulation and transfer of selected genes between different organisms, species, genera and phyla. Once transferred, these genes may be transferred to offspring of the modified individual through normal reproductive processes. Genetic engineering has resulted in the production of Genetically Modified Organisms (GMOs). Genetically Modified Organisms (GMOs) can therefore be defined as organisms in which the genetic material has been altered in a way that it does not occur naturally.
## PRINCIPLES OF BIOTECHNOLOGY
The science of biotechnology is based mainly on two core technologies. These are :
1. Genetic engineering which refers to artificial synthesis, isolation, modification, combination, addition and repair of the genetic material (DNA) to introduce it into the host organism so as to alter its phenotype as per the human needs.
Genetic engineering was started by Paul Berg, Herbert Boyer and Stanley Cohen, (1973) who generated first recombinant DNA molecule by combining a gene from a SV 40 virus with the DNA of bacteriaphage lambda.
Collins and Hohn (1979) developed a method for obtaining recombinant plasmids (having a small piece of lambda DNA) packed into the protective coat of $\lambda$ phage and then transduced into E. coli.
2. Biochemical engineering processes to enable the growth of desired microbe/eukaryotic cell in large quantities in a sterile medium (Tissue culture technique) for the manufacture and multiplication of biotechnological products (antibiotics, vaccines, enzymes, medicines, hormones, etc.)
## Recombinant DNA Technology
Recombinant DNA technology, popularly known as 'genetic engineering' is a stream of biotechnology which deals with the manipulation of genetic material by man in vitro. It involves a number of techniques where a DNA sequence (with desired gene) is cut into fragments and then introduced into host cells with or without the help of carriers (called vectors) to alter its phenotype, as per the requirement.
## Basic Tools of Recombinant DNA Technology
1. Enzyme :
A number of specific kind of enzymes are employed in genetic engineering. These include lysing, cleaving, synthesising, and joining enzymes.
(i) Lysing enzymes: These are used to open up the cells to get DNA for genetic experiments. Lysozyme is commonly used to dissolve the bacterial cell wall.
(ii) Cleaving enzyme: These are used to break DNA molecule. They are of 3 kinds-
(a) Exonucleases, which cut off nucleotides from 5' or 3' ends of DNA molecule;
(b) Endonucleases, which cleave DNA duplex at any point except the ends; and
(c) Restriction endonucleases, which cleaves DNA duplex at specific points called restriction sites in such a way that fragments produced after cleavage either have single-stranded free ends or unpaired free bases on the free ends. The single-stranded free ends are called 'sticky ends' because they can join similar complementary ends of DNA fragment from some other source. The free ends with no unpaired bases are called blunt ends. Restriction endonucleases are also called molecular scissors or a chemical scalpel. These enzymes are used in genetic engineering to form recombinant DNA molecules.
They are found in bacteria and their function is to recognise and cleave foreign DNA such as DNA of infecting virus in order to provide protection against them. Recognition site of restriction enzymes is a sequence of 4 to 8 base pairs that reads same on the two strands ( $5^{\prime}$ to $3^{\prime}$ direction or from $3^{\prime}$ to $5^{\prime}$ direction). Such sequences are called palindromes. For example, restriction endonuclease EcoRI is found in the bacterium Escherichia coli which recognises the base sequence GAATTC in DNA duplex and cleaves its strands at different positions i.e., between G and A producing sticky ends. EcoRV restriction enzyme recognises the base sequence GATATC and cleaves both the strands exactly at the opposing nucleotide leaving blunt ends on the resulting DNA fragments.
Fig.: Action of restriction enzymes (a) EcoRI producing sticky ends (b) EcoRV producing blunt ends
(iii) Synthesising enzymes: These play a role in the synthesis of DNA strands complementary to existing DNA or RNA templates. They are of 2 types: (a) Reverse transcriptases, which helps in the synthesis of complementary DNA strands from an RNA template; (b) DNA polymerases, which aid in the synthesis of complementary DNA strands from DNA templates.
(iv) Joining enzyme: These enzymes help in joining two DNA fragments. For example, DNA ligase from Escherichia coli is used to join DNA fragments by forming a phosphodiester bond. This enzyme is called molecular glue.
(v) End modification enzyme: These enzymes make changes to the ends of DNA molecules such as alkaline phosphatases cuts off phosphate group from the $5^{\prime}$ end of linearised circular DNA to check its recircularisation.
## 2. Cloning Vectors (Vehicle DNA)
The DNA used as a carrier for transferring a fragment of foreign DNA into a suitable host is called vehicle DNA. It is also called cloning vector or gene carrier. The desired gene is introduced into a vector wherein recombinant DNA (r-DNA) is formed. The vector carrying r-DNA divides in the host independently, thereby forming several copies of r-DNA. Five types of DNA are used as vehicles : plasmid DNA, bacteriophage DNA, DNA of plant and animal viruses, transposons (jumping genes), and artificial DNA. The first two are more commonly used vectors.
(i) Plasmids: Plasmids are small, double stranded, closed circular, symbiotic DNA molecules that occur naturally in bacteria, outside the bacterial chromosome (extra chromosomal DNA). A bacterial cell may possess one or many copies of one or more plasmids. They are inherited from parent bacterial cell to daughter cells and have capability of self replication in the cytoplasm of bacterial cell. The circular molecule of DNA can be broken to yield a linear molecule which passes from one bacterial cell to another (transformation). There are many kinds of plasmids which differ from one another in size and in composition of genes. They vary in size from 2 kb to more than 400 kb .
(ii) Bacteriophage vectors: Bacteriophages (or phages) are viruses that infect bacteria by introducing their DNA into the bacterial cells. The injected DNA molecule of the virus integrates into the bacterial chromosome. This viral DNA multiplies with the bacterial chromosome as prophage. The prophage may dissociate from the bacterial chromosome and assemble. They can be introduced into host cells by a process called transformation (discussed later) into a number of phages which burst out of the cell and reinfect the neighbouring cells (Lytic pathway). This property of bacteriophages to transfer their DNA into specific bacterial cells where they get integrated and multiply along with bacterial chromosome gave scientists the idea that specially designed phage vectors (containing foreign DNA) would prove best tools for gene cloning. Moreover, they are more advantageous than plasmid vectors because - (i) They can be used for large DNA fragments; and (ii) They can easily be detected at the time of cloning experiments.
## 3. Passenger DNA
It is the DNA which is transferred from one organism into another by combining it with the vehicle DNA. Three types of DNA are used as passengers : complementary, synthetic and random.
(i) Complementary or Copy DNA (cDNA): It is synthesised on RNA templates with the help of reverse transcriptase enzyme and necessary nucleotides. The DNA strand is isolated from the hybrid RNA-DNA complex by using enzyme RNase H which digests the RNA strand from DNA-RNA duplex. A complementary DNA strand is then synthesised on the isolated single-stranded DNA template with the help of DNA polymerase. The cDNA duplex so formed can be joined to vehicle DNA for introduction into a host cell.
Fig.: cDNA synthesis
(ii) Synthetic DNA (sDNA): It is synthesised with the help of DNA polymerases on DNA template or from free deoxyribonucleotides without a template.
Artificial Synthesis of DNA on Template: In 1961, Kornberg and his coworkers synthesised DNA from a mixture of deoxyribonucleoside triphosphates, DNA polymerase enzyme, metal ions and a segment of viral DNA to act as a template and a primer. The synthetic DNA showed biological activity. When introduced into bacteria, this DNA coded for the formation of virus particles similar to the one from which template DNA was taken.
Artificial Synthesis of DNA without a Template: Hargobind Khorana and his colleagues produced the first synthetic DNA in 1965 by purely chemical means. They synthesised the gene coding for yeast alanine tRNA which needed only 77 base pairs. Unfortunately, this gene did not function. Khorana and his associates in 1979 synthesised the gene coding for tyrosine-tRNA of E. coli. This gene had 207 nucleotide pairs. It functioned in the bacterium E. coli when introduced in combination with a phage DNA. Genes for hormones somatotropin and insulin have also been artificially synthesised.
(iii) Random DNA: It refers to small fragments formed by breaking a chromosome of an organism with the help of restriction enzyme.
A team of 17 researchers at the J. Craig Venter Institute has created the largest man-made DNA structure by synthesising and assembling the 5,82,970 base pair genome of a bacterium, Mycoplasma genitalium. A chemical synthesiser joined together various short iterations of man-made adenine, cytosine, guanine and thymine that were then assembled into a working genome that can successfully produce the proteins that enable life. Using stretches of DNA, known as cassettes, roughly 1,000 base-pairs in length, the researchers assembled a simplified version of M. mycoides genome from scratch. The final synthetic genome was then inserted into an existing Mycoplasma capricolum cell. The synthetic cell then went on to behave as a M. mycoides, producing proteins from the instructions encoded by the synthetic genome and even dividing and growing.
## Methods of gene transfer:
The method of introducing the recombinant DNA into host cell depends on its type.
There are two distinct techniques for introducing foreign genetic material into a host cell genome - indirect and direct transfer.
- Indirect transfer:
The indirect transfer through a vector requires - (i) selection and isolation of the desirable fragment(s) of DNA which contains gene sequence(s) that needs to be cloned known as insert; (ii) Generation of recombinant DNA( $r$ DNA) molecule by insertion of these inserts (DNA fragments) into a vector that can replicate within a host cell (Recombinant DNA $=$ Vector + insert). For instance, insulin gene is cut off from rat's DNA and linked to a bacterial plasmid, gives recombinant DNA. (iii) Introduction of the $r$ DNA molecules into host cells. The process of transferring a DNA into a prokaryotic host cell is called transformation. In this method, bacterial cells are first treated with a specific concentration of a divalent cation such as calcium to create transient pores in the cell wall. Then the cells with recombinant DNA are incubated on ice followed by placing them briefly at $42^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ (heat shock) in order to force recombinant DNA into the host cells.
- Direct transfer:
The direct introduction of DNA involves -
(i) Electroporation, i.e., application of electric impulses to change the porosity of protoplasts so that they directly imbibe the purified DNA.
(ii) Micro-injection, i.e., direct injection of DNA fragments into the nucleus of the host cell with the help of a micropipette.
(iii) Biolistic or gene gun i.e., bombarding cells with high velocity micro-particles of gold or tungsten coated with DNA to facilitate entry of the foreign genes.
(iv) Other methods of gene transfer are- liposome mediated gene transfer, calcium phosphate precipitation method, transformation by ultrasonication (DNA uptake by ultrasonic waves) etc.
Thus, genetic engineering can be defined as the generation of new combination of heritable material by the insertion of desired genes or DNA of the cell into any carrier system so as to allow their incorporation into a host organism.

## Process of Recombinant Technology
Recombinant DNA ( $r$ DNA) process involves a sequence of several steps:
## Step 1: Isolation of a genetic material (DNA)
In order to isolate the gene of interest, complete DNA should be isolated in pure form i.e., it should be free from other macromolecules such as proteins, lipids, carbohydrates and even RNA. DNA is enclosed within the membranes of cell and nucleus so the cell is opened to release DNA along with other macromolecules. The cells or tissues are treated with lysing enzymes such as lysozyme (to digest bacterial cell wall), cellulase (for digesting plant cell wall), and chitinase (to digest fungal cell wall). DNA is treated with ribonuclease and protease to remove RNA and protein, respectively. Other molecules can be removed by appropriate treatments and purified DNA is ultimately collected as fine threads by precipitation after the addition of chilled ethanol.
## Gene Library
A gene library is a collection of cloning vectors which contains the DNA fragments representing the entire genome.
Setting up of Gene Library: A chromosome of an organism is broken into small fragments comprising one or a few genes with the help of restriction endonucleases. Each fragment is joined to a suitable vehicle DNA, forming recombinant DNAs of different nature. These are then introduced into a host (bacterial, yeast, plant or animal) cell. The foreign DNA fragment replicates as the host cell divides. This produces a group of cells, each containing a foreign DNA fragment comprising one or a few genes. The daughter cells identical to the parent cell are referred to as a clone of cells. The process is called DNA cloning. Each clone contains a different fragment of the same foreign DNA. The various clones representing all the genes of an organism are known as gene library of that organism.
Complementary DNA (c-DNA) formed from an RNA template by using the enzyme reverse transcriptase may be cloned as explained above for setting up a gene library. The collection of c-DNA fragments is termed c-DNA library.
Use of Gene Library: From a gene library, it is possible to identify a clone containing the specific gene. This gene can be multiplied by growing the relevant clone in a culture. The base sequence in this gene can be found using different methods. From the base sequence, the sequence of amino acids in a polypeptide can be worked out on the basis of the triplet code.
## Step 2: Cutting at DNA at specific locations
Next step is the isolation of a gene of interest from purified DNA. To do this, purified DNA is incubated with a restriction enzyme. When cut, each fragment of DNA has an overhanging piece of single-stranded DNA on its ends called "sticky ends". Cloning vector is also treated with the same restriction enzyme, resulting in a single-stranded DNA molecule with sticky ends complementary to those found on the fragmented source DNA. Now, both the 'gene of interest' from the source DNA and the cut vector with space are mixed and ligase is added. This results in the preparation of recombinant DNA.
## Step 3 : Amplification of gene of interest using PCR
PCR stands for Polymerase Chain Reaction. It is the process of amplifying a desired gene of interest i.e., multiple copies of the gene (or DNA) of interest is synthesised in vitro (outside living cells). It involves two sets of primers (small chemically synthesised oligonucleotides that are complementary to the regions of DNA of interest) and the enzyme DNA polymerase [isolated from a bacterium, Thermus aquaticus]. This enzyme remains active during the high temperature induced denaturation of double stranded DNA.
PCR consists of three steps:
(i) Step 1 - Denaturation: In this step, reaction mixture is heated to $94^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ to separate the two strands of DNA. These separated DNA strands act as template.
(ii) Step 2 - Annealing: The temperature of reaction mixture is lowered to $50^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ to allow the primers to bind the complementary regions on the template DNA.
(iii) Step 3 - Extension: Now, the temperature is raised to $75^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ and DNA polymerase adds nucleotides complementary to template strand to the free hydroxyl end of each primer.
These steps are repeated in cyclic manner many times until the desired level of amplification is achieved.
The amplified fragment can now be used to ligate with a vector for further cloning.

## Gene Bank
Gene bank is a place for the storage of genetic material in the form of clones of known DNA fragments, seeds, spores and frozen spermatozoa, eggs and embryos. A gene bank provides materials for use in genetic engineering and breeding experiments, particularly concerned to the extinct species. Preserved human sperm, ova and embryos can also be used for fertility purposes in human females.
## Step 4 : Insertion of recombinant DNA into the host cell/organism
The vector containing the desired DNA sequence is next transferred to a suitable host cell or organism. There are several methods of introducing the ligated DNA into recipient cells.
## Host cell
In genetic engineering, a suitable host cell is required to obtain a large number of copies of specific DNA or to get the gene product (protein). Most commonly used host cells are bacterial cells, yeast cells, mammalian and plant cells. E. coli is the most commonly used bacterial cell as host. In addition to E. coli, other bacteria such as Bacillus, Pseudomonas and Streptomyces may be used as hosts for gene cloning experiments.
Fig.: Processes of Recombinant DNA Technology
## Step 5 : Obtaining the foreign gene product
When a piece of alien DNA in a cloning vector is transferred into a bacterial, plant or animal cell, the alien DNA gets multiplied or produces a desirable protein (when expressed under appropriate conditions).
The protein so obtained is called a recombinant protein. The cells harbouring cloned genes of interest may be grown on a small scale in the laboratory. The cultures may be used for extracting the desired protein and then purifying it by using different separation techniques.
To produce products in large quantities, bioreactors, where large volumes (100-1000 litres) of culture can be processed, are used. Thus, bioreactor is a vessel in which raw materials are biologically converted into specific products, individual enzymes, etc., using microbial plant, animal or human cells. A bioreactor provides the optimal conditions for achieving the desired product (temperature, pH , substrate, salts, vitamins, oxygen). The most commonly used bioreacters are of stirring type.
## Step 6 : Downstream processing
After completion of the biosynthetic stage, the product is subjected through a series of processes before it is ready for marketing as a finished product. The processes include separation and purification, which are collectively referred to as downstream processing. The product has to be formulated with suitable preservatives. Such formulation has to undergo thorough clinical trials as in case of drugs. Strict quality control testing for each product is also required. The downstream processing and quality control testing vary from product to product.

## If 'denaturation' step of PCR is missed, how it will affect the process?
## SOLUTION
During denaturation step of PCR, double strands of DNA separates at high temperature to produce single stranded DNA templates. If the denaturation step is missed, the single stranded template needed for annealing of primers will not be produced. Hence, PCR reaction will not proceed further and no amplification of DNA will occur.
## APPLICATION OF BIOTECHNOLOGY
Biotechnology is applied to various fields and many industries such as food, pharmaceuticals, medicine, agriculture, etc.
## Application of Biotechnology in Agriculture
- The three options for increased food production are :
(i) Agrochemical-based agriculture
(ii) Organic agriculture
(iii) Genetically engineered crop-based agriculture
- The Green Revolution succeeded in increasing the yield of crops mainly due to
(i) use of improved varieties of crops and
(ii) use of agrochemicals
- Further increase in the yield with the existing varieties of crops are not possible using conventional methods of breeding.
- Agrochemicals (fertilisers and pesticides) cause pollution of soil and water and are too expensive for the farmers. Thus, use of genetically modified crops is a possible solution for sustained food production.
- The combined use of recombinant DNA technology and tissue culture techniques has led to the production of genetically modified plants in many crop plants. The use of genetically modified plants has been useful in the following ways :
(i) Genetic modification has made the crops more tolerant to abiotic stresses like cold, heat, drought, salinity, etc.
(ii) It has reduced the dependence of crops on chemical pesticides as they are made pest-resistant. For example, a gene from a soil bacterium, Bacillus thuringiensis (Bt) which produces a protein (Bt toxin) with insecticidal property, is introduced in crop plants to provide protection against insects.
(iii) Post-harvest losses are much reduced.
(iv) As the plants have increased efficiency of mineral usage, the early exhaustion of fertility of soil is prevented.
(v) Food produced from GM (Genetically Modified) crops has enhanced nutritional value.
(vi) Genetic modification has been used to create tailor-made plants to supply resources to industries such as starch, fuels, pharmaceuticals, etc.
Transgenic plants (of some species) are easier to produce because plants have a lot of totipotent tissue (meristem) that can be grown in culture and then induced to develop into a whole plant. One of the most common methods utilises a natural gene transfer system called the Ti-plasmid of Agrobacterium tumefaciens which transfers a piece of DNA called T-DNA into the genome of infected plants.
## Application of Biotechnology in Medicine
- The rDNA technology has been used in the production of safe and more effective therapeutic drugs.
- The recombinant therapeutics do not induce unwanted immunological responses, that are commonly observed with similar products isolated from non-human sources.
- At present about thirty recombinant therapeutics have been approved for human use, of which twelve are being marketed in India also.
(i) Genetically engineered insulin (humulin)
- Human insulin consists of two short polypeptide chains : chain A and chain B, linked by disulphide bridges.
- Insulin is secreted as prohormone which has to be processed before it becomes a mature and functional hormone.
- The prohormone contains another polypeptide called C -peptide, which is removed during maturation.
- In 1983, Eli Lilly, an American company prepared two DNA sequences coding for chains A and B of human insulin and introduced them into the plasmids of Escherichia coli to produce insulin chains.
- The two chains produced were extracted and combined by creating disulphide bridges from human insulin.
(ii) Gene therapy
- In this method, genes are inserted into the cells and tissues of an individual to correct certain hereditary diseases.
- It involves the delivery of a normal gene into the individual or embryo to replace the defective mutant allele of the gene.
- The first clinical gene therapy was given in 1990 to a four year old girl with adenosine deaminase (ADA) deficiency.
- ADA deficiency can be cured by bone marrow transplantation in some children but it is not completely curative.
- For gene therapy, lymphocytes were grown in a culture and functional ADA cDNA is then introduced into these lymphocytes using retro-virus as vector.
- These lymphocytes are then transferred into the body of the patient. However the patient requires periodic infusion of such genetically engineered lymphocytes.
- If a functional gene is introduced into the bone marrow cells at early embryonic stage, it would be a permanent cure.
## (iii) Molecular diagnosis
- Recombinant DNA techniques like PCR (Polymerase Chain Reaction) and ELISA (Enzyme linked immuno sorbent assay) are used for early diagnosis of disorders.
(a) PCR is used to detect HIV in suspected AIDS patients and to detect mutations in gene in suspected cancer patients.
(b) ELISA is based on the principle of antigen-antibody interaction. It is used to detect infection by pathogen by the presence of antigens (proteins, glycoproteins, etc.) or the antibodies synthesised against the pathogen.
## Transgenic Animals
- Transgenic animals are those animals that have had their DNA manipulated to possess, and express a foreign gene.
- Transgenic animals are used in the following ways :
(i) Transgenic animals can be specifically designed to study gene regulation and how they affect the normal functions of the body. e.g., information is obtained about the biological role of insulin-like growth factor.
(ii) Transgenic animals are designed to increase our understanding of how genes contribute to the development of diseases; they are made to serve as models for human diseases.
(iii) Transgenic animals that produce useful biological compounds can be created by introducing a portion of DNA that codes for that product from other organism(s) e.g., alpha-1 antitrypsin, a human protein used to treat emphysema. The first transgenic cow, Rosie, produced the human protein-enriched milk ( $2.4 \mathrm{~g} /$ litre); it also contained human alpha-lactalbumin, a more nutritionally balanced product for human babies.
(iv) Transgenic mice are being developed for use in testing the safety of vaccines (e.g., polio vaccine).
(v) Transgenic animals with more sensitivity to toxic substances are being developed to test the toxicity of drugs.
## Ethical issues
The modification and use of such organisms for public services has resulted in some ethical problems. Hence, the Indian Government has set up organisations which are authorised to make decisions regarding the validity of genetic modifications and the safety of introducing genetically modified organisms for public services. One such organisation is the Genetic Engineering Approval Committee (GEAC).
## Biopiracy
- The industrialised/developed nations are rich financially, but poor in biodiversity and traditional knowledge, while the developing and underdeveloped countries are rich in bioresources and traditional knowledge.
- These developed countries use the bioresources and traditional knowledge of the other countries without proper authorisation and/or compensation to the countries concerned (Biopiracy). For example, basmati rice grown in India is distinct for its unique flavour and aroma, but an American company got patent rights on Basmati through the US patent and trademark office; the new variety of Basmati has been developed by this company (crossing an Indian variety with the semi-dwarf varieties).
- Now some nations are developing laws to prevent such unauthorised exploitation of their bioresources and traditional knowledge.
## Further considerations
The use of whole animals or plants for experimental purposes also requires careful consideration. Investigators must focus on the regulations, restrictions and requirements for the conduct of work with GMOs in most countries and institutions.
Countries may have national authorities that establish guidelines for work with GMOs, and may help scientists classify their work at the appropriate biosafety level. In some cases, classification may differ between countries and they may decide to classify work at a lower or higher level, when new information on a particular vector/ host system becomes available.
Risk assessment is a dynamic process that takes into account new developments and the progress of science. The performance of appropriate risk assessment will assure that the benefits of recombinant DNA technology remain available to humankind in the years to come.
