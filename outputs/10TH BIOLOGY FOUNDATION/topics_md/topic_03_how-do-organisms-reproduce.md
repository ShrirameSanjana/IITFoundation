## 3. How Do Organisms Reproduce
## DO ORGANISMS CREATE EXACT COPIES OF THEMSELVES ?
The organisms reproduce and create new individuals which are similar to each other and also to their parents. Organisms look similar as their body designs are similar. If body designs are to be similar, the blue prints for these designs should be similar. Thus, reproduction involves making copies of the blueprints of body design. The chromosomes present in the nucleus of a cell contain information for inheritance of features from parents to next generation in the form of DNA molecules. DNA contains the blue print of the organism. It is the source of information for making proteins. If the information is changed, different proteins will be made. Different proteins will lead to an altered body design. The essential event in reproduction is the creation of a DNA copy. Cells undergo chemical reaction to build copies of their DNA. This process results in the production of two similar copies of DNA in a reproducing cell. These two copies of DNA are passed into the daughter cells.
The two daughter cells may be genetically identical or slightly different from each other. It all depends on how accurately the process of DNA copying has occurred. The copying of DNA is not truely identical each time, there can be a slight variation. The two copies of DNA can be similar, but may not be identical to the original. Sometimes the new copies of DNA may possess such drastic variations that the newborn cells simply die. On the other hand, there could be such variations which give rise to two subtly different organisms
## Importance of Variation
Usually the DNA copying, which is an essential feature of reproduction, remains consistent generations after generations. This helps the offsprings to adapt in the particular kind of niche. However, there are chances that niches can change due to reasons beyond the control of an organism. The changes may be in the form of increased temperature on earth, varying water levels, meteorite hits, or any other ecological disbalances. Under such conditions any alteration in the particular kind of niche is surely going to hit the consistent population. The population of species may be wiped out due to the sudden alteration of niche.
There are chances of changes in DNA copying which results in the production of organisms adapted to the changed niches. Such organisms have higher rate of survival. If the organism has not changed according to the niche then this organism will be wiped out. For example, a population of bacteria is growing in temperate water and the temperature of water is somehow increased due to global warming. Then this bacteria will definitely die. However, only bacteria with heat resistance will survive. Variations therefore are the basis of evolution.

## TYPES OF REPRODUCTION
There are two main methods of reproduction in living organisms : Asexual Reproduction and Sexual Reproduction
## Asexual Reproduction
Production of offsprings by a single parent without the formation of gametes is known as asexual reproduction. As only somatic cells are involved, it is also termed as somatogenic reproduction. Due to monoparental reproduction, it produces identical offsprings (clones).
## Characteristics of Asexual Reproduction
- Only one parent is involved.
- Only mitiotic cell division takes place.
- Offspring is a clone of the parent.
- Rapid mode of multiplication.
- No fusion of gametes.
- It is found in protozoans, coelenterates, tunicates, and certain worms. It is rare or absent in higher invertebrates and vertebrates.
## Types of Asexual Reproduction
Asexual Reproduction occurs in the following ways:
(i) Fission
(ii) Fragmentation
(iii) Budding
(iv) Regeneration
(v) Spore formation
(vi) Vegetative propagation in plants
(vii) Tissue culture
(i) Fission: It is the simplest and most common method of asexual reproduction. The whole body acts as a reproductive unit. The nucleus of a unicellular parent organism divides into two followed by division of cytoplasm, resulting in the formation of two daughter cells of almost equal size. The daughter cells grow in size and then divide again. It is commonly seen in Euglena, Amoeba. Depending on the number of nuclear divisions, fission can be either Binary fission (two offsprings from one parent) or Multiple fission (a large number of offsprings from one parent as seen in malarial parasite, Plasmodium.).
- Binary fission can be simple i.e. the cytoplasmic division takes place through any plane, as seen in Amoeba and Bacteria. It can be transverse; when plane of cytoplasmic division coincides with the transverse axis of the organism, as seen in Paramoecium; or it may be longitudinal, when the cytoplasmic division coincides with the longitudinal axis of the individual, as seen in Leishmania (causes Kala-azar).
- Multiple fission, in which repeated divisions of the parent nucleus are followed by cytoplasmic divisions resulting in many daughter cells. For example, malarial parasite Plasmodium.
Fig. Process of Fission
(ii) Fragmentation: Some multicellular organisms are made up of random collection of cells. Such organisms reproduce by breaking of their bodies into small pieces called fragments. Each of these fragments is capable of developing into a complete organism. Examples are flat worms, some echinoderms, sea anemones, filamentous green algae like Spirogyra, mycelial fungi and thalloid bryophytes. It is the simplest method of asexual reproduction in multicellular organisms. This cell-by cell division is not feasible in organisms with specialised tissues and organs.
Fig. Fragmentation in Spirogyra
(iii) Budding: This process involves production of new individual from an outgrowth or small projection, arise from the parent called as bud. It is commonly seen in yeast, some protozoans and certain lower organisms like Hydra and sponges. In yeast, budding takes place in unfavourable conditions. A small process, like a bud, appears at one end of the parent cell. The nucleus enlarges and divides into two daughter nuclei, one of which goes into the bud and the other remains in the parent cell. Gradually, the bud grows and becomes almost the same size as that of parent cell. Finally, a constriction appears at the base of the bud and a separating wall is laid down. Sometimes, a bud may appear on another bud which is still attached to the parent cell and gives a look of a false mycelium or pseudomycelium.
Fig. Budding in Yeast
(iv) Regeneration: This is the ability of an organism to regenerate its lost part of the body which has been removed as an injury or by autotomy (self amputation as is seen in some lizards which break off their tail). This method is applied by certain lower organisms for sexual reproduction and multiplication e.g. Planaria.
Fig. Regeneration in Planaria
(v) Spore Formation: It is a common method of asexual reproduction in fungi and bacteria. The fungal hypha develops an enlarged sporangium. The nucleus in the sporangium divides several times and each nuclei gets surrounded by a bit of cytoplasm which develops into a spore. The spores mature within the sporangium and are liberated by rupturing the sporangial wall. Non-motile and non-flagellated spores are called aplanospores while the motile and flagellated spores are called zoospores. The spores are dispersed and germinate into a new hyphae under favourable conditions after reaching a suitable substratum. e.g. Rhizopus, Mucor, Penicillium, etc.
Fig : Asexual Reproduction- Spore formation in Rhizopus
(vi) Vegetative Propagation in Plants: It is a method of asexual reproduction in which parts of plants other than seeds are used as propagule-a structural unit for multiplication. There are two methods for vegetative propagation which are classified as natural and artificial methods.
## Methods of natural vegetative propagation
- By root tuber - The adventitious roots can be grown from nodes of the running stem in sweet potato plant. The roots can be propagated vegetatively when planted in soil.
- By underground modified stems - The node or the eye of potato tuber is used as a propagule. Rhizome of ginger, corm of banana and bulb of garlic and onion are also used as propagules.
- By runner - The auxillary buds on the lowest leaf of the plant gives rise to modified branches known as runners. They produce roots at the nodes. These segments can be cut and used as propagules e.g. lawn grass.
- By leaf - Plants like Bryophyllum develop adventitious buds on their leaves which develop into new plants.

## Methods of artificial vegetative propagation
Artificial methods of vegetative propagation are man made and are developed by plant growers or horticulturists for commercial production of crops. Some common methods are :
- Cutting - A small piece of stem, root or leaf used for propagation is called cutting. Stem cutting is generally used for propagation in grapes, rose, Bougainvillea, sugarcane etc. Here, a $20-30 \mathrm{~cm}$ long piece of stem with one or more buds is planted in soil. This cutting produces adventitious roots from the lower end and shoots from the lateral buds. The cutting develops into a new plant similar to the parent. Root cuttings are done in tamarind, lemon, etc.
Fig. Vegetative propagation in Sugarcane by cutting
- Layering - In this technique, the roots are artificially induced on the stem branches before they are detatched from the parent plant for propagation e.g. raspberry, blackberry, etc.
Layering is done by bending a stem until the middle part of stem touches the soil. After some time roots arise along the part of the stem that is underground, resulting in the development of new plant.
Fig. Vegetative propagation by layering
- Grafting - This is a technique of joining together points of two different plants in such a manner that they grow as one plant. It is done in two closely related dicotyledonous plants having vascular cambia. The rooted plant in which grafting is performed, is called as stock. It should have a good root system. The portion of other plant (bud, branch, etc.) that is grafted on to the stock, is called as scion. It has the desirable quality of fruit, flower, etc. During grafting, 4-12 inches long scion with all the buds intact is placed on the cut end of the stock and tied in such a way that the cambium of the both come in contact with each other. The joint is sealed with wax or clay to prevent drying or entry of any pathogen. This is done in sweet orange, lemon, grape fruit, etc.

## Advantages and Disadvantages of Asexual Reproduction :
## Advantages :
- New generations can be created from a single individual.
- Rapid process of producing offsprings, which are exactly identical to the parents.
## Disadvantages :
- Variations are absent.
- Individuals may not be able to adapt to changing environment.
- It results into overcrowding and struggle for existence.

## Sexual Reproduction
This mode of reproduction involves two individuals to produce a new generation. Hens alone can't produce chicks, nor can bulls alone produce new calves. Both sexes, males and females are needed to produce new generations. Sexual reproduction involves the fusion of male and female gametes to form a zygote, which then develops into an individual.
The various characteristics of this type of reproduction are as follows -
- Two different sexes i.e. male and female take part in this process.
- The male sex organ produces male gametes or sperms and the female sex organ produces the female gametes or ova.
- Gametes are formed by meiosis and are haploid in nature.
- Sperms are motile and move towards ovum for fertilisation and then form a diploid zygote.
- It is a slow process of multiplication.
- Variations appear due to new combination of genes during crossing over.
- The genetic makeup of offsprings is different from that of parent.
- Diploid zygote divides by mitosis and passes through different phases of development to form a multicellular individual.
## Types of Sexual Reproduction
Sexual reproduction is of two types: Syngamy and Conjugation.
## Syngamy
This is a complete and permanent fusion of two haploid gametes to form a diploid zygote. When the two fusing gametes are morphologically and physiologically similar to each other, the process is called isogamy e.g. Monocystis. When two organisms themselves act as gametes, it is called hologamy e.g. Yeast.
## Conjugation
This is a temporary pairing of two parents to exchange male and female gametes through a temporary cytoplasmic bridge e.g. ciliated protozoans like Paramoecium.
In majority of animals, including human beings, male and female individuals are separate. Such organisms are called unisexual, i.e. they have only one sex in one individual. Some plants are also unisexual e.g. papaya, cucurbits, etc. But majority of flowering plants and a few animals (like tapeworm, earthworm, garden snail, star fish, etc.) are bisexual or hermaphrodite, i.e. individuals have both male and female sex organs.

## SEXUAL REPRODUCTION IN FLOWERING PLANTS
The reproductive units of sexually reproducing plants are flowers. The flower is a condensed shoot with the nodes present very close to each other. The different parts of the flower are attached to the nodes. All the structures present at one node are collectively called the whorl. The first or the outermost two whorls are called the non-reproductive or accessary whorls. They are calyx and corolla. The inner two whorls are androecium and gynoecium, the reproductive or necessary whorl. If a flower has either androecium or gynoecium then it is called as unisexual. For e.g. sunflower. While, If the flower contains both androecium and gynoecium then, it is called bisexual. For e.g. Hibiscus and Mustard.
## Parts of a flower
A typical flower consists of following parts -
(i) Sepals : These make outer whorl of the flower called calyx. It is usually green in colour but in some flowers it may be coloured to attract the insects. It protects the inner whorls of the flower.
(ii) Petals : These make the second whorl of flower, called corolla and consists of coloured petals. Being coloured in nature they attract various insects and animals for pollination.
(iii) Stamen : It is the male reproductive part of a flower. Each stamen consists of a stalk called filament and a flattened top called the anther. The anthers produce pollens grains which produces male gametes. The pollen grains appear as yellowish powder which stick to our hands. Stamens form the third whorl, called androecium.
(iv) Carpel : Carpels have a swollen ovary at the base, an elongated middle style and a terminal stigma. The ovary contains ovules. Each ovule possess an egg which is the female gamete. Carpel form the fourth whorld, called gynoecium.

## Structure of Androecium (Male Reproductive Organ)
Unit of androecium or male reproductive organ is called stamen. A typical stamen is differentiated into three parts - a long, thin structure called filament and a swollen spore bearing structure called anther.
## Structure of Anther
Anther has two lobes and each lobe consists of two theca (dithecous), hence appears tetragonal in a cross sectional view. Each theca has a mass of sporogenous tissue which develops into microsporangium or pollen sac. Microsporangium appears roughly circular in outline. It has four layers - outermost layer epidermis, endothecium, middle layer and innermost layer tapetum. The outer three layers provide protection and help in dehiscence of anther, while tapetum nourishes the developing microspores or pollen grains.
Fig. (a) A typical stamen (b) Three dimensional cut-section of an anther (c) Transverse section of a mature anther (d) A dehisced anther
## Microsporogenesis
Each cell of the sporogenous tissue acts as pollen mother cell (PMC). It gives rise to a microspore tetrad, containing four haploid microspores by the process of meiosis. This process of formation of spores from PMC is called as microsporogenesis. As anther matures and dehydrates, microspores dissociate from each other and form pollen grains. Several thousands of pollen grains are released after dehiscence of anther.
## Structure of pollen grain
Pollen grains represent the male gametophyte. They are generally spherical in shape and measure about $25-50$ micrometers in diameter. It has a two layered wall. The outer hard layer is called the exine which is made up of a resistant organic material known as sporopollenin. It can withstand high temperatures and strong acids, alkali and enzymes. The exine is discontinuous due to the presence of apertures, called as germ pores, where sporopollenin is absent. Pollen grains have been found as fossils because of the presence of sporopollenin. The inner wall of the pollen grain is called the intine. It is thin and continuous layer made up of cellulose and pectin. The cytoplasm of the pollen grain is surrounded by a plasma membrane.
A mature pollen grain contains two cells-the vegetative cell and generative cell.
The vegetative cell is bigger, has abundant food reserve and a large irregularly shaped nucleus. The generative cell is small and floats in the cytoplasm of the vegetative cell. It is spindle shaped with dense cytoplasm and a small nucleus. In most of the angiosperms, pollen grains are shed at the two celled stage. In some species, the generative nucleus divides mitotically to give rise to two male gametes (3-celled) before the pollen grains are shed.
Once the pollen grains shed, they have to land on the stigma (female reproductive part) before they loose viability. The period of viability of pollen grains varies from 30 minutes to few months.

## Structure of Gynoecium (Female Reproductive Organ)
The unit of gynoecium is called pistil or carpel. It may be monocarpellary (one carpel), bicarpellary (two carpels), tricarpellary (three carpels) or multicarpellary (many carpels). When the number of carpels is more than one, the gynoecium can be syncarpous (all the carpels are fused together) or apocarpous (carpels remain free). Each pistil has three parts; ovary, style and stigma. Placenta is present inside the ovarian cavity (locule) of ovary. It contain ovules, also known as megasporangia. The number of ovules in an ovary may be one as in mango, rice, wheat or many as in orchids, watermelon, papaya, etc.
## Ovule
The ovule arises as a primordium on the placenta. Ovule is attached to the placenta by a short stalk called funiculus or funicle. The body of the ovule fuses with funicle in the region called hilum. Thus hilum represents the junction between ovule and funicle. Each ovule has two protective coverings called integuments. They cover the whole ovule except at the tip, where a small opening called as micropyle is present. Opposite to the micropylar end is chalaza, which is the basal part of the ovule. Enclosed within the integuments is a mass of cells called nucellus. Cells of nucellus are rich in reserve food material. Embedded in the nucellus, is the female gameotophyte called embryo sac.

## Embryo Sac
Only one of the four megaspore develops into a functional embryo sac or the female gametophyte while the other three degenerate. The functional embryo sac nucleus divides mitotically into two nuclei which move to the opposite poles, forming a 2 -nucleate embryo sac. Two more sequential mitotic divisions in the 2 -nucleate embryo sac result in the formation of the 4 -nucleate and then 8 -nucleate embryo sac. These mitotic divisions are free nuclear i.e. they are not immediately followed by cell wall formation. After it reaches the 8 -nucleate stage, cell walls are laid down to organise into a typical female gametophyte.
There is characteristic distribution of cells within the embryo sac. Three cells are grouped at the micropylar end and constitute the egg apparatus. The egg apparatus consists of two synergids and one egg cell. The synergids have special thickenings at the micropylar tip called filiform apparatus, which play an important role in guiding the pollen tubes into the synergids. The three cells at the chalazal end are called the antipodal cells. There is one large central cell which has two polar nuclei in the centre of cell. Hence, a typical angiospermic embryo sac, at maturity, is $\mathbf{8}$ nucleated but $\mathbf{7}$ celled.

## Pollination
The transfer of pollen grains from anther of the stamen to the receptive stigma of the carpel is called pollination. Pollination is an important mechanism by which the pollen grains produced inside the pollen sacs of anthers, are transported by various agencies to the stigma of female reproductive organ. It is an essential process that facilitates successful fertilisation in plants. The pollen grains protect male gametes from drying out.
Types of Pollination: It is of two types i.e., Self Pollination and Cross Pollination.
Self Pollination: Self pollination involves the transfer of pollen grains from the anther of a flower to the stigma of the same flower or to the stigma of another flower on the same plant. In bisexual flowers, pollen grains are transfered from the anther to the stigma of the same flower. This is called Autogamy. It is generally not dependent on an external agency i.e. wind, water, insects, birds, etc for pollination, but may result in less vigorous offspring.
Cross Pollination: It is the transfer of the pollen grains from the anther of one flower to the stigma of another flower on another plant. It involves two separate plants and depends on external agencies like wind, water, air or insects and etc.
Fig. : Diagrammatic Representation of Self and Cross Pollination
Majority of plants use biotic (animals) agents and only a small portion of plants use abiotic agents (wind and water) for pollination. The contact of pollen grains with stigma depends on a chance factor. So flowers produce large amount of pollen grains to compensate for it.

## Fertilisation
Fertilisation involves the fusion of a male gamete with a female gamete. To bring about fertilisation, the pollen grains fall on the stigma and gives rise to the pollen tubes that passes through the style and enter into the ovule via one of the synergids. Here, it releases two gametes; one fuses with the egg cell to form zygote (syngamy) and another fuses with 2 polar nuclei to form a triploid primary endosperm nucleus (triple fusion) inside the embryo sac. Since two types of fusion occur, it is known as double fertilisation.

## Post Fertilisation Events
This include endosperm and embryo development, maturation of ovules and ovary into seeds and fruit respectively.
## CONNECTING TOPIC
Endosperm: The primary endosperm nucleus undergoes successive nuclear divisions to give rise to free nuclei. This stage of endosperm development is called free-nuclear endosperm. This is followed by cell wall formation and the endosperm becomes cellular. Endosperm is a triploid tissue where cells are filled with reserve food material and is used for the nutrition of the developing embryo. Hence, endosperm development precedes embryo development.
Endosperm may either be completely consumed by the developing embryo (e.g. bean, pea, etc.) before seed maturation called non-endospermic or it may persist in the mature seeds called endospermic seed (e.g. castor, coconut) and it may be used up during seed germination.
Fig. Seed of Castor showing Endosperm
## Embryo
The diploid zygote develops into embryo at the micropylar end. This starts only after formation of a certain amount of endosperm. The zygote gives rise to a proembryo which develops into a globular embryo, is followed by a heart shaped and later into mature embryo.
## Fruit
Fruits develop from the ovary. The formation of seeds and fruits is simultaneous. Wall of the ovary develops into the wall of the fruit called pericarp. In fruits like guava, mango, etc. the fruits are fleshy while in fruits like mustard, groundnut, etc. they are dry. By the time the fruit develops the other floral parts, degenerate and fall off. However, in some fruits such as apple, strawberry, etc. the thalamus contributes to fruit formation. Such fruits are called false fruits. Fruits that develop only from the ovary are called true fruits. Fruits are normally formed as a result of fertilisation but some fruits like banana develop without the act of fertilisation. Such fruits are called parthenocarpic fruits, these fruits are seedless. Such fruits can also be induced by the application of hormones. They have a great commercial value.
Tomato is the world's most popular fruit and is usually termed as a 'vegetable' in most countries.
## SEXUAL REPRODUCTION IN HUMAN BEINGS
The human beings are unisexual with two separate sexes - male and female. Both the sexes (male and female) are visibly different from each other. The individuals of the two sexes show distinctive features called the secondary sexual characteristics. This period of secondary growth of sexual characters achieved during adolescence, called puberty. Some of the male secondary sexual characteristics are facial hair, cracking of voice, etc. and female secondary sexual characteristics include breast development,
broadening of hips, etc. Such distinguishing features are present in all the animals. These characteristics serve to identify and attract sex partners.
The reproductive system comprises of two different parts:
(1) Primary reproductive system
(2) Accessory reproductive system
Primary reproductive system includes gamete producing organs, the testes and the ovaries. Accessory reproductive system includes the glands, passages and other such associated structures.
## Male Reproductive System
The male reproductive system consists of a pair of testes, a pair of sperm ducts (Vas deferens), accessory glands, and a penis.
## Testes
A pair of testis are the primary sex organs and are present inside the scrotum. They are contained within the abdominal cavity in the embryonic stage but descend in scrotum and remain here throughout life. It happens because the testes cannot produce sperms at body temperature. A temperature $2-3$ degrees lower, is ideal for the production of sperms. The sperms produced in testes are carried by the duct called vas deferens. The vas deferens unites with the urethra coming from the urinary bladder. Thus urethra forms a common passage for both urine and sperms. Prostate and seminal vesicle add their secretions into the vas deferens. Thus the sperm flows in the fluid which also provides the required nutrition.

## Structure of Testes
Each testes is covered by a thick connective tissue layer. Internally, the testis is lobed. There are 15-20 lobes, each having a network of seminiferous tubules and the interstitial cells between the tubules. The seminiferous tubules produce sperms that are passed from the tubules to the posterior region of testis. The sperms are produced by a process called the spermatogenesis. The interstitial cells are also know as Leydig cells and they secrete testosterone hormone. In addition, they also form a packing tissue between the seminiferous tubules.
## Duct System
From the seminiferous tubules, the sperms are passed into a network of 10-12 ducts called the efferent ducts or the vasa efferentia. They are then passed into a highly coiled tubular part called the epididymis. Epididymis is an organ that extends from the top of the testis along its side to its back. The sperms from the epididymis pass into a distinct tube called the vas deferens, also called the sperm duct. It rises in the
Fig.: Diagrammatic view of male testis
abdominal cavity and loops over the ureter of that side. The passage that connects the testicular region with the abdomen is called the inguinal canal. Sometimes, the intestine bulges into the scrotum through this canal due to pressure in the abdomen. This causes hernia. Vas deferens is joined by the duct from the seminal vesicle to form the ejaculatory duct. The duct then passes through the prostate and opens into the urethra. The system of tubules consisting of the vasa efferentia, epididymis, vas deferens, ejaculatory tube and the urethra is the duct system associated with the male reproductive system.
## Glands
The various glands associated with the male reproductive system are as follows:
Seminal Vesicle: A pair of glands that are present behind the urinary bladder and secrets a fluid into the common ejaculatory duct. This fluid along with the sperms is called the semen, (a milky fluid).
Prostate: It is a bi-lobed gland near the opening of the urethra. The prostate also pours its secretion into the urethra. It is alkaline and mixes with the semen.
Cowper's Gland: They are a pair of small ovoid glands that secrete lubricating fluid into the urethra just before it enters the penis. The secretions of these glands make the sluggish sperms more active and help in the passage of sperms through the duct system and then in the ejaculation.
## Penis
Penis is a muscular organ containing erectile tissue. The tissue is richly supplied with blood vessels. On sexual stimulation, the penis is gorged, with excess of blood which causes it to become erect. During sexual intercourse, the penis is inserted into vagina of the female for the ejaculation of semen.
## Female Reproductive System
The female reproductive system consists of a pair of ovaries, a pair of oviducts, uterus, vagina and vulva. The main functions of the female reproductive system are to produce eggs, receive the sperms, provide the site for fertilisation, implantation of the growing embryo and development of the foetus. It also produces hormones that control the various stages of ovulation and maintenance of pregnancy.

## Ovaries
Ovaries are a pair of oval structures that are present one on either side. The ovaries produce eggs one at a time, every alternate month. The eggs are produced by the germinal epithelial cells of the ovary.
## Oviducts / Fallopian tubes
Oviducts / Fallopian tubes are a pair of tubes of about 12 cm in length. They run from the ovaries of each side to the uterus. At the ovarian end the tube is funnel-shaped with the end of the tube thrown into a number of folds. These folds are ciliated which help to sweep the egg produced by the ovary into the fallopian tube. The fallopian tubes are the sites for fertilisation of the egg by the sperm.
## Uterus
Uterus is pear-shaped structure, broader on the upper end and narrower on the lower end. The upper end is called the body of the uterus and the lower end is called the cervix. At the upper end, it receives the oviducts of either side, whereas at the lower end the cervix opens into the vagina which then opens to the outside.
The uterine wall has three layers. They are the innermost endometrium made up of several glands and blood vessels, the middle myometrium madeup of smooth muscles and the outer perimetrium madeup of connective tissue. The inner surface of the uterus provides a site for the implantation of the embryo. The uterine wall plays an important role during childbirth. Cervix is made of sphincter muscles that control the opening and closing of the uterus.
## Vagina
Vagina is a 9 cm long muscular tube that receives the penis during coitus. It is lined with epithelial cells. The vaginal opening in young females is partially covered with a thin mucous membrane called hymen. This often breaks early in females during strenuous work or sports.
## Vulva
Vulva is the external female genitalia and comprises of mons pubis. The vaginal opening has two pair of folds on either side. The outer fold is thicker with hair, sweat glands and sebaceous glands and is called labia majora. The inner folds are thinner and devoid of hair. They are called labia minora. Covered by the upper part of the folds is the female equivalent of the penis, called the clitoris. It is also an erectile and highly sensitive organ. In this region, there are two openings, the upper urethral meatus or opening and the lower vaginal orifice.
## Gametogenesis
Sperms are produced in seminiferous tubules present in testis. This process is known as spermatogenesis. The precursors of the sperm, the diploid spermatogonium divides mitotically to produce numerous diploid spermatocytes. Spermatocytes undergo meiotic cell division to produce 4 haploid spermatids. Spermatids differentiate and develop into sperm which are single celled male gametes with a head, midpiece, and a tail.
Ovum (egg cell), the female gamete is produced in the ovaries of female reproductive system. This process is known as oogenesis. In contrast to males, the initial steps in egg production occur prior to their birth in females. Diploid oogonia and primary oocytes are produced in foetus and by the time she is born, females have about 1-2 million oocytes. In oocytes, the first meiotic division is initiated and then stopped. No further development occurs until the girl attains puberty. After maturity, the primary oocytes recommence their development, usually one at a time and once a month. The primary oocytes grow further and completes the meiosis I, forming a large secondary oocyte and a small polar body. Only after fertilization, meiosis II is completed. After completion of meiosis II, secondary oocytes gets converted into a fertilized egg or zygote.
## Menstruation
The reproductive cycle in the females is called as menstrual cycle. First menstruation begins at puberty and is called as Menarche. Menstrual cycle has an average time period of about 28 to 29 days. One ovum is released during the middle of each menstrual cycle. Cycle starts with the menstrual phase when menstrual flow occurs and it lasts for $3-5$ days. Menstrual flow results due to breakdown of endometrial lining of the uterus and its blood vessels that come out through vagina. Menstruation occurs only if released ovum is not fertilised. Pregnancy can be indicated by lack of menstrual cycle. It may also be caused by stress, poor health etc. Menstrual cycle ceases at about 50 years of age and this is termed as menopause. Cyclic menstruation is an indicator of normal reproductive phase and extends between menarche and menopause.
## Structures of Gametes
Each sperm is a small cell of about $50 \mu \mathrm{~m}$ length and $2.5 \mu \mathrm{~m}$ diameter. It constits of a head, middle piece and a tail region. The head consists of the nucleus and a structure called the acrosome at the tip. The acrosome secretes lytic substance that break down the wall of the egg for fertilisation. The beating of the tail propels the sperms forward at the rate of $1-4 \mathrm{~mm}$ per minute. Each egg or ovum is a spherical cell much bigger than the sperm. It mainly consists of a single nucleus and some reserve food made of lipid droplets.

## Fertilisation
Fertilisaiton is defined as the fusion of sperm with the egg nucleus to form a diploid cell known as zygote. The fertilisation is internal in the human reproductive sytem. It is achieved by the insertion of the male organ, penis into the vagina of the female. The sperms are deposited in the vagina of the females during a process called as copulation or sexual intercourse. The release of sperms is called ejaculation and at one ejaculation there may be upto 600 million sperms. Of these, only a few from thousand make the journey from the vaginal canal to the oviducts. This may take $4-8$ hours. The life of the sperm is $1-3$ days after ejaculation.
In the fallopian tubes, many sperms, surround an egg. However, only one enters the egg leaving behind the tail. The enzymes of the acrosome digest the several layers of tissue to reach the egg cytoplasm. Once the sperm is reached inside the egg, the male and female nuclei become lighter and are called pro-nuclei. Both the pro-nuclei fuse to form a zygote. Once a sperm enters inside an egg, the thickening of the outer walls of the egg blocks the entry of other sperms.

## Post-Fertilisation Events
Once the egg is fertilised, ovulation and menstruation do not take place. The fertilised structure, now called the zygote, which is diploid. It starts dividing and forms a blastocyst, the first stage of an embryo. The blastocyst gets implanted on the uterine walls. It then grows into a foetus.
The foetus remains attached to the mother through an umbilical cord which is embedded in a tissue called placenta at one end. The placenta in turn is embedded into the uterine wall and is richly supplied with vessels. The nutrients from the mother's blood pass into the umbilical cord and the waste from the foetus pass into the mother's blood through the placenta.
The foetus is fully grown and ready to come out from the womb in 40 weeks. The uterine walls start muscular contractions called the labour that helps in pushing the foetus to the outside through the vaginal canal. The placenta detaches from the uterine wall once the baby is out. The umbilical cord is cut and tied near the baby's body.

## Reproductive Health
Sexual maturation does not necessarily mean that the body or mind is ready for sexual acts or having and bringing up children. Contraception gives individuals a choice to conceive or have children at their own will. Not only this, contraception also helps in controlling population growth. Goverment has come out with various programmes that encourage smaller families to use various contraceptive methods. Contraceptive devices are used to prevent unwanted pregnancy and STDs (Sexually Transmitted Diseases).
## Contraceptive Method
An ideal contraceptive should be user-friendly, easily available, effective and reversible. It should not have any side-effects and should not interfere with sexual drive, desire and sexual act. The contraceptive methods can be of two types.
1. Natural/Traditional methods
It works on the principle of avoiding chances of fusion of ovum and sperms. It includes :
(i) Periodic abstinence: It involves avoiding of coitus from days 10 to 17 of the menstrual cycle, when ovulation could be expected. It is called fertile period and the chances of fertilisation in this period are very high.
(ii) Coitus interruptus (withdrawal): It involves withdrawal of penis from the vagina just before ejaculation, in order to avoid insemination inside vagina.
(iii) Lactational amenorrhea: It involves the prevention of conception by breastfeeding the child. Ovulation and the cycle do not occur during the period of lactation. It has no side effects. However, this is effective up to 6 months following parturition (delivery). A chance of failure of this method is high.
2. Artificial methods
(A) Barrier methods
These method also prevent fusion of sperm and ovum. It is available for both male and female partners.
(i) Condoms : Condoms are made of rubber/latex sheath. They are used to cover the penis in male or vagina and cervix in the female. They are used just before coitus so that the semen does not enter into female reproductive tract.
Condoms are very popular because:
- It protects the user from sexually transmitted diseases.
- It is disposable.
- It can be self-used and thereby gives privacy to the user.
(ii) Diaphragms, cervical caps and vaults:
These are barriers made of rubber that are inserted into the female reproductive tract to cover the cervix during coitus. They prevent conception by blocking the entry of sperms through the cervix. They are reusable.
(iii) Spermicidal creams, jellies and foams are used with the barriers to increase contraceptive efficiency.
(B) Intra Uterine Devices (IUDs)
These devices are used only by females. These are inserted by doctors or expert nurses inside uterus. It makes the uterus unsuitable for implantation and cervix hostile to the sperms. IUDs includes :
(i) Non-medicated IUDs (e.g. Lippes loop)
(ii) Copper releasing IUDs (e.g. CuT, Cu7, Multiload 375)
(iii) Hormone releasing IUDs (e.g. Progestasert, LNG-20)
(C) Oral contraceptives
This method is used only by female. It is used in the form of tablets, hence known as pills. They contain hormoneseither progestogens or progestogen-oestrogen combinations. Pills are taken daily for 21 days starting within the first five days of menstrual cycle. After a gap of 7 days (during which menstruation occurs) it has to be repeated in the
same pattern till the female desires to prevent conception. They inhibit ovulation and implantation as well as alter the quality of cervical mucus to prevent the entry of sperms. Pills are very effective with lesser side effects E.g. Saheli which is a non-steroidal preparation used as an oral contraceptive. It is a 'once a week' pill with very few side effects and high contraceptive value.
## (D) Injectables
It involves usage of hormone, either progestogens alone or in combination with oestrogen by females as injections or implants under skin. Their mode of action is similar to that of pills. It is very effective for longer periods.
## (E) Emergency contraceptives
These are used within 72 hours of coitus, rape or casual unprotected intercourse. It involves administration of progestogens or progestogen-oestrogen combinations and IUDs.
## (F) Surgical methods (also known as sterilization methods)
These are the permanent or terminal methods to prevent pregnancy. This method blocks the transport of gametes, thereby preventing conception. It is very effective but reversibility is poor. It includes:
(i) Vasectomy: It is the sterilization procedure in males. In this, a small part of the vas deferens is removed or tied up through a small incision on the scrotum.
(ii) Tubectomy: It is the sterilization procedure in females. In this, a small part of the fallopian tube is removed or tied up through a small incision in the abdomen or through vagina.

## Infertility
There are many diseases/disorders which may affect the reproductive system in both males and females. One of them is infertility. It is the inability of a sexually active non-contracepting couple to achieve pregnancy in one year. In females, the common causes may include hormonal imbalances, blocked oviducts, damaged uterus or cervix and many others. In males, it may be due to the absence of sperms, low sperm count, abnormal sperms, auto immunity, impotence etc.
Infertility treatments can be either hormonal or modern like in vitro fertilisation (IVF). In IVF, the sperm and ovum are fused in a test tube to form a zygote, which is later implanted in the uterus of female for further development. These babies are called test tube babies. Other methods include gamete intra-fallopian transfer (GIFT). Here, gametes are transferred to the oviducts and fertilisation takes place inside the body itself.
