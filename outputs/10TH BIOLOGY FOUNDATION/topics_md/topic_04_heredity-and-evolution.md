## HEREDITY
Transmission of characters (resemblances as well as variations) from parents to the offspring from one generation to another is called heredity.
Although the offsprings inherit characters (or traits) of the parents and resemble them very closely, but the resemblance is not identical in all respects. The offsprings are never a true copy of the parents. In fact, no two individuals are exactly alike and the members of any one species differ from one another in some traits. The differences in the traits shown by the individuals of a species, and also by the offsprings of the same parents, are referred to as variations.
For example, in human beings, few people have hanging earlobes called free earlobe. In others, the earlobe is closely attached to the side of the head and is called closely attached earlobe. So free earlobes and attached earlobes are the two variations found in humans.

## Accumulation of Variation during Reproduction :
We see different organisms around us. They all look different as species differ from each other. But when you look for differences among individuals of the same species, no two individuals are identical to each other. We acquire a common body design along with subtle changes. For example, a bacterium produces two bacteria by asexual reproduction. One of the offspring bacterium has a variation due to which it has higher tolerance of heat. This characteristic of heat resistance will go on accumulating in the offsprings of successive generations of this bacterium, and this will ultimately give rise to a variant of bacteria which will be highly heat resistant and would be able to survive even at very high temperatures. The advantage of variation to a species is that it increases the chance of its survival in a changing environment.
Only minor differences will be generated due to small inaccuracies in DNA copying mechanism. The diversity is quite high in case of sexually reproducing organisms because of reshuffling of traits during gamete formation and chance fusion of gametes during fertilisation. If the DNA comes from two parents, the differences will be pronounced. These differences will be passed on to the offsprings and would create diversity.
Fig. Creation of diversity over succeeding generations. The original organism at the top will give rise to, say, two individuals, similar in body design, but with subtle differences. Each of them, in turn, will give rise to two individuals in the next generation. Each of the four individuals in the bottom row will be different from each other. While some of these differences will be unique, others will be inherited from their respective parents, who were different from each other. Variations arise either due to an environmental influence or genetic factors like recombination of genes, mutation etc. The environmental factors influence the phenotype of individuals and these are not heritable. Genetic traits affect the genes and are heritable and important from evolutionary perspective.

## Rules for the Inheritance of Traits: Mendel's Contribution
The study of genetics was started by Gregor Johann Mendel, an Austrian Monk, in 1850s. However, his studies were not recognised by the world till after his death. Mendel is known as 'Father of Genetics'.
Before Mendel, although people knew that parents with blue eyes would get blue eyed children, etc., they did not know the exact mechanism of transfer of these traits from the parents to the offspring. They did not know that there is a physical basis of heredity. This was identified by Mendel through his pioneering work in pea plant (Pisum sativum). He blended his knowledge of science and mathematics and was the first one to keep count of individuals exhibiting a particular trait in each generation.
Fig. Gregor Johann Mendel
## Mendel's experimental material.
Mendel picked common garden pea plant for his experiment as they can be grown easily in large numbers and their reproduction can be manipulated. Pea plants have both male and female reproductive organs and they can either self-pollinate themselves or can cross-pollinate with another plant. Mendel was able to selectively cross-pollinate pure bred plants with particular traits and observe the outcome over many generations.

Mendel proposed that something is passed on, unchanged, from parents to offsprings over successive generations, and called it factors. These factors are now known as genes, which are units of inheritance and contain information that is required to express a particular trait. Genes which code for a pair of contrasting traits are called alleles (alternative form of a gene that is located at a specific position on a specific chromosome). When the alleles of a pair are same, they are called homozygous. When the alleles of a pair are different; one masks the expression of other, is termed as heterozygous. The allele which expresses itself even in the presence of another allele is called dominant, and is represented by the first capital letter of the trait. For example; in case of a tall pea plant, dominant allele is represented by $\mathbf{T}$. On the other hand, an allele that fails to express itself in the presence of a dominant allele is called recessive. It is represented by the small letter of the corresponding dominant trait. For example, the dwarf pea plant is represented by $\mathbf{t}$.
Punnett square (devised by R.C. Punnett) is used to predict the outcome of a breeding experiment. It gives the probability for genotypes (genetic makeup) and not the phenotypes (actual expressed traits or morphological characteristics).
## Mendel's Monohybrid cross
Mendel crossed tall and dwarf pea plants (Parental or P generation) to study the inheritance of one gene/single trait. He collected seeds as a result of this cross and grew them to generate plants of first hybrid generation called Filial progeny or F1 generation. Mendel found that all the F1 progeny plants were tall; they all resembled to one of their parents and none were dwarf. He then self-pollinated the F1 progeny and found that some of the plants were dwarf. To his surprise, the trait which was absent in F1 generation reappeared in $\mathbf{F 2}$ generation. The proportion of tall plants was $3 / 4$ and the dwarf plants were 1/4. All plants were either tall or dwarf and none in between; there was no blending of characteristics in the progeny. The ratios of tall to dwarf plants for F2 generation were 3:1 (phenotypic ratio) and 1:2:1 (genotypic ratio). Mendel found similar results for other traits of the pea plants.
## Mendel's Dihybrid cross
Mendel also crossed two plants that differed in two traits. For example, he crossed seeds with round shaped and yellow color (RRYY) to the seeds with wrinkled shape and green color (rryy). Yellow color and round shape are dominant traits while green color and wrinkled shape were recessive characters. In F1 generation, both yellow and green seeds as well as round and wrinkled seeds were present in 3:1 ratio. When F1 generation was self-pollinated, the phenotypes round, yellow; wrinkled, yellow; round, green and wrinkled, green appeared in the ratio $\mathbf{9 : 3 : 3 : 1}$. Such a ratio was also observed for several other pairs of characters that Mendel studied.

## Mendel's Laws of Inheritance
Mendel's observations of his experiments led to the principles or laws:
1. Law of Dominance - It states that dominant alleles are always repressed by recessive alleles. Hence, the cross between homozygous dominant and homozygous recessive plants will always result in dominant phenotype with heterozygous genotype.
2. Law of Segregation: According to this law, for any particular trait, the pair of alleles of each parent separate and only one allele passes from each parent to an offspring. Paternal allele is inherited as a matter of chance (we now know that segregation of alleles occurs during the process of meiosis).
3. Law of Independent Assortment: This is also known as 'Inheritance law'. According to this law, different pairs of alleles are passed to offsprings independently of each other. Today, we know this is due to the fact that the genes for independently assorted traits are located on different chromosomes.

## Deoxyribonucleic Acid (DNA)
Structure of DNA was determined by X-ray diffraction studies. A double helix model of DNA was proposed by Watson and Crick in 1953. They suggested that:
(i) Each DNA molecule consists of two polynucleotide chains.
(ii) The chains are helically coiled around a common axis.
(iii) Each chain is complementary to another one.
(iv) There are 10 bases per turn of helix and one complete turn of helix is of $3.4 \AA$.
(v) The diameter of the DNA molecule is $20 \AA$.
The DNA molecule is a polymer consisting of several thousand pairs of nucleotide monomers. Each nucleotide consists of the pentose sugar deoxyribose a phosphate group and a nitrogenous base which may be either purine or pyrimidine. The two purines are Adenine (A) and guanine (G). The two pyrimidines are thymine (T) and Cytosine (C).
Deoxyribose and a nitrogeneous base together form a nucleoside. A nucleoside and a phosphate together form a nucleotide.
$$
\left[\begin{array}{l}
\text { Nucleoside }=\text { Deoxyribose }+ \text { Nitrogenous base } \\
\text { Nucleotide }=\text { Deoxyribose }+ \text { Nitrogenous base }+ \text { Phosphate }
\end{array}\right]
$$
Deoxyribose is a five carbon sugar molecule, which forms the phosphate backbone of the DNA molecule.
Genes are arranged linearly along long chains of DNA sequence, called chromosomes. The DNA of the chromosome is associated with proteins that organise, compact and control the access to the DNA, forming a material called chromatin. In eukaryotes, chromatin is composed of nucleosomes - segments of DNA wound around histone protein. The complete set of hereditary material in an organism i.e., the combined DNA sequences of all chromosomes is called genome.
Haploid organisms have only one copy of each chromosome while diploid organisms (most animals and many plants) contain two of each chromosome i.e., they have two copies of every gene. The two alleles for a gene are located on identical loci of sister chromatid, each allele inherited from a different parent. Thus each gamete or germ cell has one chromosome from each pair which may be either maternal or paternal. When the two germ cells combine, the normal number of chromosomes is restored in the progeny. This ensures the stability of chromosome number and DNA of a species.

## How do genes control traits?
Genes correspond to regions with DNA, a molecule composed of 4 types of nucleotides. The sequence of these nucleotides contains the informations that organisms inherit. DNA is a double stranded structure, each strand acts as a template for creating a new complementary strand.
The sequence of nucleotides in a gene is translated by cells to produce a chain of amino acids, creating proteins. The order of amino acids in a protein corresponds to the order of nucleotides in the gene. This relationship between nucleotide sequence and amino acid sequence is known as genetic code. The amino acids in a protein determine how it folds into a three-dimensional shape. This structure is, in turn, responsible for the protein's function. Proteins carry out almost all the functions needed for cells to live. A change in the DNA can change amino acid it codes and thus, changing its shape and function. This can have a serious effect in the cell and on the organism as a whole e.g., sickle-cell anaemia, which is caused due to change in a single nucleotide.

## Sex Determination
## Sex determination is a biological system that determines the development of sexual characters in an organism.
Most sexual organisms have two sexes: Males and females. Genetically, both the sexes have different alleles or even different genes that specify their sexual morphology. In humans, the total chromosome complement is $46 ; 44$ of which are autosomes while 2 distinct chromosomes are the sex chromosomes, which determine the sex of an organism and various sex-linked characteristics.
In humans, sex is predetermined in the sperm gamete. The egg gamete mother cell is said to be homogametic because all its cells possess the XX sex chromosomes, sperm gametes are said to be heterogametic because around half of them contain the X -chromosome and others possess the Y -chromosome to compliment the first X -chromosome.
There are two possibilities that can occur during fertilisation between male and female gametes, ( XX and XY ).
All offsprings will inherit an X chromosome from their mother regardless of whether they are boys or girls. Thus, the sex of the children will be determined by what they inherit from their father.
Fig. Sex determination in human beings by Checker Board Method
Many sex-determination systems exist. In a number of insects including grasshoppers, cockroaches and crickets, there is a variation where the females have two copies of the sex chromosome (XX), but males have only one (XO). The 'O' denotes the absence of the second sex chromosome. In some species of reptiles like turtles, the sex is determined by the temperature at which the egg is incubated. In tropical clown fish, the dominant individual in a group becomes female while the others are male. Some species have no sex determination system. Certain species of snails and common earthworm are hermaphrodite. A few species of insects, fishes and reptiles reproduce by parthenogenesis and are females only.

## EVOLUTION
Evolution is the product of two opposing forces- Processes that constantly introduce variation in traits, and processes that make particular variants become more common or rare. The main cause of variation is mutation, gene flow or reshuffling of genes in sexual reproduction. Natural selection occurs and only few individuals in each generation will survive, as resources are limited and organisms produce more offsprings than their environment can support. Over many generations, mutations produce small random changes in traits which are filtered by Natural Selection and the beneficial changes are retained. This causes adjustment of traits so that they become suited to an organism's environment. These adjustments are called adaptations. However, not every trait is an adaptation.
Evolution: A gradual development of more complex species from pre-existing simpler forms.

## Lamarckism
Jean Baptist Lamarck (1744-1829) tried to explain the principle behind the process of evolution in the book "Philosophie Zoologique". Lamarck proposed the "Theory of inheritance of acquired characters". According to this theory-
(a) Environment keeps on changing and thus, influence the organism by creating new needs. The new needs are met with by producing new structures. The changed environment put certain old organs out of use.
(b) A constant use of an organ results in its better devlopment whereas disuse leads to its ultimate degeneration and loss.
(c) The modifications produced during the whole life of an organism are transferred to its offspring. As the process is continued, after several generations, a new form (species) will be formed.
## Darwin's Theory of Evolution
Charles Darwin (1809-1882) gave this theory in his book entitled. 'The origin of Species'. Darwin's theory presumes "descent with modification" i.e., complex creatures evolve from more simplistic ancestors over time. Genetic mutations occur within an organism's genetic code; the beneficial mutations are preserved because they help in survival, a process known as "Natural Selection". These beneficial mutations are passed on to the next generation. Over the time, beneficial mutations accumulate and the result is an entirely different organism.
Fig. Charles Robert Darwin
Darwin found that some fossils resembled present day forms while others were different, though related. Plants and animals of different places were different, but they had the same type of climate. In Galapagos islands, he fould different species of finches (sparrow-like birds) having varied forms of beaks, size and food habits. All of them were related to finches of mainland. Darwin was fascinated by the creation of large number of breeds of animals by human efforts. He, therefore concluded that variations occur in organisms. Out of these variations, the ones suited to organisms were selected by nature and are inherited. On accumulation, they produce new species. These conclusions were publised in 1859 in his book "Origin of species by Means of Natural Selection. The Preservation of Races in the struggle of Life". These books popularly called 'Origin of Species'.
Theory of Natural Selection (Darwin, 1859): This theory states that organic evolution occurs through natural selection and accumulation of inheritable variations which provide structural and functional superiority to some individuals over others in their survival and reproduction.
The important features of this theory are as follows :
(i) Reproduction. Organisms have a very high reproductive potential, producing a very large number of offsprings.
(ii) Limited food and space. There is a limitation of food and space an earth. Therefore, populations of different species cannot increase beyond a certain limit.
(iii) Struggle for existence. There is a competition amongst organisms to obtain optimum resources. The competition is maximum amongst members of the same species, i.e., intraspecific competition.
(iv) Variations. A large number of variations occur in size, structure, physiology and behaviour of individuals of a species. The variations are of three types-useful, neutral and harmful.
(v) Natural Selection (Survival of the Fittest). In the struggle for existence, only those individuals survive and reproduce which have the most useful variations.
(vi) Inheritance of useful Variations. Individuals with useful variations reproduce and transfer the variations to the next generation. Next generation repeats the process of formation of new variations and natural selection, a process called a continuous selection.
(vii) Formation of new species. Accumulation of variations produces a completely new species.

## How does Evolution Occur ?
Darwin's Theory of Natural Selection has been divided into five parts to explain clearly how evolution happens in nature.
1. Organisms produce a large number of offsprings.
2. Every individual must struggle to survive as there are not enough resources.
3. There are variations within a species.
4. Some variations allow members of a species to survive and reproduce better than others.
5. Organisms that survive and reproduce pass their traits to their offsprings and therefore the helpful traits gradually appear in more and more of the population.
This example of how brown hares in polar region became white hares will demonstrate each of the above points.
(a) There were brown hares living in cold, snowy polar region. Many of them were killed and eaten by other animals very few survived.
(b) Hares were competing for life, trying to avoid predators to survive.
(c) As a result, a few albino (white) hares were born due to mutation in the genes.
(d) This variation helped albino hares to hide. Predators had a hard time finding the albino hares in the snow and hence they were able to survive and reproduce.
(e) In the following generations, more white hares survived and reproduced resulting in an entire population of white hare. The albino hares have adapted to fit better in their environment than the brown hares. This is called Natural Selection. Natural selection is the phenomenon where nature selects traits favourable to the species in its environment.
Now, if we examine the fifth point in the example, survival of more albino hare in the population changed the colour characteristic from normal brown to white, over a period of time. This change took place only in a small population of hare living in cold and snowy regions i.e., there is an accidental change or mutation in the genes in a small population, even if this change gives no survival advantage to the possessor. This phenomenon is called Genetic Drift and it is a random genetic change in a population. The elimination of the genes of certain traits from the small population, when a section of the species population dies of natural reasons or imigrates to other region, is called Genetic drift. It alters the gene frequency of the remaining population.
## Speciation
The origin of new species from the existing one due to reproductive isolation of a part of its population is called Speciation. The geographical isolation make sub-populations become more and more different from each other due to geographical barriers. Ultimately, reproductive isolation occurs between individuals of these two groups i.e., they will no longer be able to exchange genetic material between them. At this point, natural selection works on the genetic variation within that population and results in formation of few species.
## CONNECTING TOPIC
## Four types of speciation occur in nature. They are:
1. Allopatric speciation: Population splits into two geographically isolated regions and each population undergoes genotypic and/or phenotypic divergence. e.g., Darwin's finches
2. Peripatric speciation: New species are formed in isolated small peripheral populations that are prevented from exchanging genes with the main population. Genetic drift plays an important role here e.g., underground mosquito is a variant of Culex pipiens.
3. Parapatric speciation: Two populations are partially seperated geographically, so individuals of each species may come in contact with the other from time to time, but reduced fitness of the heterozygote prevents breeding between the two species. e.g., Ensoltina salamanders.
4. Sympatric speciation refers to the formation of two or more descendant species from a single ancestral species all occupying the same geographical location. e.g., cichlids in East Africa.

## Artificial Selection
Artificial selection is the process by which man selects traits which are useful to him for improving the qualities of domesticated plants and animals. New species have been created by domesticated Animal Husbandry e.g., reproductive isolation leads to differences in the species of fruit flies after eight generations using different food types.
Hybridisation between two different species sometimes leads to a distinct phenotype. For e.g. Mariana mallard.
The individual having the desired traits are selected and allowed to interbreed among themselves. This process of artificial selection when repeated for many generations produces a new breed. For example many useful plants like wheat, rice, pulses and vegetables are produced through this method. The plants like cabbage, cauliflower, broccoli kholrabi and kale are all formed from a wild cabbage species.

Different organisms show common features which provides evidence in favour of evolution i.e., they can be considered to have been evolved from common ancestor. The more the number of features the organisms/species have in common, the more closely they are related. The more closely they are related, the more recently they would have had a common ancestor. The following sources provide evidence for evolution :
(i) Homologous Organs : These are organs having same structure, but may be similar or different in functions. Example, forelimbs of man (arm or hand), whale (flipper or paddle), bird (wings), lizard and frog. All have the same selection but modified for various functions. This indicates that they all have inherited a common plan from a common ancestor.

## Fossils
Fossils are organic remains of plants and animals that existed long ago. An entire organism or a part of its body was buried in the sediments of earth resulting in the formation of fossils. The study of fossils indicates the structural features of organisms that are not alive and their comparison with the existing forms which gives an indication as to how evolution has taken place. Fossils are found deeply embedded in sedimentary rocks. Most recent rock layers are on top, the oldest fossils are found in the deepest rocks. In many places, old rocks have become exposed due to erosion and weathering.
Sedimentary rocks are not the only source of fossils. Insects have been found in amber, while the entire woolly mammoths have been found to be preserved in ice.
Fig. Fossil formation
By arranging fossils in the order in which they have appeared on earth many fossil series have been established. Such series demonstrate how various forms of life have changed gradually over a period of millions of years. As a result, the evolution of a number of animals such as horse, camel and elephant, has been clearly understood. Plenty of fossil evidence is now available so as to establish human evolution too.
Learn More
Dating of fossils is the process of finding out the age of fossil. The most effective way of dating is to measure the amount of radioactive material in the rock or soil. Half-life periods are calculated on the basis of the ratio of the radio active material and its products of decay. The most commonly used radioactive materials are uranium and carbon -14 .
## Human Evolution
The evolutionary sequence from earliest known human ancestors is not a straight line but is a 'tree' with many dead ends. As the fossil evidences are limited, the scientists are unable to tell about number of human species that have existed and which was ancestor of the others. The 'tree' of human evolution provides a simple guide to relationship but does not clearly indicate ancestry.

Paranthropus was strongly built "man-apes" that lived in southern and eastern Africa between three and one million years ago. Australopithecus are thought to be the earliest hominids i.e., human-like people. They had a small brain and projecting jaw. They stood upright and walked on two feet. This is known from their leg bones and backbone, and also from the footprints found in Tanzania ( 3.7 million years old).
Homo habilus or the "handyman" is the earliest known species of Homo (genes to which the modern day human belongs). It lived in the woodlands and Savannahs of Africa. It had a brain size of 650-800 ml. It made and used simple stone tools, and was a successful forager and scavenger.
Homo erectus was the first human to leave Africa and move to Europe and Asia. It had a sloping forehead, flattish face and brain size between $850-1100 \mathrm{ml}$. These humans exploited more habitats than their ancestors. They were the first ones to use fire. Homo neanderthals were the first humans to have adopted to life in cold climates of Europe and Asia. They had strong physique and large brain. They wore clothes, made a range of tools and used fire to keep warm. They were the first humans to bury their dead. Homo sapiens or the "modern human" first evolved in Africa. They had a large brain, were considerably intelligent and had developed the ability to use language. Modern man took control of their surroundings as they developed agriculture, societies and technology.

## Origin of life on Earth
Darwin's theory of evolution tells us how life evolved from simple to more complex forms and Mendel's experiments give us the mechanism for the inheritance of traits from one generation to the next. But neither tells us anything about how life began on earth in the first place.
Haldane's Theory - J.B.S. Haldane, a British scientist (who became a citizen of India later), suggested in 1929 that life must have developed from the simple inorganic molecules which were present on earth soon after it was formed. He speculated that the conditions on earth at that time, which were far from the conditions we see today, could have given rise to more complex organic molecules that were necessary for life. The first primitive organisms would arise from further chemical synthesis.
How did these organic molecules arise?
Miller and Urey's Experiment - An answer was suggested by the experiment conducted by Stanley L. Miller and Harold C. Urey in 1953. They assembled an atmosphere similar to that thought to exist on early earth (this had molecules like ammonia, methane and hydrogen sulphide, but no oxygen) over water. This was maintained at a temperature just below $100^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ and sparks were passed through the mixture of gases to simulate lightning. At the end of a week, $15 \%$ of the carbon (from methane) had been converted to simple compounds of carbon including amino acids which make up protein molecules.
## Evolution Vs Progress
Is evolution of species a progress to higher, more complex and intelligent forms, as often thought?
If one sees the tree of human evolution, one can see that there are multiple branches at each and every stage of evolution. Speciation does not intend to convey the dying out of an inferior species giving way to superior ones. Species adapt to the conditions available in their environment. Some older and simpler species live on quite effectively. Scorpion is one of the most primitive invertebrate which has lived successfully for millions of years; it can withstand heat and nuclear radiation levels that would easily kill man.
The process of evolution in nature has resulted in every organism reaching a certain stage by various processes. The same process has brought man to the stage of a human being and considers him as a specific kind of species distinct from other ones. In fact, there is no real progress in the idea of evolution. It simply implies the generation and shaping of diverse life forms by environmental selection. The only progressive trend in evolution seems to be that more and more complex body designs have emerged over time.

1. Evolution is the sequence of gradual changes which takes place in the primitive organisms over millions of years and new species are produced.
Darwin's theory of Evolution: Charles Robert Darwin gave the theory of evolution in his famous book, 'The Origin of Species'.
It known as 'The Theory of Natural Selection' (Darwinism).
## According to Darwin's theory of evolution :
(i) There is natural variation within any population and some individuals have more favourable variations than others.
(ii) Population remains fairly constant even though all species produce a large number of offsprings.
(iii) This is due to 'competition' or struggle for existence between same and different species.
(iv) The struggle for survival within population eliminates the unfit individuals and those with 'favourable variations' survive and pass on these variations to their progeny to continue. This is called natural selection.
(v) The favourable variations are accumulated over a long time period leading to the origin of a new species.
2. The main causes of evolution are as follows:
(i) Mutations are random, undirected changes in DNA composition. Although most mutations are neutral or harmful to the organism, some prove advantageous in certain environments. Mutations are rare and do not change allele frequencies very much, but they provide the raw material for evolution.
(ii) In gene flow between populations, if the alleles that a migrant carries are different from those in the population from which the migrant comes or to which it migrates, allele frequencies will change.
(iii) In any population, chance events kill or prevent reproduction by some of the individuals. If the population is small, chance events may eliminate a disproportionate number of individuals who bear a particular allele, thereby greatly changing the allele frequency in the population. This change is termed as a genetic drift.
(iv) Many organisms do not mate randomly. If only certain members of a population can mate, then the next generation of organisms in the population will all be offspring of this selected group, whose allele frequencies may differ from those of the population as a whole. Population bottleneck and founder effect, two types of genetic drift, illustrate the consequences of small population size on allele frequency.
