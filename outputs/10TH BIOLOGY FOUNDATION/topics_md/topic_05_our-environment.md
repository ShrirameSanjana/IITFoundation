## ECOSYSTEM
The term Ecosystem was coined by Sir Arthur Tansley in 1935. Ecosystem is essentially a technical term for nature. Ecosystem can be defined as a self-sustaining structural and functional unit of the biosphere. This system depends upon the sun for its energy.
## Components of Ecosystem
An ecosystem has two basic components :-
(i) Abiotic components include all non-living components that affect the distribution, number, metabolism and behaviour of organisms in an ecosystem. These include
(a) Inorganic substances like carbon, nitrogen, oxygen, water, etc. These occur either in the form of compounds dissolved in water, in the soil or in free state in the air.
(b) Organic compounds like carbohydrates, proteins, lipids, nucleic acid, etc. These are present in living organisms and dead organic matter. Dead organic matter is broken down by the action of decomposers into inorganic substances for recycling.
(c) Climatic factors include light, temperature, humidity, wind, rainfall, water, sunlight and edaphic factors such as soil texture or topography.
(ii) Biotic components include the living components of the ecosystem.
The biotic components usually have
(a) Producers that convert light energy into chemical energy and then into food, e.g., plants.
(b) Consumers that depend upon producers for food e.g., animals and few plants.
(c) Decomposers that take the chemicals from producers and consumers and break them into simpler form so that they can be reused. e.g., fungi, bacteria, etc.
An ecosystem is a self-contained unit of living organisms (plants, animals and decomposers), and their non-living environment (soil, air and water). The examples of ecosystems are: a grassland (meadow); a forest; a desert; a mountain; a pond; a lake; a river; and sea. When we say that a forest is an ecosystem then it means the physical environment of the forest like soil, air and water alongwith all the plants and animals that occur in the forest.
Different ecosystems are in contact with one another. Though they are distinct, but exchange of materials can occur amongst them.
Ecosystem can be small (rotting piece of wood) or large (e.g., forest, ocean), temporary (e.g. is red pond) or permanent (e.g. lake), natural and artificial.
The study of an ecosystem is called Ecology. The term Ecology was introduced by a German biologist, Hannus Reiter (1868). Ecology (Oekologie) is derived from Greek words; Oikos meaning 'house' and logos meaning 'study'. Ecology is defined as a scientific study of plants and animals in relation to each other and to their environment. It is one of the disciplines of environmental science.
## Types of Ecosystem
Depending on the human interference and degree of the impact of authropogenic activities, ecosystem can be classified as-
1. Natural Ecosystem : These are self-regulating ecosystems without any direct human interference.
Natural ecosystem are of two types-
- Aquatic Ecosystem This ecosystem is found in water bodies which may be either fresh water or sea water. e.g., ponds, lakes, rivers (fresh water), estuarine, marine (salt water)
- Terrestrial ecosystems - This ecosystem occurs an land. They are of three types desert, grass land and forest.
2. Artifical ecosystem : These ecosystems are created and maintained by human beings. Agroecosystem is the largest manmade ecosystem and garden is a common artificial ecosystem maintained by man. Aquarium is also an example of an artificial ecosystem.
## Food Chain
Food in an ecosystem can be transferred from one organism to the other through food chain. The sequence of living organism in an ecosystem in which one organism consumes another organism to transfer food energy is called food chain.
Food chains are attempts to demonstrate the fate of organisms in an ecosystem.
Let us have a look at the ecosystem operating in a grassland. Here, is a lot of grass (producer) is eaten by small insects (consumer), and the insects are killed and eaten by a toad, which in turn is caught by a snake. The snake is then eaten by hawk. Traditionally, food chains are drawn with the producer at the bottom. Successive trophic levels are connected by arrows which lead from the organism being eaten to the organisms eating it. Although, decomposers play an important role in an ecosystem, they are usually not included. It is rare for a food chain to have more than five levels. Herbivores (plant eaters) are known as primary consumers; first-level carnivores (animal eaters) as secondary consumers; second-level carnivores as tertiary consumers; thirdlevel carnivores as quaternary consumers; and so on. Omnivores (both plant and animal eaters) can also be present at any trophic level of food chain after producers.

## Trophic Level
The trophic level of an organism is the position it occupies in a food chain. Food chains start at trophic level 1 with primary producers, move to herbivores or omnivores at level 2, carnivores at level 3 and finish with apex predaters at level 4 or 5 . The food chain occurs on land as well as in water. Few examples are-
In the aquatic food chain, the phytoplankton is eaten by zooplankton. The zooplankton is eaten by the fish and the fishes are eaten by the seal. The predator seals are then eaten by the shark.
The interdependence of the populations within a food chain helps to maintain the balance of plant and animal populations within a community. For example, where there are too many giraffes, there will be insufficient trees and shrubs for them to eat. Many giraffes will starve and die. If there are fewer giraffes then food is available for the lions to eat and some lions will starve to death. When there are fewer lions, the giraffe population will explode. So, the predatory-prey population balance each other.
## Characteristics of Food Chain
1. It shows nutritive interaction between living organisms.
2. It is always straight.
3. There is a unidirectional flow of energy. (sun → producers → consumers)
4. There are usually 3-4 trophic levels in a food chain.
5. Organisms can occupy different positions in different food chains.
6. As per second law of thermodynamics, $80-90 \%$ of energy is lost as heat at each trophic level.
## Importance of Food Chain
1. It provides pathways for passage of food and its energy in the ecosystem.
2. It provides information about the living components of the ecosystem.
3. Food chains indicate the interactions and interdependence among the different organisms.
4. It helps in identifying the effect of decrease or increase in the population at a trophic level.
5. If a non-degradable chemical happens to enter a food chain at one level, it will undergo increase in concentration with rise of trophic levels (Biomagnification).
## Food Web
Food web is a network of food chains operating in an ecosystem which connects at various trophic levels to form a number of feeding connections amongst different organisms of the biotic community. Here, each organism is eaten by two to many other kinds of organisms which in turn are eaten by several other organisms. For example, deer is prey for jackal, wolf and tiger. Rabbit is prey for fox, jackal, hawk and a number of carnivores. Mouse is food for hawk, snake, fox and wild cat. Likewise, wolf can feed on fox, jackal, wild cats, deer, goat, sheep, etc. A rabbit can feed on a avariety of plants and does not starve if its favourite food becomes scarce. It begins to feed on alternate plants. Similarly, if the population of rabbit decreases, its predators begin to eat mice, shrews and squirrels. Meanwhile, rabbits increase in number and the balance of nature is restored. There are many food chains in a food web.
Fig. A simple terrestrial food web
Food web is further classified into two broad categories :
(i) Grazing web : This food web has plants or photosynthetic organisms at its base followed by herbivores/omnivores and carnivores.
(ii) Detrital web : This food web has decomposers e.g., bacteria or fungi at its base that feed on dead organic matter. These two food webs are often connected to each other. For e.g., mushrooms produced by decomposers in the detrital web become a food source for deer, squirrels, and mice in the grazing web.
## Characteristics of Food Web
1. Food webs are never straight as they are formed by interlinking of food chains.
2. Food web provides alternative pathways of food availability. For examples, if a particular species of producer is destroyed, the herbivore can feed on an alternative species.
3. Greater alternatives available in a food web makes the ecosystem more stable.
4. Food webs Reeps a check on overpopulation of species.
5. Food webs help in ecosystem development.

## Ecological Pyramids
Charles Elton (1927) developed the concept of ecological pyramids. They are also called 'Eltonian pyramids'.
Ecological pyramids can be defined as graphical representation of ecological parameters like number of individuals or the amount of biomass or the amount of energy present at various trophic levels of a food chain, with producer forming the base and top carnivore at the apex.
An ecological pyramid may be upright (tapering towards the tip), or inverted (wide at the tip) or spindle shaped (broad in the middle and narrow above and below). On the basis of the parameters used, ecological pyramids are of three types:
(i) Pyramid of Numbers is a graphical representation of number of individuals at different trophic levels in a food chain in an ecosystem. The pyramid is upright in case of grassland and pond ecosystem where the number of organisms decrease from producer to top carnivore level. An inverted pyramid is seen in parasitic food chain where a single oak tree (producer) supports a large number of herbivorous birds followed by larger number of parasites and then even greater number of hyperparasites.
Fig. Pyramid of Numbers
(ii) Pyramid of Biomass is a graphical representation of biomass (total amount of organic matter in an ecosystem at any time) per unit area at different trophic levels. In a terrestrial ecosystem, the maximum biomass occurs in producers, and there is a progressive decrease in biomass from lower to higher trophic levels. Thus, the pyramid of biomass in a terrestrial ecosystem is upright. In an aquatic ecosystem, the pyramid of biomass is inverted or spindle shaped where the biomass of phytoplanktons (producers) is less as compared to the higher trophic levels.
Fig. Pyramid of Biomass
(iii) Pyramid of Energy is a graphical representation of the amount of energy trapped per unit time and area at different trophic levels of a food chain with producers forming the base and the top carnivores at the tip. Pyramid of energy is always upright as at each transfer, $80-90 \%$ of the energy is used up and only $10 \%$ of the energy is available to the next trophic level.
Fig. Pyramid of Energy
## Energy Transfer in an Ecosystem
Linderman (1942) put forth the 'Ten percent Law' for the transfer of energy from one trophic level to the next. According to this law, during the transfer of organic food from one trophic level to the next, only about ten percent of the organic matter is stored as flesh and the remaining is lost during transfer or used up in respiration.
Plants utilise sun energy for primary production and can store only $10 \%$ of the utilised energy for the herbivores. When the plants are consumed by animals, about $10 \%$ of the energy in the food is fixed and available for next trophic level i.e. the carnivores. When a carnivore consumes that animal, only about $10 \%$ of energy is fixed for the higher level. So, at each transfer 80-90\% of potential energy is dissipated as heat and only $10-20 \%$ of energy is available for the next trophic level.

## Pollution
Pollution is derived from a Latin word 'polluere' which means contamination. Pollution is defined as the introduction of substances (contaminants) into the natural environment that causes adverse changes. These substances that cause pollution are called as pollutants. Pollution can be broadly classified into following categories -
Air pollution - The contamination of the earth's atmosphere by the release of various harmful substances like chemicals, harmful gases or fumes etc. is called air pollution. The various sources of air pollution are burning of fossils fuels, release of harmful fumes/chemicals from the industries/factories and mining operations. It can cause various respiratory, cardiovascular and skin diseases in humans.
Water pollution - The contamination of water bodies by water pollutants (contaminants) is termed as water pollution. The various sources of water pollution are human and animal wastes, untreated industrial sewage, agricultural runoff containing pesticides and fertilizers etc. Sometimes it can be natural as in case of eutrophication (nutrient enrichment of water body resulting in oxygen depletion).
Soil Pollution - The contamination or degradation of soil by the presence of chemicals or manmade substances is termed as soil pollution. The various sources include acid rain, industrial waste disposal, agrochemicals (pesticides and fertilizers), oil spills etc. Noise Pollution - It is usually man made and is caused by the excessive amount of noise thereby disrupting natural balance. The various sources are social events, household noises, heavy machines in industries, noises from construction sites etc.

## Global Warming
Scientist have determined that a number of human activities are contributing to global warming by adding excessive amounts of greenhouse gases to the atmosphere $\left(\mathrm{CO}_{2}, \mathrm{CH}_{4}, \mathrm{CFCs}, \mathrm{N}_{2} \mathrm{O}\right)$. Carbon dioxide accumulate in the atmosphere and trap heat that normally would exit into outer space.
While many greenhouse gases occur naturally and are needed to create the greenhouse effect. They keep the earth warm enough to support life. The human use of fossil fuels is the main source of greenhouse gas emission. By driving cars, using electricity from coal-fired power plants, or heating our homes with oil or natural gas, we release carbon dioxide and other heat trapping gases into the atmosphere. Deforestation is another significant source of greenhouse gases as fewer trees convert less carbon
dioxide to oxygen.
During the 150 years of the industrial age, the atmospheric concentration of carbon dioxide has increased by 31 percent. Over the same period, the level of atmospheric methane has risen by 151 percent, mostly from agricultural activities such as raising cattle and growing rice.
As the concentration of greenhouse gases increases, more heat is trapped in the atmosphere and less escapes back into space. This increase in trapped heat changes the climate and alters weather patterns. This may affect species extinction, the length of seasons, and can cause natural disasters (floods, storms etc.).
## Few actions one can take to Reduce Global Warming
1. Reduce, Reuse, Recycle - Reduce waste by choosing reusable products instead of disposables. Recycle paper, plastic, newspaper, glass and aluminium cans. By recycling half of your household waste, you can save 2,400 pounds of carbon dioxide.
2. Use less heat and air conditioning - Proper insulation of the room can lower the heating or airconditioning cost by more than 25 percent. Setting the thermostat just 2 degrees lower in winter and higher in summer could save about 2,000 pounds of carbon dioxide each year.
3. Change a light bulb - Replace regular light bulbs with Compact Fluorescent Light (CFL) bulbs. CFLs last 10 times longer than incandescent bulbs, use two-third less energy, and give off 70 percent less heat.
4. Drive less and Drive smart - Lesser driving means fewer emissions. Explore the community mass transit system and check out options for car-pooling to work or school.
When you do drive, make sure your car is running efficiently. Every gallon of gas you save not only helps your budget but also keeps 20 pounds of carbon dioxide out of the atmosphere.
5. Buy energy-efficient products - Buy appliances which are energy efficient. Avoid products that come with excess packaging, especially plastic molded and other packaging that can't be recycled.
6. Use less hot water - Set the water heater at 120 degrees to save energy and make sure that it is well insulated. Wash clothes in warm or cold water to reduce the use of hot water and the energy required to produce it. This change alone can save atleast 500 pounds of carbon dioxide annually in most households. Use energy saving settings in your dishwasher and let the dishes air-dry.
7. Use the 'Off' switch - Save electricity and reduce global warming by turning off lights when you leave a room, and using only as much light as you need. Remember to turn off the television, music system and computer when you are not using them.
It is good idea to turn off water when you are not using it especially, while brushing your teeth, washing your vehicle, etc. In this way you'll reduce the water bill and also save energy needed to pump water to your house.
8. Plant a tree - Trees absorb carbon dioxide and give off oxygen during photosynthesis. Trees are an integral part of the natural atmospheric cycle, but very few are left to counter the increase in carbon dioxide caused by human activities. A single tree can absorb about one ton of carbon dioxide during its lifetime.
These steps will take you a long way towards reducing energy use and also your monthly budget. And less energy use means less dependence on the fossil fuels that create greenhouse gases and contribute to global warming.
## OZONE DEPLETION
About $90 \%$ of earth's ozone is concentrated in the stratosphere. Stratospheric ozone protects the earth from potentially damaging doses of ultraviolet radiations. Its concentration is maximum at $23-25 \mathrm{~km}$ above equator and $11-18 \mathrm{~km}$ above poles. This rich zone of ozone in the stratosphere is called ozone layer or ozonosphere. It is also called ozone shield because this region intercepts the ultraviolet radiations.
## Ozone layer
In the stratosphere, ozone is being photodissociated and generated simultaneously by the absorption of harmful ultraviolet radiations coming from the sun.
These two reactions are in equilibrium, hence maintaining a steady concentration of ozone in the stratosphere.
Thinning of ozone layer is called ozone depletion. With the technological advancement in the past century various man made substances have caused depletion of ozone from the stratosphere. Thinning of ozone was noticed in 1985 above the Antartica, by Farman and coworkers. In 1987, the United Nations Environment Programme (UNEP) succeeded in reaching an agreement control CFC production. The widely known substances which cause ozone depletion are :
- Refrigerants (CFCs-chloroflurocarbons and HCFs- hydrofluorocarbons)
- Insulating foams
- Solvents
- Aerosols
Primary health issues caused by ozone depletion are because of the easy penetration of ultraviolet rays from the sun. It has harmful effects on man, animals and plants:
- Sunburn
- Skin Aging
- Various forms of skin cancer.
- Eye problems like cataract, cornea damages, etc.
- Attacks immune system and makes humans prone to infections/diseases.
This is a global issue and environmentalists are working towards developing an effective strategy to check the thinning of ozone layer. The Helsinki Declaration in 1989 had majority of the nations pledge to phase out CFC by the year 2000. Efforts are still on and each one of us has to contribute to this.

## WASTE
Accumulation of wastes due to its improper disposal is a major problem in a country. Population in our country has been growing at a rapid rate. With this increase, there has been an increase in the amount of wastes being produced, especially in the cities. Every person on an average generates about 400 to 500 grams of wastes per day. At this rate, in a city of about 10 lakh people, around 500 tones of wastes is being produced per day.
In order to understand the problem and work towards a solution, one must know the types of wastes being generated. Waste can be classified into two categories :
(1) Biodegradable Wastes : These are the wastes which are broken down and disposed off naturally by saprophytes or decomposers. Common saprophytes are microscopic organisms or microbes like bacteria and fungi. They secrete enzymes which bring about the solubilisation of wastes. Biodegradable wastes are generally organic wastes, e.g., garbage, sewage, livestock waste, used tea leaves, waste paper, waste cotton, wool, leather, wood and jute articles, food leftovers, etc. Besides natural degradation by microbes, they can also be disposed off through waste treatment plants or converted into an agricultural resource of manure and compost. Biodegradable wastes should not be allowed to pile up anywhere as they attract wildlife.
(2) Non-biodegradable Wastes : These are the wastes which cannot be broken down by decomposers because they do not have enzymes for the same. We cannot eat coal to obtain energy because we do not possess enzymes for the same. Most of the non-biodegradable wastes are human made, e.g., plastic, crockery, metallic cans, glass articles, polythene bags, synthetic fibres, silver foil, many pesticides etc. Industries also produce a number of non-biodegradable wastes consisting of heavy metals, synthetic chemicals, toxins, etc., Some of these wastes can be broken down by heat, pressure and oxidation under specific conditions.

5. Incineration:- 'Incineration' means 'reducing to ashes' i.e. burning of substance at very high temperature (above $1000^{\circ}$ C) to form ash. This reduces the volume of the waste. Incineration is carried out in a furnace called incinerator. All organic matter present in the waste is removed as carbon dioxide and water. The ash left behind is removed from time to time and used in landfill.
6. Sewage treatment:- The ultimate return of used water to the environment is done by treating sewage. Sewage treatment involves three stages-primary, secondary and tertiary. The solids are first separated from the waste water. The dissolved biological matter is slowly converted into a solid mass by using water-borne bacteria. Finally, the biological solids are neutralized and then disposed off or reduced. The treated water is disinfected chemically. The final effluent is either discharged into a stream or water body or used for irrigation.
7. Bioremediation:- This is a rapidly developing clean up technology. Cleaning the environment with biological options such as microbes and plants is called bioremediation. Some naturally occuring bacteria and other micro- organisms have the capability to degrade or absorb or detoxify the wastes such as heavy metals.

(I) Producers: The primary producers of food are the grasses. If herbs and shrubs are present, they also contribute to the primary production of food.
(II) Consumers: The consumers in a grassland ecosystem are of three levels.
(a) Primary consumers: These feed directly from the grasses (grazing) and include herbivores such as Cows, Buffaloes, Goats, Rabbits, Mouse etc. and also insects, termites, centipede, millipedes etc.
(b) Secondary consumers: These consumers are the carnivorous animals such as snakes, lizard, jackal, foxes, frogs etc. which feed on the primary consumers.
(c) Tertiary consumers: Hawk, Eagles and vultures constitute the tertiary consumer in the grassland ecosystem which preys upon the secondary and primary consumer.
(III) Decomposers: The organic matter of the grassland is decomposed by the microbes like actinomycetes, fungi (Mucor, Aspergillus, Rhizopus, Penicillium, and Cladosporium), aerobic and anaerobic soil bacteria etc. They release the minerals back into the soil thus making the soil fertile.
