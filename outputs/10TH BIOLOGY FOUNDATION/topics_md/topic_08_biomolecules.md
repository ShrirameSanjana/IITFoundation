## BIOMOLECULES
Molecule involved in the maintenance and metabolic processes of living organisms. Molecules with molecular weights less than 1000 Da are referred to as micromolecules while those with large size, complex structure \& found in acid insoluble fraction are called macromolecules.

## MICROMOLECULES
Micro molecules are molecules of low molecular weight (less than 1000 Da ) and have higher solubility. These include water, minerals, amino acids and nucleotides.
## Water
Water is essential for life. Water is the major constituent of almost all life forms. Most animals and plants contain more than $60 \%$ water by volume. Without water life would probably never have developed on our planet.
Water has a very simple atomic structure. This structure consists of two hydrogen atoms bonded to one oxygen atom. The nature of the atomic structure of water causes its molecules to have unique electrochemical properties. The hydrogen side of the water molecule has a slight positive charge. On the other side of the molecule, a negative charge exists. This molecular polarity causes water to be a powerful solvent and is responsible for its strong surface tension. The resulting polarity of charge causes molecules of water to be attracted to each other forming strong molecular bonds.

- Water in a pure state has a neutral $\mathbf{p H}$. As a result, pure water is neither acidic nor basic. Water changes its pH when substances are dissolved in it. Rain has a naturally acidic pH of about 5.6 because it contains naturally derived carbon dioxide and sulphur dioxide molecules.
- Water conducts heat more easily than any liquid except mercury. This fact causes large bodies of liquid water like lakes and oceans to have essentially a uniform vertical temperature profile.
- Water molecules exist in liquid form over an important range of temperature from 0-100 ${ }^{\circ}$ celsius. This range allows water molecules to exist as a liquid in most places on our planet.
- Water is a universal solvent. It is able to dissolve a large number of different chemical compounds. This feature also enables water to carry solvent nutrients in runoff, infiltration, groundwater flow, and living organisms.
- Water has a high surface tension. In other words, water is adhesive and elastic, and tends to aggregate in drops rather than spread out over a surface as a thin film. This phenomenon also causes water to stick to the sides of vertical structures despite gravity's downward pull. Water's high surface tension allows for the formation of water droplets and waves, allows plants to move water (and dissolved nutrients) from their roots to their leaves, and the movement of blood through tiny vessels in the bodies of some animals.
- Water molecules are the only substance on Earth that exist in all three physical states of matter: solid, liquid and gas. Incorporated in the change of state are massive amounts of heat exchange. This feature plays an important role in the redistribution of heat energy in the Earth's atmosphere. In terms of heat being transferred into the atmosphere, approximately $3 / 4^{\text {th }}$ of this process is accomplished by the evaporation and condensation of water.
- The freezing of water molecules causes their mass to occupy a larger volume. When water freezes it expands rapidly adding about $9 \%$ by volume. Fresh water has a maximum density at around $4^{\circ}$ celsius. Water is the only substance on this planet where the maximum density of its mass does not occur when it becomes solidified.
## Minerals
An organism may have upto 40 elements. They are divisible into two categories, essential and nonessential elements.
An essential element is the one which takes part in nutrition, growth, development and functioning of the organisms. The organisms are unable to complete vegetative or reproductive growth in the absence of the essential element. Deficiency of the element produces disorders which can be rectified only by the supply of the element, e.g., carbon, hydrogen, oxygen, nitrogen, calcium, magnesium, manganese, etc. Carbon, hydrogen and oxygen are called framework elements, as they produce cell wall and storage products. $\mathrm{C}, \mathrm{H}, \mathrm{O}, \mathrm{N}, \mathrm{S}$ and P are called protoplasmic elements, as they form protoplasmic or organic substances like proteins, nucleic acids, etc. Plants have only 17 essential elements while animals have $25-27$ essential elements.
A nonessential element is the one that is not involved in nutrition, metabolism, growth and functioning of the organism. The organism does not show any disorder in the absence of that element, e.g., bromine.
Minerals are substances present in the earth's crust. Deficiency or absence of the essential mineral produces disorders which can be corrected only by the supply of that mineral. Minerals occur in the living beings in two states (i) complex state as component of organic molecules (e.g., phosphorus, sulphur). (ii) inorganic molecules or ions (e.g., $\mathrm{Na}^{+}, \mathrm{K}^{+}, \mathrm{Ca}^{2+}, \mathrm{Mg}^{2+}, \mathrm{Cl}^{-}$). Living beings maintain a balance between the amounts of minerals present in the complexed and free states.
Depending upon their concentration required, essential minerals are differentiated into two categories - major or minor (in case of animals) or macronutrients (in case of plants)
Major minerals : Calcium, Phosphorus, Sodium, Chlorine, Magnesium, Potassium, Nitrogen and Sulphur.
Minor minerals : Iron, Copper, Cobalt, Manganese, Molybdenum, Zinc, Fluorine, Iodine and Selenium. Minerals required in extremely low concentration are known as ultra-trace minerals, e.g., Silicon, Vanadium, Aluminium, Boron, Chromium, Tin.
## Amino acid
Amino acids are organic compounds containing an amino group and an acidic group as substituents on the same carbon i.e., the $\alpha$-carbon. Hence, they are called $\alpha$-amino acids. They are substituted methanes. There are four substituent groups occupying the four valency positions. These are hydrogen, carboxyl group, amino group and a variable group designated as R group. Based on the nature of R group, there are many amino acids. However, those which occur in proteins are only of twenty types. The R group in these proteinaceous amino acids could be a hydrogen ( the amino acid is called glycine), a methyl group (alanine), hydroxy methyl (serine), etc.
The chemical and physical properties of amino acids are essentially of the amino, carboxyl and the R functional groups. Based on number of amino and carboxyl groups, there are acidic (e.g., glutamic acid), basic (lysine) and neutral (valine) amino acids. Similarly, there are aromatic amino acids (tyrosine, phenylalanine, tryptophan). A particular property of the amino acid is the ionizable nature of $-\mathrm{NH}_{2}$ and -COOH groups. Hence in solutions of different pH , the structure of amino acid changes.

## Nucleotide
Living organisms have a number of carbon compounds in which heterocyclic rings can be found. Some of these are nitrogen bases - adenine, guanine, cytosine, uracil, and thymine. When found attached to a sugar, they are called nucleosides. If a phosphate group is also found esterified to the sugar they are called nucleotides. Adenosine, guanosine, thymidine, uridine and cytidine are nucleosides. Adenylic acid, thymidylic acid, guanylic acid, uridylic acid and cytidylic acid are nucleotides. Nucleic acids like DNA and RNA consist of nucleotides only. DNA and RNA function as genetic material.

## MACROMOLECULES
Macromolecules are large sized complex chemicals that have high molecular weight (M.W.), low solubility and complex formation. They usually produce colloidal complex. Macromolecules generally belong to four classes of organic compounds - carbohydrate, lipids, proteins and nucleic acids.
## Carbohydrates
Carbohydrates are the compounds of carbon, hydrogen and oxygen where hydrogen and oxygen are in the ratio of $2: 1$. Thus, they are regarded as hydrates of carbon.
Carbohydrates, often termed as sugars, are widely distributed in both plant and animal tissues. They are indispensable for living organisms, serving as skeletal structure in plant and in insects and crustaceans. They are also an important source of energy required for the various metabolic activities of living organisms, the energy being derived as a result of breakdown of carbohydrates into $\mathrm{CO}_{2}$ and water by the process of respiration. Carbohydrates are synthesised by green plants from $\mathrm{CO}_{2}$ and $\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}$ in the presence of sunlight by a process called as photosynthesis.
The name carbohydrate is used for the compound having general formula $\mathbf{C}_{\mathbf{x}}\left(\mathbf{H}_{\mathbf{2}} \mathbf{O}\right)_{\mathbf{y}}$. They are so called because they can be treated as hydrates of carbon. For example glucose $\left[\mathrm{C}_{6} \mathrm{H}_{12} \mathrm{O}_{6}\right]$ and sucrose $\left[\mathrm{C}_{12} \mathrm{H}_{22} \mathrm{O}_{11}\right]$ etc.
Carbohydrates can be classified into three groups.
## Monosaccharides
Monosaccharides, often called 'simple sugars', are compounds which possess a free aldehyde ( -CHO ) or ketonic ( $=\mathrm{CO}$ ) group and two or more hydroxyl (- OH ) groups. They cannot be hydrolysed into smaller units. Their general formula is $\mathrm{C}_{\mathrm{n}}\left(\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}\right)_{\mathrm{n}}$. Monosaccharides may be sub-divided into trioses, tetroses, pentoses, hexoses, heptoses, etc. depending upon the number of carbon atoms they possess; and into aldoses or ketoses depending upon whether they contain an aldehyde or ketonic group. ## Oligosaccharides
Oligosaccharides are composed of usually 2-9 monosaccharide units. Water molecule is eliminated and the units are linked through an oxygen bridge. It is a glycosidic linkage. The monosaccharide units may be the same or different in various oligosaccharides. An oligosaccharide yielding 2 molecules of monosaccharide as a disaccharide and so on.
## Polysaccharides
They are complex carbohydrates which are formed by polymerisation of large number of monosaccharide monomers (more than 9 but usually numerous). Polysaccharides are also called glycans. They are long chained. The chains may be branched or unbranched. Depending upon the composition, polysaccharides are of two types, homopolysaccharides and hetero-polysaccharides.
Let us perform a test to demonstrate the presence of simple carbohydrates (monosaccharides and some disaccharides).
Benedict's solution is used to test for simple carbohydrates. It is a blue coloured liquid that contains copper ions. When Benedict's solution and simple carbohydrates are heated, the solution changes to orange red/brick red. This reaction is caused by the reducing property of simple carbohydrates. The copper (II) ions in the Benedict's solution are reduced to copper (I) ions, which causes the colour change. Sometimes a brick red solid, copper oxide, precipitates out of the solution and collects at the bottom of the test tube.
## Significance of carbohydrates
(i) They act as bio-fuels to provide energy for functioning of living organisms.
(ii) They act as constituents of cell membrane.

## Cellulose
Cellulose is a polysaccharide consisting of a linear chain of several hundred to over ten thousand $\beta(1-4)$ linked D-glucose units. It is the structural component of primary cell wall of green plants. About $33 \%$ of all plant matter is cellulose.
For industrial use, it is obtained from wood pulp ( $40-50 \%$ ) and cotton ( $90 \%$ ). It is used to produce paper, rayon, etc. Converting cellulose into biofuel is still under investigation.

## Proteins
Proteins are high molecular mass, long chain polymers of $\alpha$-amino acids. The name protein is derived from the Greek word proteios which means of 'first importance'. Proteins are constituents of cells and hence are present in all living bodies. They contain the elements carbon, hydrogen, oxygen, nitrogen and sometimes sulphur and phosphorus.
Fig. Primary structure of protein
The amino acids are structural units of proteins. Amino acids are molecules containing an amine group, a carboxylic acid group and a side chain that varies between different amino acids. There are four substituent groups occupying the four valency positions. These are hydrogen, carboxyl group, amino group and a variable group designated as R group. There are twenty two amino acids present out of which twenty are encoded by the universal genetic code.
Polymerisation of amino acids creates proteins. The amine group of one amino acid and the carboxyl group of the second amino acid undergo a condensation reaction forming a peptide bond and a molecule of water.
Fig. Condensation of two amino acids to form a peptide bond
The most basic amino acid is arginine followed by lysine and histidine. Glycine is the simplest amino acid i.e., it does not have two isomers.
## Configuration of Protein Molecule
(i) Primary structure refers to the sequence of different amino acids in a polypeptide chain. This is determined by the gene corresponding to the protein. In this, the protein exists as a long chain. Such a polypeptide is non-functional. The amino acid present at the first (or left), is called N -terminal ( $-\mathrm{NH}_{2}$ group) amino acid and the last (or right), is called C -terminal (- COOH group) amino acid.
(ii) Secondary structures are alpha helix and beta strand formed by hydrogen bonding between the main chain peptide groups. e.g., keratin. When two or more, polypeptide chains are held together by intermolecular hydrogen bonds, the structure of protein is described as pleated sheet of silk fibre.
(iii) Tertiary structure refers to three-dimensional structure of a single protein molecule. Hydrogen, peptide and disulphide bonds are formed to fold the secondary structures into a compact globule.
(iv) Quaternary structure is a larger assembly of several polypeptide chains called subunits. Two or more polypeptide chains of tertiary structure are united by different types of bonds to form quaternary structure of proteins. Different polypeptide chains may be similar or dissimilar types (e.g., haemoglobin). Haemoglobin is made up of two alpha and two beta polypeptide chains. Quaternary structure is most stable structure of proteins.

(b) Some of the proteins act as enzymes and catalyse the biochemical reactions.

## Lipids
Lipids are important constituents of our diet because of their high energy value. Lipids are a group of organic compounds including fats and fat-like substances. For the first time the term "lipid" was used by a German biochemist Bloor in 1943 for a major class of tissue components and foodstuffs.
Lipids are widely distributed throughout the plant and animal kingdom. In plants they occur in seeds, nuts and fruits; and in animals they are stored in adipose tissues, bone marrow and nervous tissues. Many substances like cooking oil, butter, ghee, waxes, natural rubber and cholesterol which we come across in our daily life are either lipids or rich in lipids.
They also serve as an insulating material in sub-cutaneous tissues and around certain organs.
Fats in combination with proteins (lipo-proteins) are important constituents of cell membranes and mitochondria.
In general, lipids comprise a heterogeneous group of compounds which are sparingly soluble in water but show considerable solubility in organic solvents like ether, chloroform, benzene, petroleum, ether.
On hydrolysis they yield fatty acids which are utilised by the living organisms.
Lipids are the compounds of carbon, hydrogen and oxygen. The number of oxygen atoms in a lipid molecule is always less than the number of carbon atoms. That is why they require more oxygen for their oxidation and release more energy in comparison to carbohydrates.
Some lipids also contain small amounts of phosphorus, nitrogen and sulphur.
Fig. Fats and oils (lipids)
A triglyceride is an ester derived from glycerol and three fatty acids (from tri- and glyceride). Triglycerides are the fatty acids, formed by condensation of one molecule of glycerol and 3 molecules of fatty acids.
## Significance of lipids :
(a) Simple lipids act as energy reservoir for animals. They are even richer sources of energy than carbohydrates.
(b) Simple lipids serve as shock absorbers and heat insulators in the subcutaneous tissues and certain organs.
(c) Phospholipids act as structural components of cell membranes.
## Nucleic Acids
Nucleic acids are macromolecules of utmost importance. These are polymeric compounds of nucleotides so called as polynucleotides. Nucleotides are formed of C, H, O, N and P. Each nucleotide is formed of three components i.e., heterocylic nitrogen base, pentose sugar and phosphate group.
On the basis of structure, nitrogen bases are broadly classified into two types:
Pyrimidine : These consist of one pyrimidine ring, composed of two nitrogen and four carbon atoms. E.g., cytosine, thymine (in DNA) and uracil (in RNA).
Purines: These consist of two rings i.e., one pyrimidine ring ( $2 \mathrm{~N}+4 \mathrm{C}$ ) and one imidazole ring ( $2 \mathrm{~N}+3 \mathrm{C}$ ). E.g., adenine and guanine.

## Formation of polynucleotide chain :
A polynucleotide chain is formed by inter linking of large number of nucleotides with $\mathbf{5}^{\prime}-\mathbf{3}^{\prime}$ phosphodiester bonds. These bonds link the $5^{\prime}$-carbon of a pentose of one nucleotide with $3^{\prime}$ - carbon of a pentose of adjoining nucleotide. The bond between hydroxy group of sugar and phosphate molecule is called an ester bond. As their is one such ester bond on either side, it is called phosphodiester bond. So the backbone of a nucleic acid chain is formed of alternatively arranged phosphate and pentose sugar molecular while the nitrogen bases 'stick out' inward from the pentose sugar of the back bone.

## METABOLIC BASIS FOR LIVING
In biochemistry, metabolic pathways are series of chemical reactions occurring within a cell. In each pathway, a principal chemical is modified by a series of chemical reactions. Enzymes catalyze these reactions, and often require dietary minerals, vitamins, and other cofactors in order to function properly. Because of the many chemicals (a.k.a. "metabolites") that may be involved, metabolic pathways can be quite elaborate. In addition, numerous distinct pathways co-exist within a cell. This collection of pathways is called the metabolic network. Pathways are important to the maintenance of homeostasis within an organism. There are two types of pathways catabolic (break-down) and anabolic (synthesis) which often work interdependently to create new biomolecules as the final end-products.
A metabolic pathway involves the step-by-step modification of an initial molecule to form another product. The resulting product can be used in one of three ways :
- To be used immediately, as the end-product of a metabolic pathway
- To initiate another metabolic pathway, called a flux generating step
- To be stored by the cell
Autoradiography is an important technique used to study the synthesis of molecules and to trace the metabolic pathways or events in the cells.
A molecule called a substrate enters a metabolic pathway depending on the needs of the cell and the availability of the substrate. An increase in concentration of anabolic and catabolic intermediates and/or end-products may influence the metabolic rate for that particular pathway.
Each metabolic pathway consists of a series of biochemical reactions that are connected by their intermediates : the products of one reaction are the substrates for subsequent reactions, and so on. Metabolic pathways are often considered as a flow in one direction. Although all chemical reactions are technically reversible, conditions in the cell are often such that it is thermodynamically more favourable for flux to flow in one direction of a reaction. For example, one pathway may be responsible for the synthesis of a particular amino acid, but the breakdown of that amino acid may occur via a separate and distinct pathway. One example of an exception to this "rule" is the metabolism of glucose. Glycolysis results in the breakdown of glucose, but several reactions in the glycolysis pathway are reversible and participate in the re-synthesis of glucose (gluconeogenesis).
Glycolsis was the first metabolic pathway discovered.
As glucose enters a cell, it is immediately phosphorylated by ATP to glucose 6-phosphate in the irreversible first step.
In times of excess lipid or protein energy sources, certain reactions in the glycolysis pathway may run in reverse order to produce glucose 6-phosphate which is then used for storage as glycogen or starch.
- Metabolic pathways are often regulated by feedback inhibition.
- Some metabolic pathways flow in a 'cycle' wherein each component of the cycle is a substrate for the subsequent reaction in the cycle, such as in the Krebs Cycle.
- Anabolic and catabolic pathways in eukaryotes often occur independently of each other, separated either physically by compartmentalisation within organelles or separated biochemically by the requirement of different enzymes and co-factors. Several distinct but linked metabolic pathways are used by cells to transfer the energy released by breakdown of molecules into ATP and other small molecules used for energy (e.g. GTP, NADPH, FADH). These pathways occur within all living organisms in some form :
1. Glycolysis
2. Aerobic respiration and/or Anaerobic respiration
3. Citric acid cycle/Krebs cycle
4. Oxidative phosphorylation

## ENZYMES
Almost all enzymes are proteins. There are some nucleic acids that behave like enzymes. These are called ribozymes. One can depict an enzyme by a line diagram. An enzyme like any protein has a primary structure. i.e., amino acid sequence of the protein. An enzyme like any protein has the secondary and the tertiary structure. When you look at a tertiary structure you will notice that the backbone of the protein chain folds upon itself, the chain criss-crosses itself and hence, many crevices or pockets are made. One such pocket is the 'active site'. An active site of an enzyme is a crevice or pocket into which the substrate fits.
Thus enzymes, through their active site, catalyse reactions at a high rate.
Enzyme catalyst differ from inorganic catalysts in many ways. Inorganic catalysts work efficiently at high temperatures and high pressures, while enzymes get damaged at high temperatures (say above $40^{\circ}$ ). However, enzymes isolated from organisms who normally live under extremely high temperatures (e.g., hot vents and sulphur springs), are stable and retain their catalytic power even at high temperatures (upto $80^{\circ}-90^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ ). Thermal stability is thus an important quality of such enzymes isolated from thermophilic organisms.
## Properties of Enzymes :
(i) All enzymes are proteins; they catalyse the biochemical reactions in living cells and hence are called biocatalysts.
(ii) Each enzyme catalyses only one particular reaction as they are specific to the substrate; this is due to the configuration of the active site on the protein.
(iii) Each enzyme requires a specific (optimum) pH and temperature for their maximum activity.
- The enzymes generally function at a narrow range of temperature and pH .
(iv) Enzymes accelerate a reaction by reducing the activation energy.
(v) When a substrate binds to an enzyme, an enzyme-substrate complex is formed; at this state there occurs a change in the structure of the substrate, which is called transition state structure.
- It is due to the breaking of the bonds of the substrate that reduces activation energy requirements.
(vi) This binding of the substrate induces the enzyme to alter its shape and fit more closely around the substrate.
(vii) The enzyme releases the product(s) of the reaction and the free enzyme is ready to take up another molecule of the substrate.

## COFACTOR
Enzymes are composed of one or several polypeptide chains. However, there are a number of cases in which non-protein constituents called co-factors are bound to the enzyme to make the enzymes catalytically active. In these instances, the protein portion of the enzymes is called the apoenzyme. Three kinds of cofactors may be identified: prosthetic groups, co-enzymes and metal ions.
Prosthetic groups are organic compounds which are tightly bound to the apoenzyme. For example, in peroxidase and catalase, which catalyze the breakdown of hydrogen peroxide to water and oxygen, haem is the prosthetic group and it is a part of the active site of the enzyme.
Co-enzymes are also organic compounds but are loosely associated to the apoenzyme Co-enzymes serve as co-factors in a number of different enzyme catalyzed reactions. The essential chemical components of many coenzymes are vitamins, e.g., coenzyme nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide (NAD) and NADP contain the vitamin niacin.

## SOLUTION
