## Control and Coordination
## Foundation
## CONTROL AND CO-ORDINATION IN PLANTS
Plants do not have a nervous system, muscular system or sense organs as in animals, but they still respond and react to the stimuli.
Stimulus is a kind of change that causes movement in plants. It may be in the form of light, temperature, chemical, gravity, touch etc. Plants respond to these stimuli by showing different kind of movements which occur due to the action of different hormones, present in the plants. These hormones are called plant hormones or phytohormones.
Hence, we can conclude that the process of control and coordination in plants is performed by some chemical substances, known as plant hormones.
## Plant Hormone or Phytohormones
Phytohormones (phyto plant) are chemical substances which are synthesized by the plant in extremely low concentration. They are formed in one tissue or organ of the plant and are then transported to other sites where they regulate the growth, development, reproductive processes, longevity, and even death of the plant. These small molecules are derived from secondary metabolism and are responsible for the adaptation of plants to environmental stimuli. These are of two main types-growth promoters which have a positive effect on a process and thus promote it, whereas the growth inhibitors which have a negative effect and cause inhibition.
## Types of Phytohormones
Phytohormones are of different types named as : auxins, gibberellins, cytokinins, abscisic acid and ethene (ethylene).
## Auxin
Charles Darwin was the first to study phototropism in 1880. Phototropism is the growth of the plant parts such as root and shoot in response to a light stimulus. The existence of auxin in plants is demonstrated by F.W. Went in 1928 by a test named as Avena Curvature Test. He found that more amount of auxin is present on dark side while less amount is present on illuminated side . Due to more amount on dark side, it promotes the growth of that side which resulted in bending of shoot tip towards light. Auxin is found in natural form e.g., IAA (Indole acetic acid) as well as synthetic form e.g., 2,4-dichlorophenoxyacetic acid (2,4-D), 2,4,5-trichlorophenoxy acetic acid (2,4,5-T), naphthalene acetic acid (NAA). IBA (Indole butyric acid) is both natural and synthetic auxin.
## Role of auxins :
(i) Auxin produces apical dominance under which it inhibits the development of lateral buds and let the apical buds grow. This phenomenon is called Apical dominance. It is widely applied in tea plantation and hedge making.
(ii) Auxin stimulates growth by promoting cell elongation.
(iii) Auxin initiates the development of dwarf shoots.
(iv) Application of auxin induces parthenocarpy i.e. formation of fruits without fertilisation. E.g. orange, lemon, grapes, banana etc.
(v) Rooting on stem is promoted by IBA and NAA.
(vi) It initiates uniform flowering in pineapple and litchi plants.
(vii) Unwanted weeds are removed by the application of 2,4-D.

## Gibberellins
The gibberellins were first discovered by Kurosawa in Japan in the course of his studies on a fungal disease of rice known as bakanae disease (foolish seedling) which is caused by fungi Gibberella. Yabuta extracted a chemical substance from this fungi and named as Gibberellin.
## Role of gibberellins :
(i) Gibberellins are the group of growth promoters. The best known naturally occurring gibberellin is Gibberellic Acid.
(ii) Gibberellins induce stem elongation by increasing the length of internodes.
(iii) Gibberellin application suppresses "genetic dwarfism" and causes the plant to grow to the normal height.
(iv) It promotes flowering in long day plants under unfavourable short day conditions.
(v) Gibberellins are much more potent than auxins in inducing parthenocarpy and enhancement of fruit yield in grapes and tomatoes.
(vi) It breaks the seed dormancy of seeds, buds and rhizome.
(vii) It induces seed germination in lettuce and cereals.
## Cytokinin
The first natural cytokinin was identified and crystallized from immature corn grains by Miller Letham and named as zeatin. Miller also isolated a synthetic cytokinin named as kinetin from autoclaved Herring fish sperm DNA.
## Role of cytokinins :
(i) Cytokinins are a group of chemical substances that are responsible for cell-division in plants.
(ii) It promotes growth of laternal buds.
(iii) It also causes cell enlargement in some plants.
(iv) It delays senescence (process of aging in plants) in plants.
(v) Cytokinins inhibit rooting.
(vi) It also induces stomatal opening.
## Abscisic Acid
Addicott obtained a substance from mature cotton fruits and named it as abscisin. Abscisic acid or ABA is also called as stress hormone because it protects the plants from adverse conditions. It also increases tolerance of plants to various type of stresses. ABA is the most widely spread growth inhibitor in the plants.
## Role of abscisic acid :
(i) It is a naturally occurring hormone which inhibits many physiological activities of plants. It accelerates the senescence and abscission (shedding of leaves, fruits and flowers) of leaves.
(ii) It inhibits germination of seeds such as those of lettuce but this can be reversed by kinetins.
(iii) It inhibits gibberellin stimulated growth and called antigibberellin.
(iv) It induces bud and seed dormancy.
(v) It also inhibits cell division and cell enlargement hence also called anticytokinin and antiauxin.
(vi) ABA induces stomatal closing so acts as antitranspirant.
## Ethylene
Ethylene is a gaseous hormone synthesised endogenously in minute quantities by plant tissues.
## Role of ethylene :
(i) It is the chief fruit ripening hormone.
(ii) Ethylene also induces epinasty. It promotes leaf abscission. It induces flowering in pineapples and checks the elongation in stems and roots. It also promotes rooting like auxin.

## MOVEMENTS OF CURVATURE
These are changes in orientation of some plant parts in relation to other parts caused by intrinsic or external stimuli. Curvature movements can be growth movements or turgor movements. They can be autonomic or paratonic. Autonomic movements occur due to intrinsic reason while paratonic movements occur due to external stimuli. Paratonic or induced movements of curvature are of two types, tropic (directional) i.e. dependent on growth and nastic (non-directional) i.e., independent of growth. Nastic movements are also called nasties.
Concept Map : Classification of Plant Movement
## Autonomic Movements
These movements are of three kinds :
(i) Locomotion movements: These type of movements are free and spontaneous movements. These movements occur due to movement of the protoplasm and are limited to aquatic plants. e.g. streaming of cytoplasm, movement of cilia or flagella, motility of zoospores.
Fig. Movement of Cilia in Paramoecium
(ii) Growth and curvature movements: Plants are attached to one place. They can only change the direction of their organs by means of curvature. These movements take place due to the difference in the ratio of growth of different parts. It can be further classified as nutation and nastic movements.
(a) Nutation movements are seen in turners and climbers. This movement results in curvature and helps in turning around the support.
(b) Nastic movements are due to differences in the rate of growth on two opposite sides. e.g. opening of petals and circinately coiled leaves of ferns.
(iii) Variation movements: These movements take place due to changes in the turgidity

of cells. e.g. Desmodium gyrans or the telegraph plant.
The elliptical upward and downward movement of the two lateral leaflets is due to change in the turgidity of cells.
## Tropic Movements
Tropism is a plant growth movement that is determined by an enviromental stimulus. When the stimulus is received by the specific part of the plant, it shows the response in the form of growth movements due to unequal growth on its two sides by the action of phytohormone. These movements are very slow and called tropism movement. If the movement of plant is towards the stimulus it is termed as positive tropism, and if it is away then it is termed as negative tropism.
Depending upon the nature of the stimulus there are various types of tropic movements:
(i) Phototropism
(ii) Geotropism
(iii) Hydrotropism
(iv) Chemotropism
(v) Thigmotropism
## Phototropism (photo-light, tropism-curve)
In phototropism, the stimulus is light. The movement of plant organs in response to unilateral effect of light is known as phototropism. Stems are positively phototropic because they bend towards the sunlight while roots are negatively phototropic because these bend away from light. Leaves are transversely phototropic as they keep their faces at right angles to the direction of light.
Fig. Response of the plant to light
## Perform an experiment to show phototropism.
Take a well watered plant and keep it in a dark chamber into which light enters by means of a tiny opening. Leave the plant in the chamber for a few days and then observe.
It will be seen that the shoot is found bending towards the source of light, showing that shoot is positively phototropic.
## Geotropism (geo-gravity, tropism-curve)
The movement of a plant part in response to gravity is called geotropism. If the plant part moves in the direction of gravity, it is called positive geotropism. On the other hand, if the plant part moves against the direction of gravity, it is negative geotropism (Please note that the force of gravity acts in the downward direction). Now, the roots of a plant move downwards in the direction of gravity, so the plant roots show positive geotropism. On the other hand, the stem (or shoot) of a plant moves upwards against the direction of gravity, so the stem (or shoot) of a plant shows negative geotropism.
Fig. Plant showing geotropism
Perform an experiment to show geotropism.
Take a potted plant and rest it on its side so that the stem lies horizontally. Take another plant of about the same size and fix it on a Clinostat (a special apparatus which slowly rotates the plant) as in setup B. Leave the two setups undisturbed for a few days.
After few days, one observes the shoot growing upwards after showing a curvature as seen in setup $A$. In $B$ setup, the shoot continues to grow horizontally without any curvature.
This shows the effect of gravity. The stem is negatively geotropic so it moves away from gravity in (A) while the effect of gravity is neutralised in (B) by the rotating Clinostat hence, the stem stays straight.
## Hydrotropism (hydro-water, tropism-curve)
The movement of an organ of plant in response to the stimulus of water is known as hydrotropism. Roots are positively hydrotropic. Positive hydrotropic response of roots is stronger than their geotropic response. The roots of plants always go towards water, even if it means going against the pull of gravity. Though roots normally grow downwards but in order to reach water, they can grow sideways or even upwards. The roots grow in the direction of source of water so as to obtain water for the developing plant. Since roots always grow (or move) towards water, therefore, roots are positively hydrotropic.
Perform an experiment to understand the response of a plant to water (hydrotropism).
We take two glass troughs $A$ and $B$ and fill each one of them (two-third) with soil. In trough $A$, we plant a tiny seedling (Fig. a) and in trough B, we plant a similar seedling and also place a small 'clay pot' inside the soil (Fig. b). Water the soil in trough $A$ daily and uniformly. Do not water the soil in trough $B$, but put some water in the clay pot buried in the soil. Leave both the troughs for a few days.
Now, dig up the seedlings carefully without damaging their roots. We will find that the root of seedling in trough $A$ is straight. On the other hand, the root of seedling in trough $B$ is found to bend to the right side (towards the clay pot containing water) [see Fig (b)]. This can be explained as follows:
In trough A, the root of seedling gets water from both sides (because the soil is watered uniformly). But in trough B, the root gets water oozing out from the clay pot which is kept on the right side. So, the root of seedling in trough $\boldsymbol{B}$ grows and bends towards the source of water to the right side. This experiment shows that the root of a plant grows towards water. In other words, the roots of a plant are positively hydrotropic.
## Chemotropism (chemo-chemical, tropism-curve)
Chemotropism is a directional growth that occurs in response to a chemical stimulus. It is best seen in the growth of pollen tube inside style, ovary and ovule. Here every region produces its own nutrients and chemotactic chemicals for growth and passage of pollen tube. Chemotropism can be observed by germinating a number of pollen grains in a minimal medium. A drop of nutrient medium containing boron will result in bending of pollen tubes towards it from other directions.
Fig. Chemotropism in pollen tubes
## Thigmotropism (thigmo-touch, tropism-curve)
It is directional growth movement of curvature which occurs in response to touch or contact. It is also called stereotropism. Thigmotropism is found in twiners and tendrils. Tendrils are thin, thread like growths on the stems or leaves of climbing plant. These are sensitive to the touch (or contact) of other objects. After initial contact with support due to nutation, the tendril or twiner shows less growth in the region of contact and more growth on the other side. As a result, they bend towards the support. Later on bending or coiling may occur in lower parts of the tendril as well.

## - What is the necessity of tropic movements?
## SOLUTION
The various types of tropic movements help the plants to survive. For example, even if a seed is planted upside down, its root will still grow downwards into earth because it is positively geotropic. The root will also grow towards water because it is positively hydrotropic. Similarly, the shoot of such a seed will grow upwards because it is negatively geotropic, and towards light as it is positively phototropic.
## Nastic Movements
The movement of a plant in response to an external stimuli where the direction of response is not determined by the direction of stimulus, is called Nastic Movement.
The nastic movements of plants are induced by stimuli such as heat, light, humidity, touch (or contact) etc. The main difference between tropic and nastic movements is that tropic movement is a directional movement of plant parts, but nastic movement is not a directional movement of plant parts with respect to the stimulus. In nastic movement, what ever the direction of the stimulus can be, it affects all parts of a plant equally, and they always move in the same direction.

## Sensitive Plant
Mimosa pudica (also called Action Plant, Humble Plant, Live and Die, Mori-Vivi, Shame Plant, Touch-Me-Not) is a very senstitive plant.

Sensitive plant is so named because it shows a rapid motion response to various stimuli; for instance, the leaves close when lightly touched. Greater disturbance causes the petioles to droop as though wilted and ready to fall from the plant. If left undisturbed for about ten minutes, the leaves rise and open again. The leaves also fold and droop at night and when exposed to cold. If the plant is anesthetized with ether or chloroform, it becomes unresponsive to touch. Sensitive plant is so sensitive that it may shed leaves after handling and shipping, but it recovers quickly and is easy to grow. However, because the plant responds so drastically to changes, it must be treated very consistently, with no extreme or sudden changes in environmental factors such as light, temperature, humidity, and soil moisture.
Sensitive last will need plenty of water. The soil should be evenly moist but not constantly wet. This plant has delicate, hairlike, fibrous roots. The roots are subject to rot if kept too wet, and to burning and shriveling if too dry-especially in hot weather. Mimosa pudica is a very sensitive plant.
The opening and closing of the plant's leaflets and the entire leaf are controlled by a fluid filled sac-like structures found at the base of the compound leaf and each leaflet. The swollen base of the leaf stalk is called ˋpulvinus'. When the plant is touched, electrical signals are flashed by the cells. The cells in the 'pulvinus' respond to this signal by flushing out potassium and water. With the massive loss of water, the pulvinus bends over and the leaflets fold. The responses of the plant were first observed by Indian scientist Jagadish Chandra Bose to prove that plants also feel pain.

## CONTROL AND COORDINATION IN ANIMALS
Animals have the ability of locomotion, hence they are different from plants.
Also they are more complex than plants. They have so many specialised organs and organ systems which perform specialised functions for their control and coordination. This ability has developed as they have to search for food, unlike plants which are autotrophic. As the animals have to move from one place to another, they have to continuously encounter changes in the environment. This occurs due to the presence of two systems in the animal body i.e., Nervous System (nervous coordination) and the Endocrine System (chemical coordination). Both these systems act in a coordinated manner in animals, so as to regulate the various body activities. Both these systems are performed by the help of different sense organs, receptors and stimulus.

## Human Nervous System
Nervous system is the master controlling system of the body for all our actions (thinking and behaviour). This system is concerned with receiving stimuli from the external or internal environment of the body, interpreting the stimuli and producing the appropriate response to these stimuli. To achieve this, highly specialised cells are required which can receive the messages and conduct them to the chief centres of the nervous system where they are to be interpreted and then returned to the relevant parts of the body. Nervous systems are found in most multicellular animals, but vary greatly in complexity. Sponges have no nervous system.
## Neuron (A Unit of Nervous System)
The neuron or the nerve cell is the structural and functional unit of nervous system. It is the largest cell in the body which looks like an electric wire.
The neuron is a special cell which can receive and conduct impulses. Its size and shape vary, depending on its location. Neurons are the largest cells present in the human body and can be as long as 100 cm .
Nerve cell consists of three parts : (i) Cell body (ii) Dendrites (iii) Axon
Cell body: It is also called the cyton. Its shape may vary-round, pyriform or stellate. It is rich in cytoplasm which is called as neuroplasm. It has a large and spherical nucleus. Besides other cell organelles cyton has special granules called Nissl's granules. Cyton is concerned with metabolism and growth. It receives impulses from dendrites and transmits them to axon.
Dendrite: These are short and branched protoplasmic processes stretching out from the cell body of a neuron. They contain Nissl's granules and neurofibrils. Dendrites receive the sensation and pass it to the cyton.
Axon: This is a single, long and cylindrical protoplasmic process of uniform diameter arising from the cyton. At its terminal end, axon is highly branched. Axon terminals are knob-like which end in nerve fibres or glands or form synapses with dendrites of other neurons. The axon conducts impulses away from the cyton. ## Functioning of Nervous System
A number of nerve fibres are wrapped together to form nerves. The nerves lie end to end in a chain so that the dendrites receive impulse from an axon or receptor that passes it to the next neuron in the chain via its cyton and axon. The junction between the axon and dendron of the next neuron is called synapse. It is the point of contact between the terminal branches of the axon of a neuron with dendrites of another neuron. The neurons are not connected. There is a minute gap called synaptic cleft. Within the axons, synaptic vesicles are present, filled with chemical (acetylcholine) called neurotransmitters. Transmission of nerve impulse is a chemical process. The impulse that reaches the ends of the axon fibres causes the synaptic vesicles to release the neurotransmitters (acetylcholine) into the synaptic cleft. The neurotransmitters reach the dendrites of the next neuron. This sets up a new impulse which is transmitted to the axon. The speed of transmission is about $120 \mathrm{~m} / \mathrm{s}$. The chemical is soon broken down by an enzyme to make the synapse ready for the next transmission.

Impulse is an electrical disturbance received by the dendrite and passed through the cyton to the axon. Messages are transmitted in the form of electrochemical impulses along the fibres of the neurons. It flows only unidirectionally.

## Brain
This is the highest coordinating centre in the body. It is situated in the head region within the cranial cavity of the skull. It is soft, whitish organ which weighs $1.2-1.4 \mathrm{~kg}$. It forms $98 \%$ of the weight of the whole CNS. Brain is surrounded by three protective membranes, named as piamater (innermost highly vascular layer), arachnoid mater (delicate middle layer), and duramater (outermost layer), called meninges which contain blood vessels. The space between these meninges is filled with cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) which protects the brain from mechanical shocks. The nerves arise from brain known as cranial nerves. Brain is divisible into three main regions:
(i) Fore brain
(ii) Mid brain
(iii) Hind brain
Fore brain
It forms the greatest part of the brain. It consists of three regions: Olfactory lobes, Cerebrum and Diencephalon
(A) Olfactory lobes: These are a pair of club-shaped small structures, present below the cerebral hemisphere. Both lobes are widely separated. Each lobe consists of an olfactory bulb and a narrow olfactory stalk. It is the centre of smell.
(B) Cerebral hemispheres or cerebrum : It forms the largest part of the brain. Cerebrum has two cerebral hemispheres which lie side by side and are separated by a deep cerebral fissure or corpus callosum. The surface of cerebral hemisphere has grooves (sulci) and folds (gyri) to accomodate larger number of nerve cells.

Cerebral Cortex is the outer layer of cerebrum. It is made up of grey matter and white matter. Grey matter, which has a pinkish-grey color in the living brain, contains the cell bodies, dendrites and axon terminals of neurons, so it is where all synapses are located. White matter is made of axons connecting different parts of grey matter.
Each cerebral hemisphere is divided into four lobes :
(a) Frontal lobe is present on the anterior side. This is the region for judging, planning, reasoning. It is also involved in facial muscular activities or expression of emotion.
(b) Temporal lobe is present in the lateral region. This is the region for hearing and is also associated with speech, vision, memory, sensory input, and language.
(c) Occipital lobe is present in the posterior region. This is the visual processing area of the brain. It is associated with colour determination, object and face recognition.
(d) Parietal lobe is present in the middle region. This is the region for touch, taste, temperature and conscious association.

(C) Diencephalon : It is the smallest and unpaired part of brain which connects the forebrain to the mid brain. It lies on the lower side of cerebrum. It is divided into three parts named as thalamus (present on the sides), hypothalamus (floor) and epithalamus (roof). Thalamus is critically involved in relaying sensory and motor signals to the cerebral cortex and regulating consciousness, sleep, and alertness. ## Mid brain
It extends from the pons varoli to the lower portion of the diencephalon. Mid brain is sub divided into optic lobes and cerebral peduncle.
(A) Optic lobes (tectum) : There are four round solid optic lobes called corpora quadrigemina. Two of them are called superior colliculi and other two at the back called inferior colliculi. The superior colliculi are centres for sight reflexes and the inferior colliculi are centres for hearing reflexes.
(B) Cerebral Peduncles : These are the paired structures located at the ventral side of the midbrain which connects hind brain to mid brain. They contain the large ascending (sensory) and descending (motor) nerve tracts that run to and from the cerebrum from the pons varoli, a part of hind brain.
## Hind brain
It consists of three parts : Cerebellum, Pons varoli and Medulle oblongata.
(A) Cerebellum: It is the second largest part of the brain. It is made up of two large lateral cerebellar hemispheres and a central vernis. Like cerebrum of fore brain, the cerebellum consists of grey matter and white matter. Grey matter - located on the surface of the cerebellum and is tightly folded, forming the cerebellar cortex. White matter - located underneath the cerebellar cortex that contains a number of nerve fibres or axons. It regulates voluntary actions such as posture and muscular movements. It also maintain equilibrum of the body.
(B) Pons varoli : It is located in the centre of brain below the cerebellum. It carries impulses from one hemisphere of cerebellum to other and co-ordinates muscular movements of body. It controls the respiratory function.
(C) Medulla oblongata : It is the posterior most part of the brain which lies below the cerebellum. It connects the spinal cord with the brain which continues posteriorly into the spinal cord. It contains a fluid-filled cavity called fourth ventricle. It is the centre of involuntary actions such as, heart beat, vomiting, salivation, sneezing, coughing, swallowing etc.
## Spinal Cord
It lies in the mid-dorsal region along the longitudinal axis of the body. It is a slender, cylindrical structure, about 45 cm long, originating from medulla oblongata and extending downwards upto the lumber region. ## Peripheral Nervous System
All the nerves of the body make up the peripheral nervous system. They all enter or leave the central nervous system. They are grouped into two types: Somatic and Autonomic Nervous System.
## Somatic PNS
It is under the control of the will i.e., it regulates voluntary movements of the body. The nerves arise directly from CNS and connect different body parts for voluntary control of the brain. It is classified into two categories, cranial nerves and spinal nerves.
(a) Cranial Nerves arise from the brain except olfactory, optic and auditory nerves and spread to various parts of the head.
They are $\mathbf{1 2}$ pairs in number.
Cranial Nerves I, II and VIII are sensory nerves.
Cranial Nerves III, IV, VI, XI and XII are motor nerves.
Cranial Nerves V, VII, IX and X are mixed (sensory and motor) nerves.
(b) Spinal Nerves, arise from the spinal cord along its length and spread throughout the body except the head. They are mixed nerves i.e., both sensory and motor. In humans, there are $\mathbf{3 1}$ pairs i.e., 8 pairs cervical nerves, 12 pairs thoracic nerves, 5 pairs lumbar nerves, 5 pairs sacral nerves, and 1 pair coccygeal nerves.
## Involuntary PNS or Autonomic Nervous System (ANS)
It is not under the control of human will. Two antagonistic (opposite) sets of nerves constitue ANS. It consists of visceral nerves. Visceral Nerves mostly arise from spinal cord and a few from the brain. They regulate the activities of internal organs such as heart, lungs, urinary bladder, blood vessels, etc. besides regulating normal functions of the body. They form the autonomic nervous system. ANS is subdivided into sympathetic and parasympathetic nervous system.

## Reflex Action
This can be defined as an involuntary functioning or sudden action of any organ or body part in response to a particular stimulus. The function or action occurs immediately, without the involvement of the will or consciousness.
A familiar example of reflex action in man is the withdrawal of hand after touching with hot object. It is common that if someone suddenly comes in contact with hot object, one immediately retracts. In such actions, the impulses are carried by the sensory nerves from the receptor organ to the central nervous system and back to the effector organ. The path or the route through which the impulses travel from the receptor organ, via CNS, is called a reflex arc and simple reflex arc includes :
The reflex arcs are of two types :
(i) Somatic reflex arc involves effector organs located in body (soma) structures e.g. skeletal muscles.
(ii) Visceral reflex arc involves effector organs located in the visceral organs e.g. gonads.
Fig. A reflex arc
A simple reflex arc consists of only one sensory neuron and one motor neuron. But, in majority of reflexes, a sensory neuron influences several motor neurons through intermediate neurons of the spinal cord. This forms a complex reflex arc.
Some common examples of reflex actions are knee jerk, blinking of eye when aimed at, withdrawl of hand on pin prick, sneezing, and secretion of saliva at the sight or smell of food.
## Significance of reflex action :
(i) It checks overloading and overtaxing of brain.
(ii) It results in quick response to a harmful stimuli without the processing done by the coordinating centres of CNS.
(iii) Tells about what abnormalities the child may be having if all reflexes are not proper.
(iv) Involuntary actions designed at increasing chances of survival.
## Endocrine System in Human Beings
In human beings, the message is communicated in the form of nerve impulse from receptor to CNS and from latter to effector. This communication is very quick but has certain limitations such as the nerve impulse can reach only those cells which are connected by nerves, and the nerve cells cannot continuously generate and transmit impulses. This is the reason why multicellular organisms have another means of communication called chemical communication. The stimulated cells release a chemical into the blood stream, the other body cells detect this chemical and the effect is produced. This is a slower means of communication as compared to the nervous system but it reaches all the cells of the body. The chemical carrying the message is called hormone and the group of specialised cells which secrete these hormones are called endocrine glands. These are ductless glands i.e. the hormones are secreted directly into the blood stream and transported to others parts of the body and to the target organ, where they are needed.
Hormones have the following characteristics:
- They may be proteinaceous or non-proteinaceous (amino acids or steroids)
- They are secreted as per need and not stored.
- Their secretion may be regulated by nerves or by feedback mechanism.
- They are transported by blood.
- They cause long term effects like growth, change in behaviour, etc.
- They do not catalyze any reactions.
- They function by stimulating or inhibiting the target organs.
There are some basic differences between the two systems, still they operate in a coordinated manner. Many important functions of the endocrine system are under the control of nervous system. Hence, the two systems are collectively termed as Neuroendocrine system.

## Hypothalamus
Fig. Human endocrine system
Hypothalamus is a part of forebrain and appeared as the floor of diencephalon. It is the main link between endocrine and nervous system. Hypothalamus produces two types of hormones i.e., releasing hormones, stimulate secretion of pituitary hormones and inhibitory hormones, inhibit secretions of pituitary hormones. Therefore, it controls pituitary gland.
## Pituitary gland
It is a pea shaped gland that is located below the hypothalamus in the brain. It is under the control of hypothalamus and controls many functions in the body. Therefore, it is referred to as "Master gland" of the body. It is made up of three lobes-anterior, middle and posterior. They secrete hormones in response to the hypothalamus. One of the hormones secreted by pituitary gland is growth hormone. The growth hormone controls the growth of human body. For example, growth hormone controls the development of bones and muscles. A person having a deficiency of growth hormone in childhood remains very short and becomes a dwarf (Dwarfism), and a person having too much growth hormone becomes tall (Gigantism).
## Hormones of anterior pituitary
- Growth hormone or Somatotropic hormone (GH/STH) - Stimulates the growth of essentially all tissues of the body, including bone.
- Thyroid stimulating hormone (TSH) or thyrotropic hormone - Stimulates synthesis and secretion of thyroid hormones by thyroid gland.
- Follicle stimulating hormone (FSH) - In females, initiate development of oocytes. In males, stimulates testes to produce sperm.
- Leuteinising hormone (LH) - Control the function of ovaries in females and testes in males, which are known as the gonads.
- Prolactin hormone - Stimulates development of mammary gland during pregnancy.
- Adrenocorticotropic hormone (ACTH) - Stimulates secretion of cortisol by adrenal cortex.
- Melanocyte stimulating hormone - Stimulates synthesis of melanin pigment present in the skin.
## Hormones of posterior pituitary
- Oxytocin (OT) - Stimulates contraction of uterus muscle during child birth and lactation.
- Antidiuretic hormone (ADH) or vasopressin - Conserve body water by decreasing urine volume.
## Pineal gland
It is a small round gland in the brain. It secretes melatonin that regulates the circadian rhythm and sexual cycle. It is well developed in children.
## Thyroid gland
It is a butterfly-shaped, bilobed gland that is situated at the base of the larynx. The two lobes are joined by an isthmus. It secrets thyroxine hormone which controls body metabolism and calcitonin which regulates calcium concentratio in blood. At the back of the thyroid, two pairs of Parathyroid gland are present. They secrete parathormone which is important in calcium and phosphorus metabolism. Both calcitonin and parathormone work together to maintain the calcium levels in blood.
Hyper and hyposecretion of thyroid hormone results in many disorders, one of them is goitre. It is the enlargement of thyroid gland caused due to deficiency of dietary iodine. Swollen neck is one of the symptoms of this disease. This disease can be prevented by adding iodide to the table salt.
## Thymus
It is a gland located between lungs behind sternum on the ventral side of aorta. It gradually decreases in size in adults. It secretes hormone called thymosin. Thymosine helps in the production of lymphocytes and antibodies formation, and hence helps in immunity.
## Adrenal glands
These glands are also known as "glands of emergency". They appear cap-like on the top of each kidney. Each adrenal gland has two layers-outer cortex and inner medulla. Adrenal cortex secretes hormones like glucosteroids and cortisones. They increase the blood sugar level in times of stress by converting protein to glucose. Some cortical cells secrete sex hormones that can cause premature sexual maturity, femine traits in male and masculine traits in female. The adrenal medulla secretes two hormones-adrenaline and noradrenaline. Adrenaline is an important hormone as it causes 'fight or flight response' i.e., it prepares the body to react in emergency situation by various means like increase in glucose levels for more energy, selective dilation and constriction of blood vessels, etc. Noradrenaline performs similar functions, but has more of an effect on blood vessels.
## Pancreas
It is a heterocine gland present at the junction of stomach and duodenum. A part secretes digestive juices which pour its secretion into a duct. The endocrine portion i.e. the islet of langerhans has three types of cells: (i) alpha cells, produce glucagon that increases the conversion of glycogen into glucose, thus increasing the level of glucose in blood; (ii) beta cells, produce insulin that increase the conversion of glucose into glycogen, thus decreasing the level of glucose in blood, (iii) delta cells, secrete somatostatin that inhibits the secretion of insulin and glucagon.
Deficiency of insulin hormone in the body causes a disease known as diabetes mellitus. In this disease, the patient excretes sugar (glucose) in urine, feels excessive thirst and also does excessive urination. This disease is caused due to hyposecretion of insulin hormone.
## Gonads
Gonads are reproductive organs. The male gonads are the testes and the female gonads are the ovaries. Both produce hormones under the influence of the hormones released from pituitary glands. Testes produce testosterone which produces the secondary sexual characteristics like moustache and beard in males. Ovary secretes oestrogen from the mature follicles that produces the secondary sexual characters like breasts development, and menstruation. Post ovulation, another hormone progesterone is produced which maintains pregnancy.

## Homeostasis
Homeostasis is keeping the organism in harmony with external environment along with internal chemical environment of the body. Excess or deficiency of hormones has a harmful effect on our body. This means that there should be some 'mechanism' to regulate the production and release of hormones in the body. This is controlled by a feed back mechanism i.e., if the sugar level rises too much this is detected by pancreas which responds by secretion of more insulin into blood, and if the blood-sugar falls below a certain level, the secretion of insulin is reduced automatically. In this manner, insulin maintains blood-glucose homeostasis.

This happens as follows:
(i) When sunlight falls straight on the plant, the auxin hormone synthesised at the tip of the stem spreads uniformly down the stem and due to equal concentration of auxin, stem grows straight.
(ii) When sunlight falls on only one side of the plant, the auxin diffuses towards the shady side of shoot.
The concentration of auxin stimulates the cells to grow longer. Therefore, the stem appears to bend towards the source of light.

Dendrites of a neuron collect information from the receptors and pass it to the cell body in the form of electrical impulse. From the cell body, impulse passes along the axon to its end. At end of the axon, the electrical impulse results in the release of certain chemicals (neurotransmitters). These chemicals cross the gap (synapse) between the endings of the axon and dendrites of the next neuron and start a similar electric impulse in the dendrites. Thus, information received and travels along the neurons of a nerve to the effector muscles or gland.
