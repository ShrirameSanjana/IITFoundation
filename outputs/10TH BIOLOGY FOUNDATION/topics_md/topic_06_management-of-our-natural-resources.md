## NATURAL RESOURCES
Natural resources are obtained from earth and its environment, and are also know as earth resources. Resources can be natural, human or human-made.
On the basis of their distribution, natural resources can be classified into:
(i) Inexhaustible resources are those which are present in unlimited quantity in nature and are not likely to be exhausted by human activity. For example, sunlight, air, rain etc.
(ii) Exhaustible resources are those which are limited and can be exhausted by human activity in the long run. For example, petroleum, natural gas etc.
(a) Renewable resources are those that can be replenished or reproduced easily. Some of them like wind, air, sunlight, etc. are continuously available and their quantity is not affected by man's consumption. Some renewable resources can be depleted by human use, but is also replenished, thus maintaining a flow. Agricultural crops take a short time for renewal; water takes a comparatively longer time, while forest takes even longer time.
(b) Non-renewable resources are those which are formed over very long geological periods. Minerals and fossil fuels are included in this category. As the rate of formation of fossil fuels is very slow, they can't be replenished once they get depleted.

## Natural Resources can also be classified in three other ways:
1. On the basis of origin:
(i) Biotic resources are those which are obtained from the biosphere such as forests, animals, plants and their products. Mineral fuels such as coal and petroleum are also included in this category because, they are formed from decayed organic matter.
(ii) Abiotic resources include non-living things like land, water, air and mineral ores.
2. On the basis of their stage of development:
(i) Potential resources are those that exist in a region and may be used in the future. For example, petroleum exists in many parts of the country, but it remains a potential resource till the time it is drilled out and put to use.
(ii) Actual resources are those that have been surveyed, their quantity and quality determined and are being used in present times e.g. wood.
3. On the basis of their distribution:
(i) Natural resources are confined to national boundries e.g. minerals, forests, etc.
(ii) Multinational resources are shared by more than one nation e.g. certain rivers, migratory birds, etc.
(iii) International resources are shared uniformly by all nations e.g. sunlight, air, etc.
Why do we need to manage our resources?
Natural resources especially the non-renewable ones are depleting day by day. If proper attention is not given to this, the day is not far when we will be left with no more coal and oil.
- Natural resources like land, forest and water provide livelihood to millions.
- Natural resources help in raising food crops and other natural food items
- Export of minerals helps in economic growth of the country.
- Natural resources like forests help to prevent soil erosion.
- Developing countries depend a lot on the natural resources for their economic development.
As the population grows, the demands for all these resources also increase, but it is upto us to utilise it wisely and not waste it or misuse it.
Renewable resources such as water, air, plants, etc are considered to be plenty and one might even think that it will not perish. But the fact is that they might not perish away but will definitely become scarce. The capacity at which these items renew themselves is at a much slower pace than the demand. For example, we might think that there is plenty of water and we might never go short of it, but the truth is that there is only $3 \%$ of fresh water on earth for human need and the rest $97 \%$ is saline water. If water is not utilised properly there will be scarcity of water in the near future.
These natural resources should be consumed to protect our environment. The management of natural resources is called as Natural Resource Management. It is to improve the quality of life of the present and the future generations.
Mother earth rich with its natural resources should be managed and preserved without which the existence of all life forms would be impossible. Healthy atmosphere would keep us healthy and increase our life span.

## SAVE NATURE
As we read in chapter V, there are three R's of waste management: Reduce, reuse, and recycle. Practicing all three of these activities every day is important for a healthy environment.
3 R's
## Reduce
Reduce/Reduction: to make something smaller or use less, resulting in a smaller amount of waste. "Source reduction" is reducing waste before you purchase it, or by purchasing products that are not wasteful in their packaging or use. A key part of waste "reduction" is "conservation" - using natural resources wisely, and using less than usual in order to avoid waste.
## Tips to practice reduction:
1. Avoid single-use containers whenever possible.
2. Purchase less products and in materials/packaging that can be readily recycled.
3. Buy concentrates rather than diluted products- the result is less waste for disposal when it is empty.
4. Refuse store bags.
5. Use durable items rather than disposable items whenever possible.
## Reuse
Reuse materials in their original form instead of throwing them away, or pass those materials to others who could use them too.
## Tips for reuse :
Carry washable cups or travel mugs instead of disposables; a lot of restaurants and convenient stores will be glad to fill or refill your own mug. When you do use disposables like plastic cups, plates, utensils, and plastic food storage bags; don't throw them away.
Bicycles or other durable goods like washers, dryers, etc. are being repaired rather than being replaced when they break. This is another form of "reuse". New is not always better, nor it is always necessary. When you do decide to replace something large and "reusable", be sure to donate the old one to charitable outlets. Use cloth gift bags and stop ripping the paper off gifts. If you remove the wrapping paper carefully, you can use it again, and there's nothing wrong with doing just that. And don't forget to use canvas or cloth bags when shopping so you don't need to make the choice between "paper or plastic." Use washable table napkins instead of paper napkins. Cloth napkins are usually much larger and they absorb more than paper products.
## Recycle
Recycling occurs when you save and take reusable materials to places where they can be remade into either the same product or new products, instead of throwing them.
Making new items from recycled ones also takes fewer energy and other resources than making products from brand new materials. Buy toilet paper, paper towels and stationery made from recycled paper, tile from crushed light bulbs, garden hoses from used tires and yard furniture from recycled plastic bottles.

## FORESTS AND WILDLIFE
The biotic component of our environment on land i.e. plants and animals are found in forests. Forests form a buffer for the earth to protect life forms. It comprises an area of diverse and complex ecosystems consisting mainly of trees, shrubs and wild plants which house an extraordinary variety of communities of living organisms. For example, the Indian forest includes a wide variety of plants and animals. India has over 45,000 plant species, 800 species of mammals, 2000 species of birds, 420 species of reptiles, 2000 species of fish, 5000 species of insects and 4000 species of molluscs etc. Due to the wide range of life forms and genetic diversity of life forms, a good forest is therefore called the 'biodiversity hot spots'.

## Role of Forest
Forests play a vital role in boosting the welfare of people and our environment. They produce many valuable things besides providing ecological balance of the land area. Some important products of the forests are :
1. Wood is needed as timber used for making furniture and houses, wooden crates for packing, paper, sports goods, boats, railway sleepers, as fire wood in developing countries, etc.
2. Food and spices like dry fruits (almond, walnut, cashew, etc.) and spices (cloves, cinnamon, cardamon, etc.).
3. Tannins, Gums, Resins and Dyes. Tannins are used in making ink and leather polish, glues are used in making adhesives, resins are used in paints, dyes like hematoxylin are used in staining tissues in anatomy studies.
4. Drugs e.g. quinine, aconite, belladona, etc.
5. Other products like rubber, cork, honey, camphor, etc.
## Stakeholders
Modern approaches in forest conservation have found that enforcement of forest laws or policies which do not recognise the legitimate needs of different groups that depend on the forest, often fail in their objectives when we consider the conservation of forests. We need to look at different stakeholders whose dependency on forest resources vary.
1. Local communities that stay next to or in the forests Tribals staying within the forest areas and villages bordering them need the five basic ' $\mathrm{F}_{\mathrm{s}}$ ' i.e., Food, Fodder, Fibre, Fuel and Fertilizer for surviving. This is obtained from the forest.
2. The forest department of the Government which owns the land undertakes protection and controls the resources from forests. They earn revenue from the sale of timber to construction and furniture markets. They also earn revenue from commercial mono-plantations grown from trees like popular, eucalyptus, teak, bamboo etc. to access the specific products for industries such as paper, plyboard, etc.
3. Industries that use various forest produce, but are not dependent on the forest produce in any one area. These include timber, paper, resin and gum, lac, natural oils, minor food products, medicines, sports equipment industries. They are neither interested in loss of valuable biodiversity nor in the sustainability of forests in particular.
4. Environment Conservationists and Nature Enthusiasts who are not in any way dependent on the forests. NGOs like the World Wide Fund for Nature (WWF), Centre for Science and Environment (CSE), International Union for Conservation of Nature and Natural Resources (IUCN) often exercise a considerable say in policy and management of forests. Historically, conservationists took up causes of protecting large animals like tigers (Project Tiger), lions, elephants and rhinoceros. They now recognise the need to preserve biodiversity as a whole, regulate industrial over-exploitation of forest resources and involve local people in the conservation of forests. Along with the Ministry of Environment and Forests, they have designated protected forest areas, wildlife sanctuaries, biodiversity hotspots for special attention and management.

## Conservation of Forest
Conservation is protection, augmentation and scientific mangement of resources so as to maintain them at their optimum level provide benefit to the present as well as future generations. Forests must be conserved because of their immense economic, protective and regulative properties. It is carried out by the following methods.
1. Afforestation. Growing of forests on uprotected barren lands. Van Mahotsava is a tree plantation movement carried out twice a year (February and July) by both government and voluntary agencies.
2. Reforestation. Developing forest cover in the area which has been damaged or cleared during exploitation.
3. Separation of Commercial Forestry. Useful plants required by industry should be planted separately preferably on waste land. Growing industry required plants is called production plantation.
4. Grazing. Grazing should be regulated according to the availability of pasturage.
5. Sustained Yield Block Cutting. A forest is divided into a number of blocks depending upon the period required by forest trees to mature. In one year, trees of one block are felled. The block is reforested immediately. In this way, annual deforestation is compensated by annual reforestation. The forest is conserved indefinitely and provides sustained yield.
6. Social Forestry. It is growing of useful multipurpose trees and shrubs on common village lands, roadsides, railsides, etc, by cooperative efforts of people of the area. The plants provide fodder, small timber and fire wood to villagers.
7. Agroforestry. Multipurpose trees and shrubs are planted on the sides of crop fields to provide fodder, fuel and timber.
NOTE: Both social forestry and agroforestry reduce pressure on the forests. Trees are also planted at all vacant places and along roads in urban areas (urban forestry) for reducing air and noise pllution.

## Red Data Book
The International Union for the Conservation of Nature and Natural Resources (IUCN) maintains an international list, published as the Red Data Book. The aim of the list is to impart information to the public and policy makers about the urgency of conservation problems. The list recognises 8 Red list categories of species.
(i) Data deficient (DD) - appropriate data on abundance and distribution of the species is unavailable.
(ii) Not evaluated (NE) - species not evaluated against the criteria of IUCN
(iii) Low risk/Least concern (LC) - species is characterized by IUCH but not being a focus of species conservation.
(iv) Vulnerable (VU) - species susceptible to become endangered unless the circumstances affecting its reproduction and survival improve.
(v) Endangered (EN) - species is at risk of becoming extinct, as it is few in number and threatened by various parameters like predation, deforestation etc.
(vi) Critically Endangered (CR) - species that face a high risk of extinction in the wild.
(vii) Extinct in the wild (EW) - species that are not known to have any living specimens in the wild and are only zoos or artificial environments.
(viii) Extinct (EX) - when the last existing member of the species dies.
The 2000 Red list contains assessment of more than 18,000 species, 11,000 of which are threatened.

## Biodiversity Conservation
Earth's rich biodiversity is vital for the very survival of mankind. Biodiversity conservation is mainly aimed at protection, enhancement and scientific management of biodiversity.
There are two basic strategies of biodiversity conservation -
(i) In-situ conservation: The conservation of plants/animals in their natural habitats. National parks, wildlife sanctuaries and biosphere reserves are the examples of protected areas.
(ii) Ex-situ conservation: The conservation of plants/animals outside their natural habitats. It includes offsite collections (botanical gardens, zoos, arboreta etc.) and gene banks (preserves genetic material like freezing cuttings from plants or seeds, freezing of sperm and eggs etc.).

## WATER FOR ALL
Water is an essential component of all forms of life. After air, it is also the most abundant substance present in the earth's biosphere. Oceans, seas, and other saline inland water bodies make $97.3 \%$ of the total global supply of water which is not available for human consumption due to high salt content. Fresh water accounts for only $2.7 \%$ of the total estimated global water supply. Of this percentage, $75 \%$ of the fresh water is frozen in polar ice caps and glaciers. Some fresh water is also present underground. The fraction of fresh water available for human beings is less than $0.003 \%$ of the total global water available.
There are three main sources of fresh water available. The first is rainwater resources of India. India receives an overall rainfall of about 105 to 117 cm annually resulting in 3 million $\mathrm{m}^{3}$ of water. Most of this water is collected in few months during monsoon. The second source is surface water, there are 14 major river systems which include Ganga, Brahmaputra, Godavari, Krishna, Kaveri, etc. Other surface water sources include ponds, lakes and streams. The third source is ground water which is the water that percolates down the surface soil into pore spaces of rocks. The total volume of ground water found in the aquifer is estimated to be $42.3 \times 10^{10} \mathrm{~m}^{3}$. Ground water provides soil moisture for plants and supplements for streams and lakes. Water table indicates the level at which ground water is found. The agricultural sector and urban sectors use this water for various proposes which has resulted a decline in its level.
Study the rainfall pattern in India and identify the regions where water is abudant and where it is scarce.
Rainfall pattern in India is -
Scanty rainfall - parts of Rajasthan, Gujarat, Punjab and Haryana
Moderate rainfall - Indo-Gangetic plains and Southeastern parts of the country.
Heavy rainfall - Kerala, North eastern states and Western Ghats.
Regions in India with water scarcity - Rajasthan, Gujarat, Maharashtra, Uttar Pradesh, Madhya Pradesh, Chattisgarh, Andhra Pradesh, Tamil Nadu.
In India, most of the water supply and regeneration is dependant on the monsoon rains. There are some worrying facts about the water situation. The effects of world climate change are being felt all over the planet. For a country like India, where agricultural activities are still dependent on regular monsoon rainfall, it is a cause of worry for adequate and reliable water supply. Erratic rainfall like heavy rains in areas receiving minimal rainfall (Rajasthan floods of 2006 in Barmer), scanty rainfall in areas receiving good rainfall (Karnataka and A.P. drought of 2006), melting of glaciers are causing problems in surface water supply, ground water recharge and agriculture.
Major diversion of water from the rivers has resulted in many downstream areas having reduced ground water recharge. This includes urban areas which have huge requirements of water and depend on dams and reservoir supply. Cities have an additional problem of clearing vegetation from land to build houses, roads, etc. Large concreting of surface areas does not allow water to seep into the ground to be stored in aquifiers leading to water shortages. Because of this, there is water run off or water remains on the surface which increases the surface run off and floods in cities causing heavy damage.
## Measurement of Water Pollution
The contamination of river water usually occurrs due to the presence of coliform bacteria. Coliform is a group of bacteria found in human intestines. The presence of coliform in the river water indicates its contamination by disease-causing micro-organisms. Although coliform itself is harmless, but its presence in river water indicates that other harmful intestinal bacteria might also be present. The pH of river water is measured by using universal indicator paper. If the pH of river water is below 7 , then the river water will be acidic and hence polluted.

## Learn More
Water pollution is a serious health hazard in India. The principal sources of water pollution and water pollutants are domestic sewage and industrial effluents. The domestic sewage contains human faeces, urine, kitchen/cloth washings, organic wastes, industrial wastes, etc. Sewage provides food for decomposers, so the population of microorganisms increases.
The microorganisms/decomposers cause decomposition of sewage and takes up most of the oxygen dissolved in water. So, the BOD (Biological or Biochemical oxygen demand) of water increases. BOD of pure drinking water is less than 1ppm (parts per million) or $\mathrm{mg} / \mathrm{l}$. A weak organic waste will have a BOD below $1500 \mathrm{mg} / \mathrm{l}$, medium organic waste between 1500-4000 $\mathrm{mg} / \mathrm{l}$, while it is above $4000 \mathrm{mg} / \mathrm{l}$ in strong organic waste. The degree of water pollution is directly proportional to BOD.COD (Chemical oxygen demand) is the amount of oxygen required to oxidize all pollutant materials in one litre of water at $20^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ in five days. The value of COD is much higher than BOD.
In sewage, phosphorus and nitrogen compounds are present which are necessary for the growth of algae. In polluted water, these accumulate and result into excessive growth of algae on water surface. Excessive growth of algae is called water bloom. Presence of extra nutrients brings about dense growth of plankton algae (termed as eutrophication) which increases organic loading and brings about reduction in the oxygen content of water causing death of aquatic animals.
Industrial effluents (or industrial discharge) contain heavy metals (mercury, lead, copper, arsenic, and calcium), inorganic pollutants (acids, alkalies and bleaching liquors) and organic pollutants (phenol, naptha, proteins, aromatic compounds, cellulose fibres, etc.). The non biodegradable pollutants like DDT, pesticides, phenolic compounds etc. are not decomposed by microorganisms. These get accumulated in tissue in increasing concentration along the food chain and the phenomenon is called biological magnification.
## Dams
Dams are large water-storing bodies across the river to regulate the flow of water. Many dams have been constructed across the major rivers which serve as storehouse of river water. The stored water is allowed to flow dowstream in order to generate electricity and carry water to long distances for the purpose of irrigation. Some examples of famous dams and canals in India are :
1. Bhakra Dam built on the river Sutlej in Punjab.
2. Sardar Sarovar Dam built on river Narmada in 1940 in Gujarat.
3. Tehri Dam on river Ganga in Tehri, Uttranchal (highest dam).
4. Tawa Dam on the Tawa river in Hoshangabad (MP).
5. Mettur Dam in Kaveri river in Tamil Nadu (oldest dam).

## Benefits of Dams
(i) Canal system originating from the dams can transfer large amounts of water to great distances which helps to raise agricultural production in distant places. For example, Indira Gandhi Canal originating from Bhakra dam has brought greenery in deserts of Rajasthan.
(ii) Dams ensure continuous water supply in the surrounding areas.
(iii) Flowing water from a height is used for generating electricity.
## Problems associated with construction of Dams
(i) Construction of dams creates displacement of a large number of peasants and tribals without adequate compensation or rehabilitation. These social problems result in protests like Narmada Bachao Andolan. The people who have been displaced by the construction of Tawa Dam in 1970s are still fighting for the benefits they were promised. The other social problems are created by the mismanagement of the officials. It has been observed that people close to the source of water grow water intensive crops like sugarcane and rice whereas people further downstream do not get sufficient water to grow these crops. This causes social problems due to the unequal distribution of water.
(ii) Construction of dam is an expensive project. Some people think that such expenditure does not generate the proportionate benefits. Others believe that no progress can happen without constructing dams as it is the most efficient way to use water resources.
(iii) Construction of dams result in deforestation and loss of biodiversity.

Khadins in Rajasthan consist of a long (100-300 m long) earthen embankment built across the lower edge of the sloping farmland. Kulhs was a canal irrigation system developed in Himachal Pradesh about 400 years ago. Stream waters were channelled into the downhill villages. Villages had made a common agreement about water use. These kulhs were under looked by few people who were being paid by the villagers. The water from these kulhs was used for irrigation as well as percolated into the soil to feed springs. Ever since these kulhs were taken over by the irrigation department, they became defunct.
In the present day, rain water harvesting comprises of water storage and ground water recharging. The rain water collected from the roof top of a house or a building is then filtered and stored in proper tanks. This stored water is either used for human consumption, irrigation or can be channelised through a drain pipe into a well to raise the water table.

(ii) Treatment of waste water : Waste water usually contains organic and inorganic impurities which must be removed by primary and secondary treatments. This water can then be used for industrial and irrigational purposes. The benefits of using treated waste water are :
(a) Conservation of water resources.
(b) Increases crop yield by conserving nutrients and reducing the need for artificial fertilizers.
(c) Reduces pollution of surface water resources.
(d) Low cost method for sanitary disposal of municipal waste water.
(iii) Storage of water : Rain water should be stored in huge tanks for use in agriculture. Dams and reservoirs also store water which can be supplied to canals and rivers throughout the year.

## COAL AND PETROLEUM
Fossil fuels consist of deposits of dead organisms. The organic matter takes centuries to form. Fossil fuels principally consist of carbon and hydrogen bonds. There are three types of fossil fuels which can all be used for energy production; coal, oil and natural gas. They are a non-renewable source of energy formed from plants and animals that lived up to 300 million years ago and are found in deposits beneath the earth. The fuels are burnt to release the chemical energy that is essential to modern society. Over $85 \%$ of our energy demands are met by the combustion of fossil fuels.

more carbon, which gives it a higher energy content. Lignite is softest and is low in carbon but high in hydrogen and oxygen content. Bituminous is in between. Today, the precursor to coal peat - is still found in many countries and is also used as an energy source.
## Petroleum
(comes from the Latin word petra, "rock," and oleum, "oil.") :
Oil is a liquid fossil fuel that is formed from the remains of marine microorganisms deposited on the sea floor. After millions of years, these deposits end up in rock and sediment where oil is trapped in small spaces. It can be extracted by large drilling platforms. Oil is the most widely used fossil fuel. Crude oil consists of many different organic compounds which are transformed to products in a refining process. It is applied in cars, jets, roads, roofs etc. Oil cannot be found everywhere on earth. Some scientists say that tiny diatoms are the source of oil. Diatoms are sea creatures of the size of a pin head. They do one thing just like plants; they can convert sunlight directly into energy. The various steps in this process are-
(1) Diatoms died and fell on the sea floor, (2) Here, they were buried under sediment and other rock, (3) The rock squeezed the diatoms and carbon eventually turned into oil under great pressure and heat. As the earth changed, moved and folded, pockets where oil and natural gas can be found, were formed.
Oil has been used for more than 5,000-6,000 years. We are currently in an energy crisis. Fossil fuels are the lifeblood of our modern society but our supply has a finite end. Fossil fuels take millions of years to make. We are using up the fuels that were made more than 300 million years ago before the time of the dinosaurs. Once they are gone it is almost impossible to get them back once again. So, it's best not to waste fossil fuels. They are not renewable; they can't really be made again. We can save fossil fuels by conserving energy.
## Pollution caused by Burning Coal and Petroleum based Fuels
When coal and petroleum based fuels are burnt, the products are carbon dioxide, water, sulphur dioxide and nitrogen oxides. If incomplete combustion takes place in an insufficient supply of oxygen then carbon monoxide is also produced . Of all the products of combustion, only water is harmless while others pollute the environment.
## Few examples are-
(i) Sulphur dioxide causes respiratory disorders. It dissolves in rain water making it acidic and causing damage to plant life, buildings, monuments and metal structures.
(ii) Nitrogen oxide also has similar effects like sulphur dioxide.
(iii) Carbon dioxide has green house effect and leads to global warming.
(iv) Carbon monoxide is a poisonous gas which gets into our blood stream, binds with RBCs and causes suffocation. Excess carbon monoxide can also cause death.
## Methods of conservation of fossil fuels
(i) Burning coal causes pollution. Coal can be converted to liquid fuel and Compressed Natural Gas (CNG) through coal gasification.
(ii) Wastage during extraction and transportation of fossil fuel should be avoided by using modern technology.
(iii) Oil wells and coal mines should be protected from fire to avoid wastage, pollution and loss of life or property.
(iv) Alternative sources of energy such as hydroelectricity, nuclear, solar, wind power and biogas plants should be used.
