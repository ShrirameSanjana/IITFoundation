## DATA
The word 'data' means information in the form of numerical figure or a set of given facts.
- Raw Data: Data obtained from direct observation and experience.
Example: Marks of 5 students in unit test is raw data or ungrouped data.
- Grouped Data : The data can be represented into classes or groups. Such a presentation is known as grouped data.
- Class Limits : Suppose, marks obtained by all of the students are divided into seven classes namely, 25-35, 35-45 and so on. In class $25-35,25$ is called Lower class limit and 35 is called Upper class limit.
- Class Size : The difference between upper and lower class limit is called Class size.
Here, class size $=35-25=45-35=10$.
- Class Mark : Class mark $=\frac{\text { Upper limit }+ \text { lower limit }}{2}$
## GROUPED AND UNGROUPED DATA / DISCRETE AND CONTINUOUS FREQUENCY DISTRIBUTION
(i) Discrete Frequency Distribution : If each data is given with their frequency, then this type of frequency distribution is called Discrete Frequency Distribution.
(ii) Continuous Frequency Distribution : If the data is given in the form of class interval with frequency, then this type of frequency distribution is called Continuous Frequency Distribution.
(iii) Measures of Central Tendency : A certain numerical values representatives of the ungrouped data is called Measures of Central Tendency, namely mean, median and mode.
## MEAN OF DISCRETE FREQUENCY DISTRIBUTION
Mean of discrete frequency distribution may be computed by any one of the following methods :
(i) Direct method
(ii) Assume mean method (Short-cut method)
(iii) Step-Deviation method
(i) Direct Method: For the discrete frequency distribution, we have
| $\boldsymbol{x}_{i}$ | $x_{1}$ | $x_{2}$ | $x_{3}$ | $\ldots \ldots \ldots$ | $x_{n}$ |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| $\boldsymbol{f}_{i}$ | $f_{1}$ | $f_{2}$ | $f_{3}$ | $\ldots \ldots \ldots$ | $f_{n}$ |
then arithmetic mean of values $x_{1}, x_{2}, \ldots ., x_{n}$ is given by
$\operatorname{Mean}(\bar{x})=\frac{f_{1} x_{1}+f_{2} x_{2}+\ldots \ldots \ldots \ldots+f_{n} x_{n}}{f_{1}+f_{2}+\ldots \ldots \ldots \ldots+f_{n}}=\sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{f_{i} x_{i}}{\Sigma f_{i}}$
Note : If $x_{i}$ and $f_{i}$ are sufficiently small, then direct method is an appropriate choice.

## WEIGHTED AVERAGE
If we have two or more groups of numbers whose individual averages are known, then combined average of all the numbers of all the groups is known as Weighted Average. Thus, if there are $k$ groups having number of numbers $n_{1}, n_{2}$, $n_{3}, \ldots, n_{k}$ with averages $A_{1}, A_{2}, A_{3}, \ldots, A_{k}$ respectively; then weighted average
$$
A_{w}=\frac{n_{1} A_{1}+n_{2} A_{2}+n_{3} A_{3}+\ldots+n_{k} A_{k}}{n_{1}+n_{2}+n_{3}+\ldots+n_{k}}=\frac{\sum n_{i} A_{i}}{\sum n_{i}}
$$

## MEDIAN
The median is that value of the given number of observations, which divides it into exactly two parts.
Median is the middle most or the central value of the variate in a set of observations, when the observations are arranged either in ascending or in descending order of their magnitudes.
- Methods to Find Median : When the data is arranged in ascending (or descending) order, the median of ungrouped data is calculated as follows:
(i) When the number of observations (n) is odd, the median is the value of the $\left(\frac{\mathrm{n}+1}{2}\right)$ th observation.
(ii) When the number of observations ( n ) is even, the median is the mean of the $\left(\frac{\mathrm{n}}{2}\right)$ th and the $\left(\frac{\mathrm{n}}{2}+1\right)$ th observations. value in 1869 and the medium in 1880.

## CUMULATIVE FREQUENCY CURVE (OGIVE)
Cumulative frequency curve or an ogive is the graphical representation of a cumulative frequency distribution. There are two methods of constructing an ogive:
(i) Less than method (ii) More than method
(i) Less Than Method: Following are the steps for construction of an ogive:
(a) Construct a cumulative frequency table of less than type.
(b) Mark upper class limits along $X$-axis.
(c) Mark the corresponding cumulative frequency along $Y$-axis.
(d) Plot the points $\left(x_{i}, f_{i}\right)$, where $x_{i}$ is the upper limit of a class and $f_{i}$ is the corresponding c.f. and join them by a smooth free hand curve.
The curve, we get is the Less Than Type Ogive.

## MEDIAN BY GRAPH
We can find the median graphically in two ways :
(i) By drawing more than and less than Ogives on same graph simultaneously.
(ii) By drawing only less than Ogive.
(i) Median by Drawing Both More Than and Less Than Ogives:
If we draw the two types of curves i.e. Less Than type Ogive and More Than type Ogive on same pair of axes, then these two curves intersects each other at a point. From this point of intersection, if we draw a perpendicular to $X$-axis, it will intersect the $X$-axis at some point. The X -coordinate of this point is the Median.
(ii) Median by Drawing Only Less than Ogive :
Generally, we use only Less Than type Ogive to calculate the median. Steps are as follows:
(a) First prepare a less than type cumulative frequency distribution table.
(b) Draw less than cumulative frequency curve (ogive).
(c) If total number of observations is N , then locate $\frac{N}{2}$ on cumulative frequency axis i.e. $Y$-axis.
(d) From this point on $Y$-axis, draw a horizontal line parallel to $X$-axis which meet the ogive at some point.
(e) Through this point on the curve, draw a perpendicular to $X$-axis.
(f) The perpendicular meets the $X$-axis at some point. X-coordinate of this point is the required median.

12. The mean of the marks secured by 25 students of section $A$ of class $X$ is 47 , that of 35 students of section $B$ is 51 and that of 30 students of section $C$ is $\mathbf{5 3}$. Find the combined mean of the marks of students of three sections of class $\boldsymbol{X}$.
Sol. Mean of the marks of 25 students of $X A=47$
∴ Sum of the marks of 25 students $=25 \times 47=1175$
Mean of the marks of 35 students of $X B=51$
∴ Sum of the marks of 35 students $=35 \times 51=1785$
Mean of the marks of 30 students of $X C=53$
∴ Sum of the marks of 30 students $=30 \times 53=1590$
Adding equations (i), (ii) and (iii), we get
Sum of the marks of $(25+35+30)$ i.e., 90 students $=1175+1785+1590=4550$
Thus, the combined mean of the marks of students of three sections $=\frac{4550}{90}=50.56$

## SOME BASIC TERMS
- Probability as a Measure of Uncertainity
(i) Experiment : An operation which can produce some well defined outcomes, is known as an experiment.
(ii) Trial : Performing of an experiment is called a trial. For example : Tossing a coin, throwing a dice.
(iii) Sample space : The set of all possible outcomes in a trial is called sample space.
- If a fair coin is tossed, there are two possible outcomes, namely head (H) \& Tail (T).
∴ Sample space $\mathrm{S}=\{\mathrm{H}, \mathrm{T}\}$
- When two coins are tossed ;
$\mathrm{S}=\{\mathrm{HH}, \mathrm{HT}, \mathrm{TH}, \mathrm{TT}\}$
- Sample space, when three coins are tossed together.
$\mathrm{S}=\{\mathrm{HHH}, \mathrm{HHT}, \mathrm{HTH}, \mathrm{THH}, \mathrm{HTT}, \mathrm{THT}, \mathrm{TTH}, \mathrm{TTT}\}$
- When an unbiased die is thrown, then sample space,
$S=\{1,2,3,4,5,6\}$
(iv) Elementary event : An event having only one outcome is called an elementary event.
(v) Compound event : An event having two or more outcomes is called compound event.
(vi) Equally likely event : Outcomes of trial are said to be equally likely if taking into consideration all the relevant evidences, there is no reason to expect one in preference to the others, i.e., probability of each elementary event is same.
For example : In throwing an unbiased die, all the six faces are equally likely to come.
