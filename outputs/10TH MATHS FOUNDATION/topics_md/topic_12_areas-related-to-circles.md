## TERMS RELATED TO CIRCLE
Chord : A line segment joining any two points of a circle is called a chord. A chord passing through the centre of circle is called its diameter. $A B$ and $C D$ both are chords but the chord $C D$ passes through the centre. Hence, $C D$ is the diameter also.
Diameter of a circle $=$ twice the radius of the circle.
Note : Diameter is the longest chord of a circle.
Arc : A part of a circle is called an arc. In Figure (i), $A C$ is an $\operatorname{arc}$ and is denoted by $\operatorname{arc} A B C$ or $\widetilde{A B C}$
(i)
Semi-circle : A diameter of a circle divides a circle into two equal arcs, each known as a semi-circle. In Figure (ii), $P Q$ is a diameter and $\overparen{P R Q}$ is a semi-circle and so is $\overparen{P B Q}$.
Minor arc : An arc of a circle whose length is less than a semi-circle of the same circle is called a minor arc. $P A Q$ is a minor arc.
Major arc : An arc of a circle whose length is greater than a semi-circle of the same circle is called a major arc. In figure (iii), $\operatorname{arc} P B Q$ is a major arc.
Sector : The region bounded by an arc of a circle and two radii at its end points is called a sector. In the figure (iv), the shaded portion is a minor sector formed by the arc $P R Q$ and the unshaded portion is a major sector formed by the $\operatorname{arc} P T Q$.
(iv)
Segment : A chord divides the interior of a circle into two parts, each part called a segment. The segment in which the centre of the circle does not lie is called minor segment and the segment in which the centre of the circle lies is called major segment. In the figure (v), the shaded region $P A Q P$ and the unshaded region $P B Q P$ are both segments of the circle.
$P A Q P$ is called a minor segment and $P B Q P$ is called a major segment.
(v)
Circumference : Take a wheel and mark a point $P$ on the wheel where it touches the ground. Rotate the wheel along a line till the point $P$ touches back on the ground. Measure the distance between the first and last position of $P$ along the line. This distance is equal to the circumference of the circle. Thus, the length of the boundary of a circle is the circumference of the circle.
The ratio of the circumference of a circle to its diameter is always a constant. This constant is universally denoted by Greek letter $\pi$.
Therefore, $\frac{c}{d}=\frac{c}{2 r}=\pi$ where $c$ is the circumference, $d$ its diameter and $r$ is its radius of the circle. An approximate value of $\pi$ is $\frac{22}{7}$. Circumference of a circle $=2 \pi r$ or $\pi d$, where $r$ is the radius and $d$ is the diameter of the circle.
The distance travelled by a person sitting on a Ferris wheel can be determined by calculating the Know! circumference of the wheel.
## AREA OF A CIRCLE
Area of a circle $=\pi r^{2}$, where ' $r$ ' is the radius of the circle.
## AREA OF A SEMI-CIRCLE
Area of a semi-circle $=\frac{1}{2} \pi r^{2}$
## AREA OF A QUADRANT OF A CIRCLE
Area of a quadrant $=\frac{\pi r^{2}}{4}$

## CONCENTRIC CIRCLES

## AREA OF CIRCULAR PATH (RING)
If we have a circular field of radius ' $r$ ', surrounded by a path of uniform width ' $d$ ' and $r+d=R$ then, the area of the circular path
$=$ Area of the outer circle - Area of the inner circle
$=\left(\pi R^{2}-\pi r^{2}\right)$ sq. unit $=\pi\left(R^{2}-r^{2}\right)$ sq. unit

## PERIMETER AND AREA OF SECTOR OF A CIRCLE
Two radii OA and OB enclose a portion of the circular region which makes a central angle $\theta$. The region is called a sector of the circle. In fig., $A O B$ is the sector with central angle $\theta$. Let ' $l$ ' be the length of $\operatorname{arc} A B$.
Then, $\frac{\overparen{A B}}{\text { circumference }}=\frac{\theta}{360^{\circ}}$ [Corresponding arcs subtend proportional central angles] or $\quad \frac{l}{2 \pi r}=\frac{\theta}{360^{\circ}}$ or $l=2 \pi r \cdot \frac{\theta}{360^{\circ}}=\frac{\pi r \theta}{180}$, where r is the radius of the circle
$\therefore \quad$ Perimeter of the sector $=O A+O B+\overparen{A B}=2 r+\frac{\pi r \theta}{180}$
Also, $\frac{\text { Area of sector } A O B}{\text { Area of the circle }}=\frac{\theta}{360^{\circ}} \Rightarrow \frac{\text { Area of sector } A O B}{\pi \mathrm{r}^{2}}=\frac{\theta}{360^{\circ}} \quad$ or Area of sector $A O B=\pi \mathrm{r}^{2} \times \frac{\theta}{360^{\circ}}$

In right $\triangle O P A, \sin \left(\frac{\theta}{2}\right)=\frac{A P}{O A} \Rightarrow \sin \left(\frac{\theta}{2}\right)=\frac{A P}{r}$

## ILLUSTRATION : 4
A chord $A B$ of a circle of radius 10 cm makes a right angle at the centre of the circle. Find the area of the major and minor segments (Take $\boldsymbol{\pi} \boldsymbol{=} \mathbf{3 . 1 4}$ )
## SOLUTION :
We know that the area of a minor segment of angle $\theta$ (in degrees) in a circle of radius $r$ is given by
$$
A=\left\{\frac{\pi \theta}{360}-\sin \frac{\theta}{2} \cos \frac{\theta}{2}\right\} r^{2}
$$
Here, $\quad r=10$ and $\theta=90^{\circ}$
$$
\begin{aligned}
& \therefore A=\left\{\frac{3.14 \times 90}{360}-\sin 45^{\circ} \cos 45^{\circ}\right\} \times(10)^{2} \Rightarrow A=\left\{\frac{3.14}{4}-\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \times \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\right\} \times(10)^{2} \\
& \Rightarrow A=\{3.14 \times 25-50\}=(78.5-50)=28.5 \mathrm{~cm}^{2}
\end{aligned}
$$
Area of the major segment $=$ Area of the circle - Area of the minor segment
$$
=\left(3.14 \times 10^{2}-28.5\right)=(314-28.5)=285.5 \mathrm{~cm}^{2}
$$
## AREA OF COMBINATIONS OF PLANE FIGURES
Whenever a plane figure is made by combination of two or more plane figures, then the area of combination of plane figures is always equal to the sum of the areas of the constituent figures.
## IMPORTANT POINTS TO BE REMEMBER
(i) Distance moved by a wheel in 1 revolution $=$ Circumference of a wheel
(ii) Number of revolutions in one minute $=\frac{\text { Distance covered in1minute }}{\text { Circumference }}$
(iii) Angle described by minute hand in 60 minute $=360^{\circ}$.
(iv) Angle described by hour hand in 12 hour $=360^{\circ}$.
(v) The sum of the arcs of major and minor sectors of a circle is equal to the circumference of the circle.
(vi) The sum of the areas of major and minor sectors of a circle is equal to the area of the circle.
