## ANGLE
Consider a ray $\overrightarrow{O A}$. If this ray rotates about its end point $O$ and takes the position $\overrightarrow{O B}$, then the $\angle A O B$ has been generated.
An angle is considered as the figure obtained by rotating a given ray about its end-point. The initial position $\overrightarrow{O A}$ is called the initial side and the final position $\overrightarrow{O B}$ is called terminal side of the angle. The end point $O$ about which the ray rotates is called the vertex of the angle. The measure of an angle is the amount of rotation performed
to get the terminal side from initial side. Degree and radian are mainly two units for measuring angles.
Angle in Standard Position : An angle is said to be in standard position if its vertex coincides with the origin $O$ and the initial side coincides with $O X$ i.e. the positive direction of $x$-axis.
When angle is measured in anti-clockwise direction from positive direction of x -axis i.e. OX then the measured value of the angle is positive and when angle is measures in clockwise direction from positive direction of x -axis i.e. OX then the measured value of the angle is negative as shown in the figure.
## SYSTEM OF MEASUREMENT OF ANGLES
## Sexagesimal or English system :
The principal unit in this system is degree $\left(^{\circ}\right)$. One right angle is divided into 90 equal part and each part is called one degree $\left(1^{\circ}\right)$. One degree is divided into 60 equal parts and each part is called one minute $\left(1^{\prime}\right)$. One minute is divided into 60 equal parts and each part is called one second ( $1^{\prime \prime}$ ).
∴ One right angle $=90^{\circ}, 1^{\circ}=60^{\prime}, 1^{\prime}=60^{\prime \prime}$
Some of the angles whose measures are $90^{\circ}, 45^{\circ}, 60^{\circ}, 30^{\circ}, 360^{\circ}, 180^{\circ}, 270^{\circ}, 420^{\circ},-30^{\circ},-420^{\circ}$ are shown in figure.
In circular system, unit of an angle is "radian".
The relation between the two units degree and radian.
$180^{\circ}=\pi$ radian
1 radian $=\frac{180}{\pi}$ degree
1 degree $=\frac{\pi}{180}$ radian
## TRIGONOMETRIC RATIOS
Let $X O X^{\prime}$ and $Y O Y^{\prime}$ be horizontal and vertical axes respectively. Let $A$ be a point on $O X$. Let the ray $O A$ start rotating in the $X Y$-plane in an anti-clockwise direction about the point $O$ till it reaches its final position $O C$ after some interval of time. (See Fig.). Thus, an angle $C O A$ is formed. Let $\angle C O A=\theta$. ( $\theta$ is a Greek letter, and we read it as "theta"). Draw $C B \perp O X$. Now clearly $\triangle C B O$ is a right angled triangle.
In right $\triangle C B O, O C$ is the hypotenuse. $B C$ and $O B$ are called side opposite to angle $\theta$ and adjacent side of angle $\theta$ respectively.
Let $C B=p, O B=b$ and $O C=h$. Horizontal axis $X^{\prime} O X$ is called $x$-axis and vertical axis $Y O Y^{\prime}$ is called $y$-axis.
There are six ratios between $C B, O B$ and $O C$. Each ratio has given a specific name. These ratios are called trigonometric ratios.
These trigonometrical ratios are :
Sine of $\theta=\frac{\text { Side opposite to angle } \theta}{\text { Hypotenuse }}=\frac{C B}{O C}=\frac{p}{h} \quad$; Cosine of $\theta=\frac{\text { Adjacent side to angle } \theta}{\text { Hypotenuse }}=\frac{O B}{O C}=\frac{b}{h}$
Tangent of $\theta=\frac{\text { Side opposite to angle } \theta}{\text { Adjacent side to angle } \theta}=\frac{C B}{O B}=\frac{p}{b} \quad$; Cotangent of $\theta=\frac{\text { Adjacent side to angle } \theta}{\text { Side opposite to angle } \theta}=\frac{O B}{C B}=\frac{b}{p}$
Secant of $\theta=\frac{\text { Hypotenuse }}{\text { Adjacent side to angle } \theta}=\frac{O C}{O B}=\frac{h}{b} \quad ; \quad$ Cosecant of $\theta=\frac{\text { Hypotenuse }}{\text { Side opposite to angle } \theta}=\frac{O C}{C B}=\frac{h}{p}$
Sine of $\theta$ is abbreviated as $\sin \theta$, Cosine of $\theta$ is abbreviated as $\cos \theta$, Tangent of $\theta$ is abbreviated as $\tan \theta$
Cotangent of $\theta$ is abbreviated as $\cot \theta$, Secant of $\theta$ is abbreviated as $\sec \theta$ and Cosecant of $\theta$ is abbreviated as $\operatorname{cosec} \theta$
$\therefore \quad \sin \theta=\frac{p}{h}, \cos \theta=\frac{b}{h} ; \tan \theta=\frac{p}{b}, \cot \theta=\frac{b}{p} ; \sec \theta=\frac{h}{b}, \operatorname{cosec} \theta=\frac{h}{p}$
Note that $\sin \theta$ is an abbreviation for "sine of angle $\theta$ " and not the product of $\sin$ and $\theta$. Same meaning of all trigonometrical ratio. Also we write the $(\sin \theta)^{\mathrm{n}}$ as $\sin ^{\mathrm{n}} \theta$ also and same for other trigonometrical ratios.
Trigonometry ratios are helpful to estimate the causes of a collision in a vehicle accident and it is also used in navigation to set the direction.

## SOLUTION :
In right angled $\triangle A B C$,
$$
\sin C=\frac{\text { opposite side }}{\text { hypotenuse }}=\frac{A B}{A C}=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} ; \quad \cos C=\frac{\text { adjacent side }}{\text { hypotenuse }}=\frac{B C}{A C}=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}
$$
and $\tan C=\frac{\text { opposite side }}{\text { adjacent side }}=\frac{A B}{B C}=\frac{1}{1}=1$
If $\sin \theta+\sin ^{2} \theta=1$, find the value of $(\cos \theta)^{12}+3(\cos \theta)^{10}+3(\cos \theta)^{8}+(\cos \theta)^{6}+2(\cos \theta)^{4}+2(\cos \theta)^{2}$
## SOLUTION :
We are given that $\sin \theta+\sin ^{2} \theta=1$
$\Rightarrow \quad \sin \theta=1-\sin ^{2} \theta$
$\Rightarrow \quad \sin \theta=\cos ^{2} \theta$
$\Rightarrow \quad \sin ^{2} \theta=\cos ^{4} \theta$
Hence, $(\cos \theta)^{12}+3(\cos \theta)^{10}+3(\cos \theta)^{8}+(\cos \theta)^{6}+2(\cos \theta)^{4}+2(\cos \theta)^{2}$
$=\cos ^{6} \theta\left\{\cos ^{6} \theta+3 \cos ^{4} \theta+3 \cos ^{2} \theta+1\right\}+2 \cos ^{4} \theta+2 \cos ^{2} \theta$
$=\left(\cos ^{2} \theta\right)^{3}\left\{\cos ^{2} \theta+1\right\}^{3}+2 \cos ^{4} \theta+2 \cos ^{2} \theta$
$=\left\{\left(\cos ^{2} \theta\left(\cos ^{2} \theta+1\right\}^{3}+2 \cos ^{4} \theta+2 \cos ^{2} \theta\right.\right.$
$=\left(\cos ^{4} \theta+\cos ^{2} \theta\right\}^{3}+2 \cos ^{4} \theta+2 \cos ^{2} \theta$
$=\left(\sin ^{2} \theta+\cos ^{2} \theta\right\}^{3}+2 \sin ^{2} \theta+2 \cos ^{2} \theta$
[using (ii)]
$=\quad 1^{3}+2\left(\sin ^{2} \theta+\cos ^{2} \theta\right)=1+2 \times 1=1+2=3$

## BASIC FORMULAE OR TRIGONOMETRIC IDENTITY
An equation involving trigonometric ratios of an angle is called a trigonometric identity, if it is true for all values of the angle(s) involved. For any acute angle $\theta$, we have
(i) $\sin \theta \cdot \operatorname{cosec} \theta=1$ or $\sin \theta=\frac{1}{\operatorname{cosec} \theta}$ or $\operatorname{cosec} \theta=\frac{1}{\sin \theta}$
(ii) $\cos \theta \cdot \sec \theta=1$ or $\cos \theta=\frac{1}{\sec \theta}$ or $\sec \theta=\frac{1}{\cos \theta}$
(iii) $\tan \theta \cdot \cot \theta=1$ or $\cot \theta=\frac{1}{\tan \theta}$ or $\tan \theta=\frac{1}{\cot \theta}$
(iv) $\sin ^{2} \theta+\cos ^{2} \theta=1$ or $\cos ^{2} \theta=1-\sin ^{2} \theta$ or $\sin ^{2} \theta=1-\cos ^{2} \theta$
(v) $\sec ^{2} \theta-\tan ^{2} \theta=1$ or $\sec ^{2} \theta=1+\tan ^{2} \theta$ or $\tan ^{2} \theta=\sec ^{2} \theta-1$
(vi) $\operatorname{cosec}^{2} \theta-\cot ^{2} \theta=1$ or $\operatorname{cosec}^{2} \theta=1+\cot ^{2} \theta$ or $\cot ^{2} \theta=\operatorname{cosec}^{2} \theta-1$
(vii) $\tan \theta=\frac{\sin \theta}{\cos \theta}$
(viii) $\cot \theta=\frac{\cos \theta}{\sin \theta}$
The trigonometric ratios sine and cosine are used to model many real-life scenario as musical tones, Did You, Know! tides, electrical currents and radio waves.
## TRIGONOMETRIC RATIOS FOR COMPLEMENTARY ANGLES
Let $X O X^{\prime}$ and $Y O Y^{\prime}$ be horizontal and vertical axes respectively.
Let $\angle P O M=\theta$
Draw $P M \perp O X . \quad \triangle P M O$ is a right triangle
Also,
$$
\begin{aligned}
& \angle P O M+\angle O P M+\angle P M O=180^{\circ} \\
& \angle P O M+\angle O P M+90^{\circ}=180^{\circ}
\end{aligned}
$$
$\therefore \quad \angle P O M+\angle O P M=90^{\circ}$
$\Rightarrow \quad \angle O P M=90^{\circ}-\theta$
i.e. $\angle O P M$ and $\angle P O M$ are complementary angles.
For angle $\left(90^{\circ}-\theta\right)$,
$$
\begin{aligned}
& \sin \left(90^{\circ}-\theta\right)=\frac{\mathrm{OM}}{\mathrm{OP}}=\cos \theta, \cos \left(90^{\circ}-\theta\right)=\frac{P M}{O P}=\sin \theta \\
& \tan \left(90^{\circ}-\theta\right)=\frac{O M}{P M}=\cot \theta, \cot \left(90^{\circ}-\theta\right)=\frac{P M}{O M}=\tan \theta \\
& \operatorname{cosec}\left(90^{\circ}-\theta\right)=\frac{O P}{O M}=\sec \theta, \sec \left(90^{\circ}-\theta\right)=\frac{O P}{P M}=\operatorname{cosec} \theta
\end{aligned}
$$

## TRIGONOMETRIC RATIOS OF SUM AND DIFFERENCE OF ANGLES
(i) $\quad \sin (A+B)=\sin A \cos B+\cos A \sin B$
(ii) $\quad \sin (A-B)=\sin A \cos B-\cos A \sin B$
(iii) $\cos (A+B)=\cos A \cos B-\sin A \sin B$
(iv) $\cos (A-B)=\cos A \cos B+\sin A \sin B$
(v) $\quad \tan (A+B)=\frac{\tan A+\tan B}{1-\tan A \tan B}$
(vi) $\quad \tan (A-B)=\frac{\tan A-\tan B}{1+\tan A \tan B}$
## FORMULAS TO TRANSFORM SUM OR DIFFERENCE INTO PRODUCT
(i) $\quad \sin C+\sin D=2 \sin \frac{C+D}{2} \cos \frac{C-D}{2}$
(ii) $\quad \sin C-\sin D=2 \cos \frac{C+D}{2} \sin \frac{C-D}{2}$
(iii) $\quad \cos C+\cos D=2 \cos \frac{C+D}{2} \cos \frac{C-D}{2}$
(iv) $\quad \cos C-\cos D=2 \sin \frac{C+D}{2} \sin \frac{D-C}{2}$
## TRIGONOMETRIC FUNCTIONS OF MULTIPLE AND SUBMULTIPLE ANGLES
(i) $\sin 2 \theta=2 \sin \theta \cdot \cos \theta=\frac{2 \tan \theta}{1+\tan ^{2} \theta}$
(ii) $\cos 2 \theta=\cos ^{2} \theta-\sin ^{2} \theta=2 \cos ^{2} \theta-1=1-2 \sin ^{2} \theta=\frac{1-\tan ^{2} \theta}{1+\tan ^{2} \theta}$
(iii) $\tan 2 \theta=\frac{2 \tan \theta}{1-\tan ^{2} \theta}$
(iv) $\cot 2 \theta=\frac{\cot ^{2} \theta-1}{2 \cot \theta}$
(v) $\sin 3 \theta=3 \sin \theta-4 \sin ^{3} \theta$
(vi) $\cos 3 \theta=4 \cos ^{3} \theta-3 \cos \theta$
(vii) $\tan 3 \theta=\frac{3 \tan \theta-\tan ^{3} \theta}{1-3 \tan ^{2} \theta}$
## CASE STUDY-1 : Express trigonometric ratios into another form
- All other trigonometric ratios, when value of $\sin \theta$ is given:
Let $\sin \theta=x$. Then, $\cos \theta=\sqrt{1-\sin ^{2} \theta}=\sqrt{1-x^{2}}$
$\tan \theta=\frac{\sin \theta}{\cos \theta}=\frac{x}{\sqrt{1-x^{2}}}, \cot \theta=\frac{1}{\tan \theta}=\frac{\sqrt{1-x^{2}}}{x}$,
$\operatorname{cosec} \theta=\frac{1}{\sin \theta}=\frac{1}{x}, \sec \theta=\frac{1}{\cos \theta}=\frac{1}{\sqrt{1-x^{2}}}$
- All other trigonometric ratios, when value of $\cos \theta$ is given.
Let $\cos \theta=x$. Then, $\sin \theta=\sqrt{1-\cos ^{2} \theta}=\sqrt{1-x^{2}}$
$\tan \theta=\frac{\sin \theta}{\cos \theta}=\frac{\sqrt{1-x^{2}}}{x}, \cot \theta=\frac{1}{\tan \theta}=\frac{x}{\sqrt{1-x^{2}}}$
$\operatorname{cosec} \theta=\frac{1}{\sin \theta}=\frac{1}{\sqrt{1-x^{2}}}, \sec \theta=\frac{1}{\cos \theta}=\frac{1}{x}$.
- All other trigonometric ratios, when value of $\tan \theta$ is given,
Let $\tan \theta=x \Rightarrow \sec \theta=\sqrt{1+\tan ^{2} \theta}=\sqrt{1+x^{2}}$
$\cot \theta=\frac{1}{\tan \theta}=\frac{1}{x}, \cos \theta=\frac{1}{\sec \theta}=\frac{1}{\sqrt{1+x^{2}}}$
$\sin \theta=\sqrt{1-\cos ^{2} \theta}=\sqrt{1-\frac{1}{1+x^{2}}}=\frac{x}{\sqrt{1+x^{2}}}, \quad \operatorname{cosec} \theta=\frac{1}{\sin \theta}=\frac{\sqrt{1+x^{2}}}{x}$.
Let us understand the above concepts in a better way in different cases through illustrative examples.
CASE-I : Express the trigonometric ratios in term of any other trigonometric ratio.
Illustration: Express the trigonometric ratios $\sin A, \cos A$ and $\tan A$ in terms of secA.
Sol. $\cos A=\frac{1}{\sec A}$
$$
\begin{aligned}
& \tan ^{2} A=\sec ^{2}-1 \Rightarrow \tan A=\sqrt{\sec ^{2} A-1} \\
& \sin A=\sqrt{1-\cos ^{2} A}=\sqrt{1-\frac{1}{\sec ^{2} x}}=\frac{\sqrt{\sec ^{2} A-1}}{\sec A}
\end{aligned}
$$
CASE-II : On proving results involving trigonometric ratios.
Illustration: If $\tan \theta+\sin \theta=m$ and $\tan \theta-\sin \theta=n$, show that $m^{2}-n^{2}=4 \sqrt{m n}$.
Sol. L.H.S $=\mathrm{m}^{2}-\mathrm{n}^{2}=(m+n)(\mathrm{m}-\mathrm{n})$
$$
\begin{aligned}
& =(\tan \theta+\sin \theta+\tan \theta-\sin \theta)(\tan \theta+\sin \theta-\tan \theta+\sin \theta)=4 \sqrt{\tan ^{2} \theta \sin ^{2} \theta} \\
& =4 \sqrt{\tan ^{2} \theta\left(1-\cos ^{2} \theta\right)}=4 \sqrt{(\tan \theta+\sin \theta)(\tan \theta-\sin \theta)}=4 \sqrt{m n}=\text { R.H.S. }
\end{aligned}
$$
CASE-III : Values of trigonometric ratios in terms of the value of one of them.
Illustration: If $\sec \theta=x+\frac{1}{4 x}$ prove that : $\sec \theta+\tan \theta=2 x$ or $\frac{1}{2 x}$
Sol. We have,
$$
\tan ^{2} \theta=\left(x+\frac{1}{4 x}\right)^{2}-1 \Rightarrow \tan \theta= \pm\left(x-\frac{1}{4 x}\right)
$$
When $\tan \theta=\left(x-\frac{1}{4 x}\right):$ In this case, $\sec \theta+\tan \theta=x+\frac{1}{4 x}+x-\frac{1}{4 x}=2 x$
When $\tan \theta=-\left(x-\frac{1}{4 x}\right):$ In this case, $\sec \theta+\tan \theta=\frac{1}{2 x}$.
Hence, $\sec \theta+\tan \theta=2 x$ or $\frac{1}{2 x}$.

## CASE STUDY-2 : Proving trigonometric identities using different methodology
- Algebraic identities:
$$
\begin{aligned}
& a^{2}-b^{2}=(a+b)(a-b) ; a^{2}+b^{2}=(a+b)^{2}-2 a b \\
& a^{3}-b^{3}=(a-b)\left(a^{2}+a b+b^{2}\right) ; a^{3}+b^{3}=(a+b)\left(a^{2}-a b+b^{2}\right)
\end{aligned}
$$
## - Rationalisation:
Rationalising factor of $a+b$ is $a-b$ and rationalising factor of $a-b$ is $a+b$.
Multiply and divide expression by its rationalising factor is called rationalisation.
- Componendo and dividendo
If $\frac{a}{b}=\frac{c}{d}$, then
by Componendo: $\frac{a+b}{b}=\frac{c+d}{d}$ or $\frac{a}{a+b}=\frac{c}{c+d}$; by Dividendo : $\frac{a-b}{b}=\frac{c-d}{d}$ or $\frac{a}{a-b}=\frac{c}{c-d}$ by Componendo and dividendo $=\frac{a+b}{a-b}=\frac{c+d}{c-d}$.
## CASE-I : Proving trigonometric identities by using algebraic identities.
Illustration: Prove that $\sin ^{6} A+\cos ^{6} A=1-3 \sin ^{2} A \cdot \cos ^{2} A$
Sol. LHS $=\sin ^{6} A+\cos ^{6} A=\left(\sin ^{2} A\right)^{3}+\left(\cos ^{2} A\right)^{3}$
$\left.=\left(\sin ^{2} A+\cos ^{2} A\right)\left\{\left(\sin ^{2} A\right)^{2}+\left(\cos ^{2} A\right)^{2}-\sin ^{2} A \cos ^{2} A\right)\right\}\left[\because a^{3}+b^{3}=(a+b)\left(a^{2}-a b+b^{2}\right)\right]$
$=\left(\sin ^{2} A+\cos ^{2} A\right)^{2}-2 \sin ^{2} A \cos ^{2} A-\sin ^{2} A \cos ^{2} A\left[\because \mathrm{a}^{2}+\mathrm{b}^{2}=(\mathrm{a}+\mathrm{b})^{2}-2 \mathrm{ab}\right]$
$=\left(\sin ^{2} A+\cos ^{2} A\right)^{2}-3 \sin ^{2} A \cos ^{2} A=1-3 \sin ^{2} A \cos ^{2} A=$ RHS
## CASE-II : Proving trigonometric identities by using rationalisation.
Illustration: Prove that $\sqrt{\frac{1-\sin \theta}{1+\sin \theta}}=\sec \theta-\tan \theta$
Sol. We have,
$$
\begin{aligned}
& \text { LHS }=\sqrt{\frac{1-\sin \theta}{1+\sin \theta}}=\sqrt{\frac{1-\sin \theta}{1+\sin \theta} \times \frac{1-\sin \theta}{1-\sin \theta}} \quad \text { [Multiplying and dividing by }(1-\sin \theta) \text { ] } \\
& =\sqrt{\frac{(1-\sin \theta)^{2}}{1-\sin ^{2} \theta}}=\sqrt{\frac{(1-\sin \theta)^{2}}{\cos ^{2} \theta}}=\sqrt{\left(\frac{1-\sin \theta}{\cos \theta}\right)^{2}}=\frac{1-\sin \theta}{\cos \theta}=\frac{1}{\cos \theta}-\frac{\sin \theta}{\cos \theta} \\
& =\sec \theta-\tan \theta=\text { RHS }
\end{aligned}
$$
CASE-III : Proving trigonometric identities by using componendo and dividendo.
Illustration: If $\operatorname{cosec} \theta+\cot \theta=p$ then show that $\cos \theta=\frac{p^{2}-1}{p^{2}+1}$.
Sol. $\operatorname{cosec} \theta+\cot \theta=p \quad \Rightarrow \frac{1+\cos \theta}{\sin \theta}=p$
$\Rightarrow \frac{(1+\cos \theta)^{2}}{\sin ^{2} \theta}=p^{2} \Rightarrow \frac{(1+\cos \theta)^{2}}{1-\cos ^{2} \theta}=p^{2}$
$\Rightarrow \frac{(1+\cos \theta)^{2}}{(1+\cos \theta)(1-\cos \theta)}=p^{2} \Rightarrow \frac{1+\cos \theta}{1-\cos \theta}=\frac{p^{2}}{1}$
Applying componendo and dividendo
$\frac{(1+\cos \theta)-(1-\cos \theta)}{(1+\cos \theta)+(1-\cos \theta)}=\frac{p^{2}-1}{p^{2}+1} \Rightarrow \cos \theta=\frac{p^{2}-1}{p^{2}+1}$.
