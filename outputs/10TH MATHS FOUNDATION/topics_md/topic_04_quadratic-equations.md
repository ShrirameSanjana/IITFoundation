## Zero(es)/Root(s) of Quadratic Equation
A real number $\alpha$ is said to be a root of the quadratic equation $a x^{2}+b x+\mathrm{c}=0$, if $a \alpha^{2}+b \alpha+c=0$.
The zeroes of the quadratic polynomial $a x^{2}+b x+c$ and the roots of the equation $a x^{2}+b x+c=0$ are same.

## Relation Between Zeroes and Coefficient of a Quadratic Equation
If $\alpha$ and $\beta$ are zeroes of the quadratic equation $a x^{2}+b x+c=0$, where $a, b, c$ are real numbers and $a \neq 0$, then
$$
\begin{aligned}
& \alpha+\beta=-\frac{b}{a} \\
& \Rightarrow \text { sum of zeroes }=-\frac{\text { coefficient of } x}{\text { coefficient of } x^{2}} \\
& \alpha \beta=\frac{c}{a} \\
& \Rightarrow \text { product of zeroes }=\frac{\text { constant term }}{\text { coefficient of } x^{2}}
\end{aligned}
$$
Quadratic Equation:
$x^{2}-(\alpha+\beta) x+\alpha \beta=0$.
## QUADRATIC EQUATIONS
A polynomial of any variable $x$ of degree two in standard form $a x^{2}+b x+c(a \neq 0)$, when equated to zero is called a Quadratic Equation of variable $x$. [In standard form of quadratic polynomial, the power of $x$ are in descending order.] Thus, standard form of a Quadratic Equation of variable $x$ is $a x^{2}+b x+c=0$; where $a, b, c$ all are real numbers but $a \neq 0$. Here ' $a$ ' and ' $b$ ' are called coefficient of $x^{2}$ and $x$ respectively. ' $c$ ' is called constant term.
The term "quadratic" comes from the Latin word "quadratus" which mean square. Know
## STANDARD FORM OF A QUADRATIC EQUATION
In the standard form of a quadratic equation $a x^{2}+b x+c=0$,
(i) all the terms (except zero) are in one side of equal sign $(=)$ and zero $(0)$ is in other side of equal sign.
(ii) the power of $x$ are in descending order.
(iii) either or both ' $b$ ' and ' $c$ ' can be equal to zero but $a \neq 0$. Therefore, a quadratic equation may be in the form of $a x^{2}+b x=0, a x^{2}+c=0, a x^{2}=0$ or $x^{2}=0$.
(iv) the term of $x^{2}$ (i.e. $a x^{2}$ ), term of $x$ (i.e. $b x$ ) or constant term (i.e. $c$ ) can not be more than one.
If not so, then by adding/subtracting the like terms or taking the common factor from like terms, we convert the quadratic equation in the standard form, in which there is not more than one term of $x^{2}, x$ and constant term.
For example: Standard form of $x^{2}-4 x+x+2=0$ is $x^{2}-3 x+2=0$
Standard form of $\sqrt{2} x^{2}-x^{2}+5 x+7=0$ is $(\sqrt{2}-1) x^{2}+5 x+7=0$.
Note : Sometimes there are two or more constant terms which can not be added/subtracted or have no any common factor, then both the constant terms are put in to a bracket so that all constant terms become a single constant term.
For example: Standard form of $\sqrt{5} x^{2}+7 x-3+2 \sqrt{7}=0$ is $\sqrt{5} x^{2}+7 x-(3-2 \sqrt{7})=0$
## ILLUSTRATION : 1
Which of the following are quadratic equations?
(i) $x+\frac{3}{x}=x^{2}$
(ii) $x^{2}+2 \sqrt{x}-3=0$
(iii) $3 x^{2}-4 x+2=2 x^{2}-2 x+4$
## SOLUTION :
(i) $\frac{x^{2}+3}{x}=x^{2} \Rightarrow x^{3}-x^{2}-3=0$
This is a polynomial of degree 3 , therefore it is not quadratic equation.
(ii) $x^{2}+2 \sqrt{x}-3=0$ is not a quadratic equation because it contains a term involving $x^{1 / 2}$.
(iii) $3 x^{2}-4 x+2=2 x^{2}-2 x+4 \Rightarrow 3 x^{2}-2 x^{2}-4 x+2 x+2-4=0 \Rightarrow x^{2}-2 x-2=0$
This is the quadratic equation.
## SOLUTION OR ROOTS OF A QUADRATIC EQUATION
The values of $x$ which satisfy the given quadratic equation are called solutions/roots of the given quadratic equation.
For example if $x=\alpha$ is one of solutions of the quadratic equation $a x^{2}+b x+c=0$, then $a \alpha^{2}+b \alpha+c=0$.
A quadratic equation has either two real roots or two not real roots. The two real roots may be equal or different.
To find the solutions (or to find the roots) of a quadratic equation, first we check whether the given quadratic equation is in standard form or not. If not, then first convert the given quadratic equation in standard form, then by using any of the following given three methods, you can solve the given quadratic equation.
Note: A quadratic equation has always two solutions/roots (i.e., two value of the variable ' $x$ ' always satisfy the quadratic equation $a x^{2}+b x+c=0$ )

## Method I : Solution of a Quadratic Equation by Factorisation
Quadratic equation : $a x^{2}+b x+c=0$
By spliting the middle term ' $b x$ ' of L.H.S, factorise the L.H.S $\left(a x^{2}+b x+c\right)$ into linear factors. Then after equating each factor to zero, we find the values of the variable ' $x$ ' of the quadratic equation $a x^{2}+b x+c=0$.
These value of $x$ are the solutions/roots of the given quadratic equation.

## Special Cases
Standard from of the quadratic equation : $a x^{2}+b x+\mathrm{c}=0, a \neq 0$
(i) If $b, c=0$, then $a x^{2}=0, \Rightarrow x^{2}=\frac{0}{a}=0, \Rightarrow x=0,0$
(ii) If $b=0$, then $a x^{2}+c=0, \Rightarrow x^{2}=\frac{-c}{a}, \Rightarrow x= \pm \sqrt{\frac{-c}{a}}$
(iii) If $c=0$, then $a x^{2}+b x=0, \Rightarrow x(a x+b)=0, \Rightarrow x=0,-\frac{b}{a}$

## Method II : Solution of a Quadratic Equation by Completing the Square
Consider the quadratic equation, $a x^{2}+b x+c=0$
(i) Shift constant term ' $c$ ' to the R.H.S. $a x^{2}+b x=-c$
(ii) Divide both sides of the quadratic equation by ' $a$ '. $\frac{a x^{2}+b x}{a}=-\frac{c}{a} \Rightarrow x^{2}+\frac{b}{a} x=-\frac{c}{a}$
(iii) Write coefficient $\frac{b}{a}$ of $x$ as $2\left(\frac{b}{2 a}\right) \Rightarrow x^{2}+2\left(\frac{b}{2 a}\right) x=-\frac{c}{a}$
(iv) Add $\left(\frac{b}{2 a}\right)^{2}$ on both sides of equal sign (=) and completing the whole square on L.H.S.
$$
\Rightarrow x^{2}+2\left(\frac{b}{2 a}\right) x+\left(\frac{b}{2 a}\right)^{2}=\left(\frac{b}{2 a}\right)^{2}-\frac{c}{a} \Rightarrow\left(x+\frac{b}{2 a}\right)^{2}=\frac{b^{2}-4 a c}{4 a^{2}} \Rightarrow\left(x+\frac{b}{2 a}\right)^{2}=\frac{b^{2}-4 a c}{4 a^{2}}
$$
(v) Taking square root on both sides of equal sign (=). $\Rightarrow x+\frac{b}{2 a}= \pm \frac{\sqrt{b^{2}-4 a c}}{2 a}$
(vi) Now, shift $\frac{b}{2 a}$ from L.H.S to R.H.S to get the value of $x . \Rightarrow \quad x=-\frac{b}{2 a} \pm \frac{\sqrt{b^{2}-4 a c}}{2 a} \Rightarrow x=\frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^{2}-4 a c}}{2 a}$

## NATURE OF ROOTS
For the quadratic equation: $a x^{2}+b x+c=0(a \neq 0)$, value of $\left(b^{2}-4 a c\right)$ is called discriminant of the quadratic equation. The value of ( $b^{2}-4 a c$ ) is denoted by $D$.
$\therefore D=b^{2}-4 a c$
The discriminant plays an important role in finding the nature of the roots of the quadratic equation.
(i) If $D=0$, then roots are real and equal.
(ii) If $D>0$, then roots are real and unequal.
(iii) If $D<0$, then roots are not real.

## SUM AND PRODUCT OF ROOTS
If $\alpha$ and $\beta$ are the roots of quadratic equation $a x^{2}+b x+c=0$, such that $\alpha=\frac{-b+\sqrt{D}}{2 a}$ and $\beta=\frac{-b-\sqrt{D}}{2 a}$, then
(i) Sum of roots, $\alpha+\beta=\frac{-b+\sqrt{D}}{2 a}+\frac{-b-\sqrt{D}}{2 a}=\frac{-b+\sqrt{D}-b-\sqrt{D}}{2 a}=-\frac{b}{a}=-\frac{\text { Coefficient of } x}{\text { Coefficient of } x^{2}}$
(ii) Product of roots, $\alpha \beta=\frac{-b+\sqrt{D}}{2 a} \times \frac{-b-\sqrt{D}}{2 a}=\frac{(-b)^{2}-(\sqrt{D})^{2}}{4 a^{2}}=\frac{b^{2}-D}{4 a^{2}}=\frac{b^{2}-b^{2}+4 a c}{4 a^{2}}=\frac{c}{a}=\frac{\text { Constant term }}{\text { Coefficient of } x^{2}}$
For example in equation $3 x^{2}+4 x-5=0$. Sum of roots $=-4 / 3$, Product of roots $=-5 / 3$

Then, the condition for one root common is $\left(c_{1} a_{2}-c_{2} a_{1}\right)^{2}=\left(b_{1} c_{2}-b_{2} c_{1}\right)\left(a_{1} b_{2}-a_{2} b_{1}\right)$
Condition for both roots are common, is $\frac{a_{1}}{a_{2}}=\frac{b_{1}}{b_{2}}=\frac{c_{1}}{c_{2}}$
## RELATION BETWEEN ROOTS AND COEFFICIENTS
If roots of quadratic equation $a x^{2}+b x+c=0(a \neq 0)$ are $\alpha$ and $\beta$, then $\alpha+\beta=-\frac{b}{a}, \alpha \cdot \beta=\frac{c}{a}$
Now,
(i) $\quad \alpha-\beta=\sqrt{(\alpha-\beta)^{2}}=\sqrt{(\alpha+\beta)^{2}-4 \alpha \beta}=\sqrt{\left(\frac{-b}{a}\right)^{2}-4 \frac{c}{a}}=\frac{\sqrt{b^{2}-4 a c}}{a}=\frac{\sqrt{D}}{a}$
(ii) $\alpha^{2}+\beta^{2}=(\alpha+\beta)^{2}-2 \alpha \beta=\frac{b^{2}-2 a c}{a^{2}}=\left(-\frac{b}{a}\right)^{2}-2 \frac{c}{a}$
(iii) $\alpha^{2}-\beta^{2}=(\alpha+\beta)(\alpha-\beta)=(\alpha+\beta) \sqrt{(\alpha-\beta)^{2}}=(\alpha+\beta) \sqrt{(\alpha+\beta)^{2}-4 \alpha \beta}=-\frac{b}{a} \sqrt{\left(-\frac{b}{a}\right)^{2}-4 \frac{c}{a}}=-\frac{b \sqrt{b^{2}-4 a c}}{a^{2}}=\frac{-b \sqrt{D}}{a^{2}}$
(iv) $\alpha^{2}+\alpha \beta+\beta^{2}=(\alpha+\beta)^{2}-\alpha \beta=\left(-\frac{b}{a}\right)^{2}-\frac{c}{a}=\frac{b^{2}-a c}{a^{2}}$
(v) $\frac{\alpha}{\beta}+\frac{\beta}{\alpha}=\frac{\alpha^{2}+\beta^{2}}{\alpha \beta}=\frac{(\alpha+\beta)^{2}-2 \alpha \beta}{\alpha \beta}=\frac{\left(-\frac{b}{a}\right)^{2}-2 \frac{c}{a}}{\frac{c}{a}}=\frac{b^{2}-2 a c}{c a}$

## SEQUENCE
The number patterns or arrangement of numbers according to definite rule or a set of rules is called a SEQUENCE.
The various numbers occuring in a sequence are called its terms. The $n^{\text {th }}$ term of the sequence is denoted by $t_{\mathrm{n}}$. The $n^{\text {th }}$ term is also called the GENERAL TERM of the sequence. For example,
(i) The numbers $\langle 1,4,9,16, \ldots$.$\rangle represent a sequence written according to the rule t_{n}=n^{2}, n \in \mathbf{N}$.
(ii) The numbers $\left\langle\frac{1}{2}, \frac{2}{3}, \frac{3}{4}, \frac{4}{5}, \ldots \ldots \ldots ..\right\rangle$ represent a sequence written according to the rule $t_{n}=\frac{n}{n+1}, n \in \mathbf{N}$.
(iii) The numbers $\langle 1,3,5,7, \ldots \ldots \ldots$.$\rangle represent a sequence written according to the rule t_{n}=2 n-1, n \in \mathbf{N}$.
(iv) The numbers $\langle 1,3,7,13,21 \ldots \ldots \ldots\rangle$ represent a sequence written according to the rule $t_{n}=n^{2}-n+1, n \in \mathbf{N}$.
(v) The numbers $\langle 1,1,2,3,5,8, \ldots \ldots \ldots$.$\rangle represent a sequence written according to the following set of rules t_{1}=t_{2}=1, t_{n}=t_{n-1}=t_{n-2}, n>2, n \in \mathbf{N}$. This sequence of numbers is called the Fibonacci sequence.
(vi) The numbers $\langle 2,3,5,7,11,13, \ldots \ldots \ldots .$. represent a sequence of prime numbers.
Note that in every sequence it is not always possible to write a specific formula.
## METHODS OF DESCRIBING A SEQUENCE
(i) A sequence may be described by writing first few terms till the rule for writing down the other terms is evident.
(ii) A sequence may be described by giving a formula for its general term (the $n^{\text {th }}$ term).
(iii) A sequence may be described by specifying first few terms and a formula (or a set of formulae) giving a relation between successive terms. Such a formula is called RECURSIVE FORMULA (or RECURRENCE RELATION).
(iv) Some sequences may not be described by any rule.
## SERIES
If $\left\langle x_{1}, x_{2}, x_{3}, \ldots \ldots \ldots.\right\rangle$ is a sequence, then the expression $x_{1}+x_{2}+x_{3}+\ldots$. is called the series associated with the given sequence.
## PROGRESSION
A sequence is said to be a PROGRESSION if its terms numerically increase or numerically decrease continuously.
