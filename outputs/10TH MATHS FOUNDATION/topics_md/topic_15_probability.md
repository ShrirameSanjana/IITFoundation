## MEASUREMENT OF PROBABILITY
- Experimental (Empirical) Approach of Probability
(i) A trial is an action which results in one or several outcomes.
(ii) The probability $\mathrm{P}(\mathrm{E})$ of happening an event E , is given by
$$
\mathrm{P}(\mathrm{E})=\frac{\text { Number of trials in which the event happened }}{\text { The total number of trials }}
$$
Note: The empirical probability depends on the number of trials.
## A Theoritical Approach (Classical Probability) of Probability
If an event ' A' happen in ' $m$ ' ways and does not happen in ' $n$ ' ways, then the probability of occurrence of event ' $A$ ', denoted by $P(A)$ is given by,
$$
P(A)=\frac{\text { Number of favourable outcomes of the event }}{\text { Number of all possible outcomes of the experiment }}=\frac{m}{m+n}
$$
and probability of not happening this event ' A ' is given by $P(\bar{A})$,
$$
P(\bar{A})=\frac{\text { Number of unfavourable outcomes }}{\text { Number of all possible outcomes }}=\frac{n}{m+n}
$$
The basic difference between experimental and theoretical approaches probability is that, in the experimental approach, the probability of an event is based on what has been actually happened while in theoretical approach, we predict what will happen without actually performing the experiments.

## INDEPENDENT EVENTS
Two or more events are said to be independent if the happening (or non-happening) of one event is not affected by the happening or non-happening of others.
- For any two Independent events $A$ and $B, P(A$ and $B)=P(A) \times P(B)$
- Also, if $P(A$ and $B)=P(A) \times P(B)$, then two events $A$ and $B$ are called independent events.

## ALGEBRA OF EVENTS
Let A and B be two events related to a random experiment. We define
(i) The event " $A$ or $B$ " denoted by " $A \cup B$ ", which occurs when $A$ or $B$ or both occur.
Thus, the event " $A$ and $B$ ", denoted by " $A \cap B$ ", which occurs when $A$ and $B$ both occur.
$P(A \cup B)=$ Probability that at least one of the two events occur $=P(A)+P(B)-P(A \cap B)$.
(ii) The event "Not $A$ " denoted by $\bar{A}$, occurs when and only when A does not occur. Thus
$P(\bar{A})=$ Probability of non-occurrence of the event $A=1-P(A)$.
(iii) $\bar{A} \cap \bar{B}$ denotes the "non-occurrence of both $A$ and $B$ " or "neither $A$ nor $B$ ".
$P(\bar{A} \cap \bar{B})=P(\overline{A \cup B})=1-P(A \cup B)$.
(iv) $\bar{A} \cup \bar{B}$ denotes the "non-occurence of both A and B " or "not A or not B ".
$P(\bar{A} \cup \bar{B})=P(\overline{A \cap B})=1-P(A \cap B)$.

## MUTUALLY EXCLUSIVE EVENTS
If two events cannot occur simultaneously, then they are mutually exclusive. If $A$ and $B$ are mutually exclusive events, then $A \cap B=\phi$.
Ex. In drawing a card from a well shuffled pack of 52 cards, consider the following events:
$A=$ the card is a spade and $B=$ the card is a heart.
The two events $A$ and $B$ are mutually exclusive. Because, both have no common card.
Again, consider other events
(a) The card is a heart. (b) The card is a king.
These two events are not mutually exclusive. Because, the card can be king of heart.
## ADDITION THEOREM OF PROBABILITY FOR MUTUALLY EXCLUSIVE EVENTS
If A and B are two events associated with a random experiment, then $P(A \cup B)=P(A)+P(B)-P(A \cap B)$
If $A$ and $B$ are mutually exclusive events then $P(A \cap B)=0$
$\therefore P(A \cup B)=P(A)+P(B)$

## GEOMETRIC PROBABILITY
In geometric probability, formula for finding the probability $P$ of occurrence of an event is given by
$$
P=\frac{\text { Measure of the specified part of the region }}{\text { Measure of the whole region }}
$$
Here 'Measure' means may be length, area or volume of the region (or space) which is decided according to the given question.
