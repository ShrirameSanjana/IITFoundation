## 10. Circles
Foundation
## (CONCEPT MAP)
## CIRCLE
A circle is the set of all points in a plane at a fixed distance from a fixed point in the plane. The fixed point is called the centre of the circle. The fixed distance is called the radius of the circle.
Usually a circle is named by its centre and radius. A circle with centre ' $O$ ' and radius ' $r$ ' is named as circle ( $O, r$ ).
OR
A circle is the locus of a point which moves in a plane in such a way that its distance from a given fixed point in the plane is always constant.
## CONGRUENT AND CONCENTRIC CIRCLES
We say that two circles are congruent if they have the same radius.
Two or more circles with the same centre are called concentric circles.
In the figure, circles $(O, p),(O, q),(O, r)$ are three concentric circles.
## CYCLIC QUADRILATERAL
If all vertices of a quadrilateral lie on a circle, then it is called a cyclic quadrilateral.
Remark : Sum of opposite angles of a cyclic quadrilateral is $180^{\circ}$.
In cyclic quadrilateral $A B C D, \angle A+\angle C=180^{\circ}$ and $\angle B+\angle D=180^{\circ}$
## Learn More
An exterior angle of a cyclic quadrilateral is equal to its opposite interior angle. In cyclic quadrilateral $A B C D, \angle C B E=\angle A D C$
## TERMS RELATED TO A CIRCLE
- A line segment that connects two distinct points on a circle is called a chord.
- A chord that passes through the centre of a circle is called a diameter. Diameter is the longest chord of a circle.
- A line that intersects the circle at exactly two points is called a secant (line).
- A tangent to a circle is a straight line which touches the circle at only one point. The point where the tangent touches the circle is called the point of contact of the tangent to the circle. In the figure, $P$ is the point of contact of the tangent to the circle.
## Learn More
Segments : The chord of a circle divide it into two parts. Each part is called a segment. Smaller part is called minor segment and larger part is called major segment.
Alternate segment and its angle: The segment of a circle opposite to the angle formed by a tangent and a chord through the point of contact is called the alternate segment for that angle.
Theorem: An angle between a tangent and a chord through the point of contact is equal to the angle in the alternate segment.
In the figure, $\angle \mathrm{APC}=\angle \mathrm{CDP}$ and $\angle \mathrm{BPD}=\angle \mathrm{DCP}$

## NUMBER OF TANGENTS FROM A POINT 'P' TO A CIRCLE
## Case I : When point ' $\boldsymbol{P}$ ' lies outside the circle
Only two tangents can be drawn from an exterior point to a circle. In the figure, $P Q$ and $P R$ are the two tangents from an exterior point $P$ to a circle.
## Case II : When point ' $P$ ' lies on the circle
Only one tangent can be drawn from a point lying on the circle.
## Case III : When point ' $\mathbf{P}$ ' lies inside the circle
No tangent can be drawn from a point lying inside the circle.
## LENGTH OF A TANGENT
Length of the segment of the tangent from the external point ' P ' and the point of contact with the circle is called the length of the tangent from the point ' P ' to the circle.
## Theorem 1:
Statement : The tangent at any point of a circle is perpendicular to the radius through the point of contact.
Given : PQ is a tangent of a circle at a point A and O is the centre of the circle.
To prove : $\mathrm{OA} \perp \mathrm{PQ}$
Construction : Take any point B on PQ . Join OB .
Proof : Distance of centre from any point on tangent other than the point of contact is greater than its distance from point of contact. Since, point B is outside the circle. Therefore, $\mathrm{OB}>\mathrm{OA}$
Hence, out of all the line segments joining the centre O to the points on the tangent, OA is the least.
Since perpendicular from a point O on the tangent PQ is least of all line segments joining the centre to the points on the tangent. Hence $\mathrm{OA} \perp \mathrm{PQ}$.
Note : Converse of Theorem-1 is also true.
## Remarks :
The line containing the radius through the point of contact is called the 'normal' to the circle at the point. Normal to a circle at the point of contact of a tangent to the circle is perpendicular to the tangent.
## Theorem 2:
Statement : The lengths of two tangents drawn from an external point to a circle are equal.
Given : O is the centre of the circle. RP and RQ are two tangents drawn from external point R to the circle.
To prove: $\mathrm{RP}=\mathrm{RQ}$
Construction : Join OP, OQ and OR.
Proof: We know that the tangent at any point of a circle is perpendicular to the radius through the point of contact.
$$
\angle \mathrm{OPR}=\angle \mathrm{OQR}=90^{\circ}
$$
In $\triangle \mathrm{OPR}$ and $\triangle \mathrm{OQR}$,
$$
\begin{aligned}
& \angle \mathrm{OPR}=\angle \mathrm{OQR}=90^{\circ}[\mathrm{By}(\mathrm{i})] \\
& \mathrm{OR}=\mathrm{OR} \quad(\text { Common sides }) \\
& \mathrm{OP}=\mathrm{OQ} \quad(\text { Radii of the same circle })
\end{aligned}
$$
So, from right angle-hypotenuse-side congruency
$$
\Delta \mathrm{OPR} \cong \triangle \mathrm{OQR}
$$
Since corresponding sides of two congruent triangles are equal.
$\therefore \mathrm{RP}=\mathrm{RQ}$
Corollary : Centre of the circle lies on the bisector of the angle between the two tangent drawn from an exterior point.
When two chords intersect each other inside a circle, the products of their segments are equal i.e. $\boldsymbol{A P} \times \boldsymbol{P B}=\boldsymbol{C P} \times \boldsymbol{P D}$. It is known as intersecting chord theorem.
The product of the intercepts of two secants from an external point are equal. i.e. $P A \times P B=P C \times P D$. It is known as intersecting secants theorem.

## COMMON TANGENTS
- If a line is tangent to two circles in the same plane, then the line is called a common tangent to the circles.
- If the two circles are on the same side of the tangent, then the common tangent is called Direct common tangent.
- If the two circles are on the opposite side of the tangent, then the common tangent is called Indirect (or transverse) common tangent.
In the figure (i), AB is the Direct common tangent and in figure (ii) AB is the Indirect (or transverse) common tangent.

## CONNECTING TOPIC
Consider two circles with centres $\mathrm{C}_{1}$ and $\mathrm{C}_{2}$ and radii $\mathrm{r}_{1}$ and $\mathrm{r}_{2}$.
Case I: If $C_{1} C_{2}>r_{1}+r_{2}$, i.e. the distance between centres $>$ the sum of radii.
Then, the circles neither touch nor intersect and one circle is out of other.
Case II : If $\mathrm{C}_{1} \mathrm{C}_{2}=\mathrm{r}_{1}+\mathrm{r}_{2}$. Then, the circles touch one another externally.
Case III : If $r_{2}-r_{1}<\mathrm{C}_{1} \mathrm{C}_{2}<r_{1}+r_{2}$. Then, the circles intersect at two distinct points. [Assuming $r_{2}>r_{1}$ ].
$$
r_{2}-r_{1}<C_{1} C_{2}<r_{1}+r_{2}
$$
Case IV : If $C_{1} C_{2}=r_{2}-r_{1}$. Then, the circle touch one another internally.
Case V : If $C_{1} C_{2}<r_{2}-r_{1}$. Then, the smaller circle completely lies inside the bigger circle.
## Incircle :
The largest circle that will fit inside the triangle i.e., the circle which touches all sides of a triangle is called incircle or inscribed circle and centre is called the triangle's incentre. Clearly, each side of the triangle is a tangent to the circle. Thus $A B, B C$ and $C A$ are tangents to the circle. Radius through the point of contact is perpendicular to the tangent.
$\therefore O D \perp A B, O E \perp B C$ and $O F \perp A C$.
Also, lengths of tangents drawn from an external point to a circle are equal.
$\therefore A D=A F, B D=B E$ and $C E=C F$.
Let us understand the above concepts in a better way in different cases through the illustrative examples.
