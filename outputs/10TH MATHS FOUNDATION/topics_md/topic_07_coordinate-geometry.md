## PLOTTING OF A POINT WHOSE COORDINATES ARE KNOWN
The point can be plotted by measuring its proper distances from both the axes. Thus, any point $(h, k)$ can be plotted as follows :
(i) Measure $O M$ equal to h along the $X$-axis.
(ii) Now measure $M P$ perpendicular to $O M$ equal to $k$.
The method of describing the location of points in a plane was proposed by the great French mathematician Rene' Descartes in 17th century. Rene' Descartes' Latinized name is Renatius Cartesius and from here the terminology "Cartisian plane" and "Cartisian coordinate system" are derived.
## DISTANCE BETWEEN TWO POINTS
Distance between any two points $P\left(x_{1}, y_{1}\right)$ and $Q\left(x_{2}, y_{2}\right)$ in the plane is the length of the line segment $P Q$.
From $P$ and $Q$ draw perpendiculars $P L$ and $Q M$ respectively on the $X$-axis and $P R$ perpendicular on $Q M$.
Then, $O L=x_{1}, O M=x_{2}, P L=y_{1}$ and $Q M=y_{2}$
$\therefore P R=L M=O M-O L=x_{2}-x_{1}$
$Q R=Q M-R M=Q M-P L=y_{2}-y_{1}$
Since $P R Q$ is a right angled triangle,
$\therefore \quad P Q^{2}=P R^{2}+Q R^{2}$
$$
=\left(x_{2}-x_{1}\right)^{2}+\left(y_{2}-y_{1}\right)^{2}
$$
(By the Pythagoras Theorem)
$\therefore P Q=\sqrt{\left(x_{2}-x_{1}\right)^{2}+\left(y_{2}-y_{1}\right)^{2}}$
i.e., Distance between any two points $=\sqrt{(\text { difference of abscissae })^{2}+(\text { difference of ordinates })^{2}}$
Corollary : The distance of the point $\left(x_{1}, y_{1}\right)$ from the origin $(0,0)$ is $\sqrt{\left(x_{1}-0\right)^{2}+\left(y_{1}-0\right)^{2}}=\sqrt{x_{1}^{2}+y_{1}^{2}}$

## APPLICATIONS OF DISTANCE FORMULA
(i) For given three points $A, B, C$ to decide whether they are collinear or vertices of a particular triangle. First we find the length of $A B, B C$ and $C A$ then we shall find that the point are
(a) Collinear, if the sum of two shorter distances is equal to the longest distance
(b) Vertices of an equilateral triangle if $A B=B C=C A$
(c) Vertices of an isosceles triangle if $A B=B C$ or $B C=C A$ or $C A=A B$
(d) Vertices of a right angled triangle if $A B^{2}+B C^{2}=C A^{2}$ or $B C^{2}+C A^{2}=A B^{2}$ or $C A^{2}+A B^{2}=B C^{2}$

(c) $A B=C D, B C=D A ; A C=B D \Rightarrow A B C D$ is a rectangle.

## SECTION FORMULA
To find the co-ordinates of a point, which divides a line segment joining two points internally in a given ratio :
Let $A\left(x_{1}, y_{1}\right)$ and $B\left(x_{2}, y_{2}\right)$ be the two given points and $P$ be a point on $A B$, which divides it internally in the given ratio $\mathrm{m}: \mathrm{n}$ i.e., $A P: P B=\mathrm{m}: \mathrm{n}$.
We have to find the co-ordinates of P . Let $\mathrm{P}=(x, y)$.
Draw the perpendiculars $A L, P M, B N$ from $\mathrm{A}, \mathrm{P}$ and B respectively on $x$-axis and $A K, P T$ from A and P on $P M$ and $B N$ respectively. Then, from similar triangles AKP and PTB, we have, $\frac{A P}{P B}=\frac{A K}{P T}=\frac{P K}{B T}$
Now, $\quad A K=L M=O M-O L=x-x_{1}$
$$
\begin{aligned}
& P T=M N=O N-O M=x_{2}-x \\
& P K=M P-M K=M P-L A=y-y_{1} \\
& B T=N B-N T=N B-M P=y_{2}-y
\end{aligned}
$$
From (i), we have, $\frac{m}{n}=\frac{x-x_{1}}{x_{2}-x}=\frac{y-y_{1}}{y_{2}-y}$
Now, $\frac{m}{n}=\frac{x-x_{1}}{x_{2}-x}$
$\Rightarrow m x_{2}-m x=n x-n x_{1}$
$\Rightarrow x(m+n)=m x_{2}+n x_{1}$
$\therefore \quad x=\frac{m x_{2}+n x_{1}}{m+n}$
Now, $\frac{m}{n}=\frac{y-y_{1}}{y_{2}-y} \quad \Rightarrow y=\frac{m y_{2}+n y_{1}}{m+n}$
Hence, $\quad x=\frac{m x_{2}+n x_{1}}{m+n}$ and $y=\frac{m y_{2}+n y_{1}}{m+n}$
Therefore, co-ordinates of a point which divides the line segment joining the points $\left(x_{1}, y_{1}\right)$ and $\left(x_{2}, y_{2}\right)$ in the ratio $m: n$ internally is $\left(\frac{m x_{2}+n x_{1}}{m+n}, \frac{m y_{2}+n y_{1}}{m+n}\right)$
The coordinates of a point $C$ which divides line segment joining two points $A$ and $B$ externally in the ratio $m: n$ are $\left(\frac{m x_{2}-n x_{1}}{m-n} \cdot \frac{m y_{2}-n y_{1}}{m-n}\right)$

## POINTS OF TRISECTION
Trisection means a line is dividing into three equal parts.
This can be done by finding two points $C$ and $D$ on the line segment $A B$, such that $A C=C D=D B$
Let us assume $A C=C D=D B=p$
Then $A C=p$ and $C B=C D+D B=p+p=2 p$
$$
\therefore \quad A C: C B=p: 2 p=1: 2
$$
Also, $A D=A C+C D=p+p=2 p$ and $D B=p$
$\therefore \quad A D: D B=2 p: p=2: 1$
Hence, we can conclude that, to find points of trisection, we find two points $C$ and $D$ which divides AB in the ratio $1: 2$ and $2: 1$, respectively.

## 1. Centroid of a Triangle
The centroid G is the point of intersection of medians $\mathrm{AD}, \mathrm{BE}$ and CF , which are the lines joining a vertex with the mid-point of opposite side.
The coordinates of the centroid G are $\left(\frac{x_{1}+x_{2}+x_{3}}{3}, \frac{y_{1}+y_{2}+y_{3}}{3}\right)$.
where $\mathrm{A}\left(x_{1}, y_{1}\right), \mathrm{B}\left(x_{2}, y_{2}\right)$ and $\mathrm{C}\left(x_{3}, y_{3}\right)$
## 2. Incentre of the Triangle
The incentre I is the centre of circle touching the sides of the triangle internally, called INCIRCLE and it is the point of intersection of the internal bisectors. AP, BQ, CR of the vertex angles.
The coordinates of incentre I are given by
$\left(\frac{a x_{1}+b x_{2}+c x_{3}}{a+b+c}, \frac{a y_{1}+b y_{2}+c y_{3}}{a+b+c}\right)$,
where $a=\mathrm{BC}, b=\mathrm{CA}$ and $c=\mathrm{AB}$ are the sides of the triangle.
3. Circumcentre of the Triangle
The circumcentre $\mathrm{O}^{\prime}$ is the centre of the circle passing through the vertices of triangle and it is the point of intersection of perpendicular bisectors of the sides of triangle.
Thus, we have $\mathrm{O}^{\prime} \mathrm{A}=\mathrm{O}^{\prime} \mathrm{B}=\mathrm{O}^{\prime} \mathrm{C}$
Circumcentre of a right angled triangle is the mid points of its hypotenuse.
4. Orthocentre: It is the point of intersection of perpendicular drawn from vertices on opposite sides of a triangle.
Remarks: (i) If the triangle is equilateral, the centroid, incentre, orthocentre, circumcentre coincides.
(ii) Orthocentre, centroid and cirumcentre are always collinear.
(iii) In an isosceles triangle, centroid, orthocentre, incentre, circumcentre lies on the same line.
## AREA OF A TRIANGLE
Let $A B C$ be any triangle whose vertices are $A\left(x_{1}, y_{1}\right), B\left(x_{2}, y_{2}\right)$ and $C\left(x_{3}, y_{3}\right)$. Draw $B L, A M$ and $C N$ perpendiculars from $B, A$ and $C$ respectively on the $X$-axis. $A B L M, A M N C$ and $B L N C$ are all trapeziums.
Area of $\triangle A B C=$ Area of trapezium $A B L M+$ Area of trapezium $A M N C-$ Area of trapezium $B L N C$
We know that, Area of trapezium
$$
=\frac{1}{2} \times(\text { Sum of parallel sides }) \times(\text { Distance between the paralleled sides })
$$
Therefore, area of $\triangle A B C=\frac{1}{2}(B L+A M)(L M)+\frac{1}{2}(A M+C N)(M N)-\frac{1}{2}(B L+C N)(L N)$
$$
\begin{aligned}
& =\frac{1}{2}\left(y_{2}+y_{1}\right)\left(x_{1}-x_{2}\right)+\frac{1}{2}\left(y_{1}+y_{3}\right)\left(x_{3}-x_{1}\right)-\frac{1}{2}\left(y_{2}+y_{3}\right)\left(x_{3}-x_{2}\right) \\
& =\frac{1}{2}\left[x_{1}\left(y_{2}-y_{3}\right)+x_{2}\left(y_{3}-y_{1}\right)+x_{3}\left(y_{1}-y_{2}\right)\right]
\end{aligned}
$$
Since area of a triangle is always positive, therefore we reject the negative sign if appear in the value of area of the triangle.
Note: (i) Area of triangle $=\frac{1}{2} \times$ Base × Altitude
(ii) Area of polygon can be calculated by dividing it into the triangular region.
(iii) If area of triangle formed by joining three points is zero, then points are collinear.
