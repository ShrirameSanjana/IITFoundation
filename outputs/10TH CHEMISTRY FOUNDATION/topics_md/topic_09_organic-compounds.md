## ALKANES
Alkanes or saturated hydrocarbons are those in which all the four valencies of carbon atom(s) are satisfied by four atoms or groups i.e. the carbon valencies are fully satisfied. These compounds contain only $\mathrm{C}-\mathrm{C}$ and $\mathrm{C}-\mathrm{H}$ types of covalent bonds. Since they are relatively inert towards most of the chemical reagents under ordinary conditions, they are called paraffins (Latin-parum, little; affinis-affinity). The IUPAC name for these compounds is alkanes. Their general formula is $\mathrm{C}_{n} \mathrm{H}_{2 n+2}$. Alkanes are quite stable compounds and give substitution reactions while alkenes and alkynes are more reactive and generally give addition reactions.

## Physical Properties of Alkanes
(a) Alkanes from $\mathrm{C}_{1}-\mathrm{C}_{4}$ are colourless, odourless gases. $\mathrm{C}_{5}-\mathrm{C}_{17}$ are colourless liquids, $\mathrm{C}_{18}-$ onwards are waxy, white solids.
## (b) Boiling points
Boiling points of alkanes are lowest of all the groups of organic compounds. The forces of attraction among alkane molecules are weakest van der Waal's forces of attraction. With the increase in the number of carbon chain length (molecular size), the magnitude of van der Waal's forces also increases.
Among isomeric alkanes, boiling point decreases with increasing branching.
Example : n-Pentane > iso-Pentane > neo-Pentane
## (c) Melting point
The melting point of alkanes does not show regular variation with increase in molecular size. Unbranched alkanes containing 2, 4, 6, 8 etc. carbon atoms have higher melting points than the unbranched alkanes containing 3, 5, 7, 9 etc. carbon atoms respectively.

## ALKENES
Alkenes are those unsaturated hydrocarbons which contain at least one carbon-carbon double bond in their molecules. Their general formula is $\mathrm{C}_{\mathrm{n}} \mathrm{H}_{2 \mathrm{n}}$ and hence they contain two hydrogen atoms less than the corresponding alkane and hence designated as unsaturated hydrocarbon having general formula $\mathrm{C}_{n} \mathrm{H}_{2 n+2}$. These are also known as olefins or olifines (olifiant $=$ oil forming) and their IUPAC name is alkenes.
The carbon-carbon double bond ( $C=C$ ), commonly known as ethylenic or olifinic bond or linkage, is the characteristic group of alkenes.

## ALKYL HALIDES
Alkyl halides (haloalkanes) are those compounds in which one or more hydrogen atoms in an alkane are replaced by halogen atoms.
Halogen derivatives of alkanes, alkenes and alkynes are known as alkyl halides (haloalkanes), alkenyl halides (haloalkenes) and alkynyl halides (haloalkynes) respectively.
On the basis of the number of halogen atom, alkyl halides are further classified as mono-, di-, tri-,
Did You Know? poly- and per-halohydrocarbons. The word perhalohydrocarbon means all the hydrogen atoms of the hydrocarbon are replaced by corresponding number of halogen atoms.
Besides the nature and number of halogen atoms, alkyl halides may be classified into primary $\left(1^{\circ}\right)$, secondary $\left(2^{\circ}\right)$ and tertiary $\left(3^{\circ}\right)$ according to the nature of the carbon atom bearing halogen.

(b) Boiling points
Greater the molecular mass, stronger the van der Waal's forces of attraction and hence higher is the boiling point.
(i) $\mathrm{RI}>\mathrm{RBr}>\mathrm{RCl}>\mathrm{RF}$ (For same alkyl group)
(ii) Boiling point decreases as the size of alkyl group decreases.
$$
\mathrm{CH}_{3}\left(\mathrm{CH}_{2}\right)_{2} \mathrm{CH}_{2} \mathrm{X}>\mathrm{CH}_{3} \mathrm{CH}_{2} \mathrm{CH}_{2} \mathrm{X}>\mathrm{CH}_{3} \mathrm{CH}_{2} \mathrm{X} \text { [For the same halogen atom] }
$$
(c) Stability order
Stability of alkyl halide decreases as the strength of the $\mathrm{C}-\mathrm{X}$ bond decreases.
$$
\mathrm{R}-\mathrm{F}>\mathrm{R}-\mathrm{Cl}>\mathrm{R}-\mathrm{Br}>\mathrm{R}-\mathrm{I}
$$

## ETHERS
Ethers are a class of compounds which contain an oxygen atom bonded to two alkyl groups (same or different). When both the alkyl groups are same they are designated as simple or symmetrical ethers and when both are different, they are called mixed or unsymmetrical ethers.
$$
\begin{aligned}
& \text { e.g. } \\
& \qquad \begin{array}{ll}
\mathrm{R}-\mathrm{O}-\mathrm{R} & \mathrm{R}-\mathrm{O}-\mathrm{R} \prime \\
\mathrm{CH}_{3}-\mathrm{O}-\mathrm{CH}_{3} & \mathrm{CH}_{3}-\mathrm{O}-\mathrm{C}_{2} \mathrm{H}_{5} \\
\mathrm{C}_{2} \mathrm{H}_{5}-\mathrm{O}-\mathrm{C}_{2} \mathrm{H}_{5} & \mathrm{CH}_{3}-\mathrm{O}-\mathrm{C}_{3} \mathrm{H}_{7} \\
\text { Simple ethers } & \text { Mixed ethers }
\end{array}
\end{aligned}
$$
Ethers form a homologous series with the general formula $\mathrm{C}_{n} \mathrm{H}_{2 n+2} \mathrm{O}$ where the value of ' $n$ ' is always more than 1 ; the functional group of ether is (- - - ). Ethers may be regarded as anhydrides of alcohol as they may be obtained by elimination of water molecule from two alcohol molecules.
Just as alcohols are considered as monoalkyl derivatives of water, ethers may be considered as dialkyl derivatives of water.

## Physical Properties of Ethers
(a) The first two members, i.e., dimethyl ether and methylethyl ether are gases; while all others are colourless, volatile pleasant smelling liquids. Among aromatic ethers anisole $\left(\mathrm{C}_{6} \mathrm{H}_{5} \mathrm{OCH}_{3}\right)$ and phenetole $\left(\mathrm{C}_{6} \mathrm{H}_{5} \mathrm{OC}_{2} \mathrm{H}_{5}\right)$ are liquids; diphenyl ether is solid at room temperature.
(b) Lower ethers are highly volatile and inflammable and also act as anaesthetics.
(c) They are sparingly soluble in water, but readily soluble in organic solvents such as benzene, chloroform, etc.
## Chemical Properties of Ethers
(a) Properties due to alkyl groups
(i) Halogenation : When ethers are treated with chlorine or bromine in dark, substitution reaction occurs at the $\alpha$-carbon atom. The extent of substitution depends upon the reaction conditions.
(ii) Combustion : Since ethers are volatile and highly inflammable, they burn in air to form $\mathrm{CO}_{2}$ and water.
$$
\mathrm{C}_{2} \mathrm{H}_{5}-\mathrm{O}-\mathrm{C}_{2} \mathrm{H}_{5}+6 \mathrm{O}_{2} \longrightarrow 4 \mathrm{CO}_{2}+5 \mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}
$$
## (b) Properties due to ethereal oxygen
(i) Chemical inertness : Since ethers do not have any active group in their molecules, so they do not react with active metals like Na , strong bases like NaOH , reducing or oxidising agents.
(ii) Basic nature : Owing to the presence of unshared electron pairs on oxygen, ethers behave as Lewis bases. Hence they dissolve in strong acids (e.g., conc. HCl , conc. $\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{SO}_{4}$ ) at low temperature to form oxonium salts.

## CARBOXYLIC ACIDS
The compounds containing the carboxyl functional group $-\mathrm{C}-\mathrm{OH}$ are called carboxylic acids. The word carboxyl is a combination of two words carbonyl ( $>\mathrm{C}=\mathrm{O}$ ) and hydroxyl ( -OH ).
## Classification of Carboxylic Acids
Depending upon the number of -COOH groups, they are classified as (i) monocarboxylic acids: containing one -COOH group (ii) dicarboxylic acids-containing two -COOH groups and so on.
Fatty acids: Aliphatic monocarboxylic acids are commonly called fatty acids because higher members are obtained by the hydrolysis of oils and fats.
