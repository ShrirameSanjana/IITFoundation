## THEORIES OF ACIDS AND BASES
## Arrhenius Concept of Acids and Bases
Svante Arrhenius in 1887, stated that acid ionizes in aqueous solution to produce hydrogen ions (which are protons), $\mathrm{H}^{+}$, and anions; and a base ionizes in aqueous solution to produce hydroxide ions $\left(\mathrm{OH}^{-}\right)$and cations. Later studies of aqueous solutions provided evidence of a small, positively charged hydrogen ion combining with a water molecule to form a hydrated proton, $\mathrm{H}^{+}\left(\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}\right)$ or $\mathrm{H}_{3} \mathrm{O}^{+}$, which is called the hydronium ion.
$$
\mathrm{H}^{+}+\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O} \longrightarrow \mathrm{H}_{3} \mathrm{O}^{+}
$$
Often, the hydronium ion or hydrated proton is represented as $\mathrm{H}^{+}(\mathrm{aq})$. Hydrogen chloride $(\mathrm{HCl})$, a gas, is an acid because it dissolves in water to yield hydrogen ions and chloride ions. This aqueous solution of HCl is referred to as hydrochloric acid.
$$
\mathrm{HCl}+\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O} \longrightarrow \mathrm{H}^{+}(\mathrm{aq})+\mathrm{Cl}^{-}(\mathrm{aq})
$$
## Problems with Arrhenius theory :
(i) The solvent has no role to play in Arrhenius theory. An acid is expected to be an acid in any solvent. This was found not to be the case. For example, HCl is an acid in water, behaving in the manner Arrhenius expected. However, if HCl is dissolved in benzene, there is no dissociation, the HCl remaining as undissociated molecules. The nature of the solvent plays a critical role in acid-base properties of substances.
(ii) All salts in Arrhenius theory should produce solutions that are neither acidic nor basic. This is not the case. If equal amounts of HCl and ammonia react, the solution is slightly acidic. If equal amounts of acetic acid and sodium hydroxide are reacted, the resulting solution is basic. Arrhenius had no explanation for this.
(iii) The need for hydroxide as the base led Arrhenius to propose the formula $\mathrm{NH}_{4} \mathrm{OH}$ as the formula for ammonia in water. This led to the misconception that $\mathrm{NH}_{4} \mathrm{OH}$ is the actual base, not $\mathrm{NH}_{3}$.
The Arrhenius theory of acids and bases will be fully supplanted by the theory proposed independently by Johnnies Bronsted and Thomas Lowry in 1923.
## Bronsted Lowry Theory of Acids and Bases
By the definition of Thomas Lowry and J.N. Bronsted working independently in 1923, "an acid is a material that donates a proton and a base is a material that can accept a proton".
Consider the example of ionization of hydrochloric acid in water. $\mathrm{HCl}(\mathrm{aq})$ acts as an acid by donating a proton to $\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}$ molecule which acts as a base.
Here, HCl act as an acid, because it has a proton available to be transferred.
$\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}$ act as a base, since it gets the proton that the acid lost.
Now, here comes an interesting data on considering the reverse reaction
$\mathrm{H}_{3} \mathrm{O}^{+}$- is an acid, because it can give a proton.
$\mathrm{Cl}^{-}$- is a base, since it has the capacity to receive a proton.
Notice that each pair, HCl and $\mathrm{Cl}^{-}$as well as $\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}$ and $\mathrm{H}_{3} \mathrm{O}^{+}$, differ by one proton ( symbol $=\mathrm{H}^{+}$). These pairs are called conjugate pairs.
$$
\mathrm{HNO}_{3}+\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O} \rightleftharpoons \mathrm{H}_{3} \mathrm{O}^{+}+\mathrm{NO}_{3}^{-}
$$
The acids are $\mathrm{HNO}_{3}$ and $\mathrm{H}_{3} \mathrm{O}^{+}$and the bases are $\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}$ and $\mathrm{NO}_{3}^{-}$.
Each conjugate pair has a proton donor and a proton acceptor. Acids are paired with bases. Each acid has, a proton available (an ionizable hydrogen) and conjugate base part. The acid ionizes releasing $\mathrm{H}^{+}$ion, whereas rest part of the original acid forms the conjugate base.
Nitric acid, $\mathrm{HNO}_{3}$, dissociates (splits) into a hydrogen ion and a nitrate ion. The hydrogen almost immediately joins to a water molecule to make a hydronium ion. The nitrate ion is the conjugate base of the $\mathrm{HNO}_{3}$ ion. In the second part of the reaction, water is a base (because it can accept a proton) and the hydronium ion is its conjugate acid.
$$
\underset{\text { Acid }}{\mathrm{HNO}_{3}+}+\underset{\text { Base }}{\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}} \longrightarrow \underset{\text { Conjugate base Conjugate acid }}{\mathrm{NO}_{3}^{-}}+\mathrm{H}_{3} \mathrm{O}^{+} ~
$$
## Problems with the theory
This theory works very nicely in all protic solvents (water, ammonia, acetic acid, etc.), but fails to explain acid base behavior in aprotic solvents such as benzene and dioxane. It also cannot explain reaction between acidic oxides ( $\mathrm{CO}_{2}$, $\mathrm{SO}_{2}$ ) and basic oxides ( $\mathrm{CaO}, \mathrm{MgO}$ etc).
$$
\mathrm{CaO}+\mathrm{SO}_{3} \longrightarrow \mathrm{CaSO}_{4}
$$

## Lewis Theory of Acids and Bases
In 1923, G.N. Lewis introduced another theory of acids and bases which could successfully explain the cases where there is no role for protons.
The G.N. Lewis idea of acids and bases is broader than the Bronsted Lowry model. According to Lewis, acids are electron pair acceptors and bases are electron pair donors.
## Postulates :
(i) An acid is a substance which can act as an electron pair acceptor. The molecule or ion should be associated with shortage of electrons to act as an acid.
Example : $\mathrm{BF}_{3}, \mathrm{H}^{+}$etc.
(ii) A base is a substance which can act as an electron pair donor. The molecule or ion should be associated with one or more lone pairs of electrons to act as a base.
Example : $\mathrm{NH}_{3}, \mathrm{OH}^{-}$etc.
(iii) Acid base neutralization can be defined as the transfer of an electron pair from a base to an acid.
$$
\mathrm{H}^{+}+: \mathrm{NH}_{3} \longrightarrow\left[\mathrm{H}^{+} \leftarrow: \mathrm{NH}_{3}\right]^{+}
$$
## Merits :
(i) This theory could explain even the acidic nature of those substances which do not contain hydrogen.
(ii) It could explain the neutralization reactions which do not involve any transfer of proton.
## Limitations:
(i) This theory failed to explain the acidic and basic nature of general acids and bases.
(ii) This theory could not explain the general neutralization reactions involving the formation of salt and water.
(iii) Generally, the acid-base reactions are fast reactions. But the formation of a coordinate bond is a slow process.
(iv) This theory cannot explain the relative strength of acids and bases.

The three different theories of acids and bases explain the nature of various acids and bases in different perceptions. However, none of the theories could convincingly explain all the properties of acids and bases and hence cannot be universally applicable. Depending on the nature and conditions of reaction, an appropriate theory is made applicable. Thus all the three theories put together can cover the entire range of acids and bases.
According to both Arrhenius and Bronsted, acid is a substance which can give $\mathrm{H}^{+}$ions. Hence, all Arrhenius acids are also Bronsted acids. However, there is a difference in the definition of base. According to Arrhenius, a base has to be $\mathrm{OH}^{-}$ion donor while according to Bronsted, base is one which accepts proton. Thus this means NaOH is base according to Arrhenius theory but not according to Bronsted theory.
Bronsted bases are proton acceptor. While Lewis bases are electron donors; they generally contain one or more lone pair of electrons and therefore, they can also accept a proton. Thus, all Lewis bases are also Bronsted bases. Whereas Bronsted acids can donate a proton but they are not capable of accepting a pair of electrons. Thus, all Bronsted acids are not Lewis acids..
## THE PH SCALE
So far, we have explained the strength of an acid or base in terms of ion concentrations. We have used qualitative terms like "concentrated", "dilute", "strong", and "weak". To improve accuracy we prefer to use quantitative terms. Instead of descriptive terms which could be misinterpreted, we use a number scale called the pH scale.
The term p in pH stands for "potenz" in German; meaning power and is the amount of hydrogen ions in a particular solution. The more ions, the more acidic, the solution is the fewer ions, the more alkaline (basic) the solution is.
The pH scale is used to rank solutions in terms of acidity or basicity (alkalinity). Since the scale is based on pH values, as the value of pH increases, concentration of $\mathrm{H}^{+}$ions decreases. The pH scale is often said to range from 0 to 14 . Anything below 7.0 is acidic, and has higher concentration of hydrogen ion $\left(\mathrm{H}^{+}\right)$in the solution; whereas anything above 7.0 is alkaline, or basic and has higher concentration of hydroxide ion $\left(\mathrm{OH}^{-}\right)$. The pH of a neutral solution is 7 .

## CALCULATION OF pH VALUE
Sorenson defined pH as the negative logarithm (to the base 10) of the hydrogen ion concentration expressed in moles per litre,
$$
\mathrm{pH}=-\log \left[\mathrm{H}^{+}\right]
$$
where $\left[\mathrm{H}^{+}\right]$is the hydrogen ion concentration in moles/litre.
Remember that sometimes $\mathrm{H}_{3} \mathrm{O}^{+}$is written, so $\mathrm{pH}=-\log \left[\mathrm{H}_{3} \mathrm{O}^{+}\right]$means the same thing. The pH scale runs from 0 to 14 . For acids, $\mathrm{pH}<7$; for bases, $\mathrm{pH}>7$; for neutral substances, $\mathrm{pH}=7$.
The negative logarithm is used because most of the pH values are positive numbers.
A change of one pH unit represents a ten-fold change in the concentration of hydronium and hydroxide ions. Thus, pH equal to 2 is ten times as acidic as pH equal to 3 , one hundred times as acidic as pH equal to 4 , one thousand times as acidic as pH equal to 5 , and so on. Similarly, pH equal to 13 is ten times as basic as pH equal to 12 and so on.
Example: The $\left[\mathrm{H}^{+}\right]$in a solution is measured to be 0.010 M . What is the pH ?

$$
\begin{aligned}
& \mathrm{pH}=-\log 0.010 \\
& \mathrm{pH}=-\log 10^{-2} \\
& \mathrm{pH}=-(-2)=2
\end{aligned}
$$

## THE pOH VALUE
Sorenson defined pH as the negative logarithm of the hydrogen ion concentration,
$\mathrm{pH}=-\log \left[\mathrm{H}^{+}\right]$
So, the pOH can be defined in a similar way: $\mathrm{pOH}=-\log \left[\mathrm{OH}^{-}\right]$
In words, the pOH is the negative logarithm of the hydroxide ion concentration.
So let's try a simple problem: The $\left[\mathrm{OH}^{-}\right]$in a solution is measured to be 0.0010 M . What is the pOH ?
The solution is pretty straightforward. Plug the [ $\mathrm{OH}^{-}$] into the pOH definition:
$\mathrm{pOH}=-\log 0.0010$
An alternate way to write this is: $\mathrm{pOH}=-\log 10^{-3}$
Since the $\log$ of $10^{-3}$ is -3 , we have: $\mathrm{pOH}=-(-3)$
pOH equal to 3 represents that solution is basic.

## IMPORTANCE OF PH IN EVERYDAY LIFE
## Blood pH
In order to function properly, the human body needs to maintain a blood pH between 7.35 and 7.45. Large deviation from this range is extremely dangerous. Values greater than 7.8 or less than 6.8 often results in death. Any condition in which the blood pH drops below 7.35 is known as acidosis. If the pH rises above 7.45 , it is known as alkalosis.
## Acid Rain
When pH of rain water is less than 5.6, it is called acid rain. When acid rain flows into the rivers, it lowers the pH of the river water. The survival of aquatic life in such rivers becomes difficult.
## pH of the Soil
Plants require a specific pH range for their healthy growth. The effect of soil pH has a significant role in the solubility of minerals or nutrients. Before a nutrient can be used by plants it must be dissolved in the soil solution. Most minerals and nutrients are more soluble or available in acidic soils than in neutral or slightly alkaline soils. Phosphorus is not readily soluble in the soil but is most available in soil with a pH range around 6.5. Strongly acidic soils ( $\mathrm{pH} 4.0-5.0$ ) can have high concentrations of soluble aluminum, iron and manganese which may be toxic to the growth of some plants.
The soil pH can also influence plant growth by its effect on activity of beneficial microorganisms. Bacteria that decompose soil organic matter are as not found in strong acid soils. This prevents organic matter from breaking down, resulting in an accumulation of organic matter and the tie up of nutrients, particularly nitrogen, that are held in the organic matter.
## pH in Our Digestive System
It is very interesting to note that our stomach produces hydrochloric acid which helps in the digestion of food without harming the stomach. During indigestion the stomach produces too much acid which causes pain and irritation. To get rid of this pain, people uses bases called antacids. These antacids neutralise the excess acid.
Magnesium hydroxide (milk of magnesia), a mild base, is often used for this purpose.
## pH Change as the Cause of Tooth Decay
Tooth decay starts when the pH of the mouth is lower than 5.5. Tooth enamel, made up of calcium phosphate is the hardest substance in the body. Bacteria present in the mouth produces acids by degradation of sugar and food particles remaining in the mouth after eating. The best way to prevent this is to clean the mouth after eating food. Using toothpastes, which are generally basic, for cleaning the teeth can neutralise the excess acid and prevent tooth decay.

## SALT
Compounds formed by the neutralization reaction between an acid and a base are known as a salts.
## Acid + Base → Salt + Water
Salts are ionic compounds which dissociate to yield a positively charged ion other than hydrogen [ $\mathrm{H}^{+}$] and negatively charged ion other than hydroxide ion [ $\mathrm{OH}^{-}$]. During salt formation, positive ion comes from base and negative ion comes from acid.
## Example:
The most familiar salt is sodium chloride, the principal component of common table salt. Sodium chloride, NaCl , and water, $\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}$, are formed by neutralization of sodium hydroxide, NaOH (a base), with hydrogen chloride, HCl (an acid):
$$
\mathrm{HCl}+\mathrm{NaOH} \longrightarrow \mathrm{NaCl}+\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}
$$
In sodium chloride $(\mathrm{NaCl})$, formation of positively charged ion, (sodium $\mathrm{Na}^{+}$) occurs from sodium hydroxide and negatively charged ion, chloride ( $\mathrm{Cl}^{-}$) occurs from hydrochloric acid.
Note: Although best known salt is sodium chloride which is commonly known as common salt. It is important to know that salt is a general name and it does not refer only to sodium chloride. In fact sodium chloride is just one member of a huge family of salts. Some other examples of salts are magnesium sulphate, potassium nitrate, calcium carbonate etc.
## Family of Salts
The salts having same positive ions or same negative ions are said to belong to a family of salts. For example calcium chloride and calcium sulphate belong to same family of salts 'calcium salts' because they both contain the same positive ion, calcium ion. Similarly calcium chloride and sodium chloride belong to same family of salts 'chloride salts' as both contain the same negative ion, chloride ion.
A salt is a compound consisting of positive metallic ion and negative ion derived from an acid, if negative ions are capable of further ionization to yield $\mathrm{H}^{+}$the salt is an acid salt, but if not, the salt is a normal salt.

## CLASSIFICATION OF SALTS
## (i) Acidic Salts
Salt which produces acidic solution when added to water is acidic salt. The pH of the acidic salt solution is less than 7 . If a polybasic acid (Example, $\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{SO}_{4}, \mathrm{H}_{3} \mathrm{PO}_{4}, \mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{SO}_{3}$ etc.) is neutralised partly by a base, the salt formed is acidic. In other words such salts are produced by the replacement of only a part of acidic hydrogen of the polybasic acids by a metal. Thus the anions of these salts retain one or more hydrogen atom of the parent acid. Acid salt exhibits all the properties of an acid in solution.
## Acidic Salts :
$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{CO}_{3}+\mathrm{NaOH} & \longrightarrow \mathrm{NaHCO}_{3}+\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O} \\
\mathrm{NaHSO}_{3}(\text { Sodium bisulphite }) & ; \quad \mathrm{KHSO}_{3}(\text { Potassium bisulphite }) \\
\mathrm{NaHSO}_{4}(\text { Sodium bisulphate }) & ; \quad \mathrm{KHSO}_{4}(\text { Potassium bisulphate })
\end{aligned}
$$
## (ii) Normal Salts
When acid and base neutralise each other completely, the salt formed is a normal salt. The pH of the salt solution of normal salt is 7 . Normal salts are produced by the replacement of all replaceable hydrogen(s) present in the molecule of an acid by metal or $\mathrm{NH}_{4}{ }^{+}$group. Thus such salts contain neither replaceable hydrogen nor hydroxyl group in the formula.
$$
\underset{\text { (Carbonic acid) }}{\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{CO}_{3}}+2 \mathrm{NaOH} \longrightarrow \underset{\substack{\text { Sodium Carbonate } \\ \text { (normal salt) }}}{\mathrm{Na}_{2} \mathrm{CO}_{3}}+2 \mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}
$$
Aqueous solution of normal salts may be neutral, acid or alkaline depending upon the nature of the constituent acid and base.
Normal Salts :
$$
\begin{array}{ll}
\mathrm{KNO}_{3} \text { (Potassium nitrate), } & \mathrm{Na}_{2} \mathrm{SO}_{3} \text { (Sodium sulphite) } \\
\mathrm{KCl} \text { (Potassium chloride), } & \mathrm{Na}_{2} \mathrm{SO}_{4} \text { (Sodium sulphate) }
\end{array}
$$
## (iii) Basic Salts
Salts which produces basic solution when added to water is basic salt. The pH of the basic salt solution is more than 7.
This type of salts are formed by incomplete neutralization of a base with an acid or by partial replacement of hydroxy radicals of a diacids or triacidic base with an acid radical.
This type of salts contain a metallic cation, a hydroxy ion of a base and anion of an acid.
$$
\left[\mathrm{Cu}(\mathrm{OH})_{2}+\mathrm{HNO}_{3} \longrightarrow \mathrm{Cu}(\mathrm{OH}) \mathrm{NO}_{3}+\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}\right]
$$
Basic salts: $\mathrm{Cu}(\mathrm{OH}) \mathrm{NO}_{3}$
$$
\mathrm{Pb}(\mathrm{OH}) \mathrm{NO}_{3}

## Other types of Salt
## (1) Double Salts
Such salts are formed by mixing saturated solutions of two simple salts, followed by crystallisation of the saturated solution.
## Example :
$\mathrm{FeSO}_{4}\left(\mathrm{NH}_{4}\right)_{2} \mathrm{SO}_{4} \cdot 6 \mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}$ : Mohr's salt, it a is mixture of $\mathrm{FeSO}_{4}$ (simple salt) and $\left(\mathrm{NH}_{4}\right)_{2} \mathrm{SO}_{4}$ (simple salt)
$\mathrm{K}_{2} \mathrm{SO}_{4} \cdot \mathrm{Al}_{2}\left(\mathrm{SO}_{4}\right)_{3} \cdot 24 \mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}$ : Potash alum, it is a mixture of $\mathrm{K}_{2} \mathrm{SO}_{4}$ (simple salt) and $\mathrm{Al}_{2}\left(\mathrm{SO}_{4}\right)_{3}$ (simple salt).
Alum is used for purification of water.
## (2) Mixed Salts
Such a salt contains two or more acidic radicals or basic radicals other than $\mathrm{OH}^{-}, \mathrm{H}^{+}$
They consists of a fixed proportion of two salts, often sharing either a common cation or common anion.
Examples : Sodium potassium sulphate, $\mathrm{NaKSO}_{4}$ (two basic radicals, $\mathrm{Na}^{+}, \mathrm{K}^{+}$)
Bleaching powder - $\mathrm{CaOCl}_{2}$ (two acid radicals $\mathrm{OCl}^{-}, \mathrm{Cl}^{-}$)
## (3) Complex Salts
Like double salts, complex salts are formed by mixing saturated solutions of two simple salts, followed by crystallisation of the solution.
These salts give a simple and a complex ion on dissociation.
Examples : Potassium mercuric iodide - $\quad \mathrm{K}_{2}\left[\mathrm{Hg} \mathrm{I}_{4}\right]$
$$
\text { Simple ion }-\mathrm{K}^{+} \quad ; \quad \text { Complex ion }-\left[\mathrm{Hg} \mathrm{I}_{4}\right]^{2-}
$$
Sodium silver cyanide - $\mathrm{Na}\left[\mathrm{Ag}(\mathrm{CN})_{2}\right]$
$$
\text { Simple ion }-\mathrm{Na}^{+} \quad ; \quad \text { Complex ion }-\left[\mathrm{Ag}(\mathrm{CN})_{2}\right]^{-}
$$
## - $\quad \mathrm{K}_{4}\left[\mathrm{Fe}(\mathrm{CN})_{6}\right]$ is which type of salt?
## SOLUTION
It is a complex salt because on dissociation it gives simple and complex ion in solution.
$$
\mathrm{K}_{4}\left[\mathrm{Fe}(\mathrm{CN})_{6}\right] \longrightarrow 4 \mathrm{~K}^{+}+\left[\mathrm{Fe}(\mathrm{CN})_{6}\right]^{4-}
$$

## Solubility of Salts
Some general observations about solubility of salts are listed below.
(i) All sodium, potassium and ammonium salts are soluble in water.
(ii) All nitrites, nitrates and bicarbonates are soluble in water.
(iii) Sulphates (except those of $\mathrm{Pb}^{2+}, \mathrm{Ba}^{2+}$ and $\mathrm{Ca}^{2+}$ ) are soluble in water.
(iv) All metallic oxides and sulphides are insoluble in water.
(v) Chlorides (except those of $\mathrm{Pb}^{2+}, \mathrm{Ag}^{+}, \mathrm{Hg}^{+}$) are soluble in water.
(vi) All metallic carbonates and phosphates are insoluble in water.
- Oxides and sulphides of $\mathrm{Na}^{+}$and $\mathrm{K}^{+}$are soluble in water.
- $\mathrm{PbCl}_{2}$ is soluble in hot water.
- Carbonates, hydroxides and phosphates of $\mathrm{Na}^{+}, \mathrm{K}^{+}$and $\mathrm{NH}_{4}^{+}$are soluble in water.
## Water of Crystallization
Water of crystallisation is the fixed number of water molecules present in the formula unit of a salt.
On the basis of water of crystallisation salts are classified as hydrated salts. Hydrated salts are those salts which contain a definite number of water molecules as water of crystallisation. Example
$\mathrm{CuSO}_{4} \cdot 5 \mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}$ - Copper sulphate penta-hydrate. It is also called blue vitriol.
$\mathrm{CaCl}_{2} \cdot 6 \mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}$ - Calcium chloride hexa-hydrate. It has 6 molecules of water of crystallisation.
$\mathrm{Na}_{2} \mathrm{CO}_{3} \cdot 10 \mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}$ - Sodium carbonate deca-hydrate. It is also called washing soda. It has 10 molecules of water of crystallisation.
## Hygroscopic Substances
These are substances which on exposure to atmosphere absorb moisture but do not change their original state after absorption.
For example :
CaO (quick lime), $\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{SO}_{4}$ (sulphuric acid), Silica gel.

## Hydrolysis of Salts
It is commonly observed that different salts, on dissolution in water do not always form neutral solutions. For example, aqueous solution of copper sulphate is acidic whereas aqueous solution sodium acetate is basic in character. However, salts of strong acids and strong bases such as sodium chloride, potassium chloride etc. form neutral aqueous solution. Salt hydrolysis is the interaction of anion or cation of the salt with water molecules to produce an acidic, basic or neutral solution.
The pH of the solution gets affected by this interaction. The cations of strong bases (such as $\mathrm{Na}^{+}, \mathrm{K}^{+}, \mathrm{Rb}^{+}, \mathrm{Ca}^{2+}, \mathrm{Ba}^{2+}$, etc.) do not hydrolyze and therefore, the solutions of salts formed by strong acids and strong bases are neutral and their pH is 7 . On the other hand, the solutions of the salts formed by strong acids and weak bases are acidic i.e., their pH is less than 7 . While the solutions of strong bases and weak acids are basic and their pH is greater than 7 .
Since salt on dissolution in water undergoes dissociation to form ions, salt hydrolysis is reverse of neutralization.
(a) Hydrolysis of salts of weak acid and strong base e.g. sodium acetate $\left(\mathrm{CH}_{3} \mathrm{COONa}\right)$, sodium carbonate $\left(\mathrm{Na}_{2} \mathrm{CO}_{3}\right)$, sodium phosphate $\left(\mathrm{Na}_{3} \mathrm{PO}_{4}\right)$ etc. give aqueous solution in alkaline range because on hydrolysis they form weak acid (not completely) ionisable) and strong base which is approximately completely ionised. Thus in this case, base overpowers the effect of weak acid and resulting solution is basic in nature. For example
$$
\mathrm{CH}_{3} \mathrm{COONa}+\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O} \rightleftharpoons \underset{\text { Weak acid }}{\mathrm{CH}_{3} \mathrm{COOH}}+\underset{\text { Strong base }}{\mathrm{NaOH}}
$$
(b) Hydrolysis of salts of weak bases and strong acids e.g. ammonium chloride $\left(\mathrm{NH}_{4} \mathrm{Cl}\right)$, copper sulphate $\left(\mathrm{CuSO}_{4}\right)$, aluminium chloride $\left(\mathrm{AlCl}_{3}\right)$ etc. give aqueous solution in acidic range because on hydrolysis they form weak base (not completely ionisable) and strong acid (get completely ionised). Thus in this case acid over powers the effect of weak base and resulting solution is acidic in nature.
$$
\mathrm{NH}_{4} \mathrm{Cl}+\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O} \rightleftharpoons \underset{\text { Weak base }}{\mathrm{NH}_{4} \mathrm{OH}}+\underset{\text { Strong acid }}{\mathrm{HCl}}
$$
(c) Salts of weak acid and a weak base e.g. ammonium acetate $\left(\mathrm{CH}_{3} \mathrm{COONH}_{4}\right.$, ammonium carbonate $\left(\mathrm{NH}_{4}\right)_{2} \mathrm{CO}_{3}$, aluminium phosphate $\left(\mathrm{Al}_{3} \mathrm{PO}_{4}\right)$ etc on hydrolysis give almost neutral, solution.
$$
\mathrm{CH}_{3} \mathrm{COONH}_{4}+\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O} \rightleftharpoons \underset{\text { Weak acid }}{\mathrm{CH}_{3} \mathrm{COOH}}+\underset{\text { Weak base }}{\mathrm{NH}_{4} \mathrm{OH}}
$$
(d) Salts of strong acid and strong base e.g. sodium chloride ( NaCl ), potassium nitrate ( $\mathrm{KNO}_{3}$ ), sodium sulphate ( $\mathrm{Na}_{2} \mathrm{SO}_{4}$ ). potassium chlorate $\left(\mathrm{KClO}_{4}\right)$ etc. Such salts on hydrolysis produce equally strong acids and bases. Thus their solutions are neutral.
$$
\mathrm{NaCl}+\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O} \rightleftharpoons \underset{\text { Strong base }}{\mathrm{NaOH}}+\underset{\text { Strong acid }}{\mathrm{HCl}}
$$

## COMMON ION EFFECT
Dissociation of acids/bases in solution is affected by the presence of other species in solution. Addition of some substances can increase or decrease dissociation of acid or base in solution. For example, acetic acid ( $\mathrm{CH}_{3} \mathrm{COOH}$ ) dissociates as :
$$
\mathrm{CH}_{3} \mathrm{COOH}(\mathrm{aq}) \rightleftharpoons \mathrm{H}^{+}(\mathrm{aq})+\mathrm{CH}_{3} \mathrm{COO}^{-}(\mathrm{aq})
$$
Now if we add salt or acid that supplies any of the above ion, for example $\mathrm{CH}_{3} \mathrm{COONa}$ or HCl to pre existing equilibrium in solution, then the equilibrium will shift to left due to common ion. Hence, dissociation of $\mathrm{CH}_{3} \mathrm{COOH}$ decreases.
Similarly, when a strong electrolyte like $\mathrm{NH}_{4} \mathrm{Cl}$ or NaOH is added to $\mathrm{NH}_{4} \mathrm{OH}$ solution then the equilibrium will shift to the left because of high concentration of either $\mathrm{NH}_{4}^{+}$ions or of $\mathrm{OH}^{-}$ions,
$$
\mathrm{NH}_{4} \mathrm{OH} \rightleftharpoons \mathrm{NH}_{4}^{+}+\mathrm{OH}^{-}
$$
Thus in short the ionisation of weak acid or weak base is suppressed by the addition of its own ion is known as common ion effect.

## Sodium Hydroxide (NaOH)
## Methods of preparation
(a) Causticisation process (Gossage process)
This is the most common method of preparation of NaOH . It is based on the interaction of a solution of soda (i.e. $\mathrm{Na}_{2} \mathrm{CO}_{3}$ ) and slaked lime (i.e $\mathrm{Ca}(\mathrm{OH})_{2}$ ).
$$
\mathrm{Na}_{2} \mathrm{CO}_{3}+\mathrm{Ca}(\mathrm{OH})_{2} \rightleftharpoons 2 \mathrm{NaOH}+\mathrm{CaCO}_{3}
$$
(b) Chlor-alkali process
A chlor-alkali plant produces two principle products; chlorine ( $\mathrm{Cl}_{2}$ ) and sodium hydroxide, which is commonly known as caustic soda $(\mathrm{NaOH})$. The term chlor-alkali is derived from the two products produced; chlor from chlorine and alkali from sodium hydroxide (sodium hydroxide is part of a group of chemicals called alkalis). The chlorine and sodium hydroxide are produced from the electrolysis of an aqueous solution of sodium chloride brine (salt water solution). Hydrogen gas is produced as a by product of the chlor-alkali process.
The overall reaction taking place in the chlor - alkali process is :
$$
2 \mathrm{NaCl}(\mathrm{aq})+2 \mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O} \longrightarrow \mathrm{H}_{2}(\mathrm{~g})+\mathrm{Cl}_{2}(\mathrm{~g})+2 \mathrm{NaOH}(\mathrm{aq})
$$
The three products produced in this process are all useful. Figure shows the different uses of products.
## Properties of NaOH
(a) $\quad \mathrm{NaOH}$ (Caustic soda) is a white translucent solid available in pellets, flakes and granules as $50 \%$ saturated solution.
(b) The m.p. of NaOH is 519 K . It is an ionic compound.
(c) It is readily soluble in water and forms a strong alkaline solution with liberation of heat.
(d) It is deliquescent and readily absorbs $\mathrm{CO}_{2}$ from air, so it should be stored in air-tight containers.
(e) It is also soluble in ethanol and methanol though the solubility in these solvents is much lower as compared to that in water.
(f) It is insoluble in ether and other non-polar solvents.
(g) It's solution leaves a yellow stain on fabric and paper.
(h) It reacts with acids to form corresponding salts.
(i) It's solution when exposed to air reacts with atmospheric $\mathrm{CO}_{2}$ and form a crust of $\mathrm{Na}_{2} \mathrm{CO}_{3}$ at the surface.
## Uses of sodium hydroxide
(a) It is used in many industries, mostly as a strong base in the manufacture of pulp, paper, textiles, soaps, dyes, cellulose, detergents etc.
(b) It is used in petroleum refining.
(c) It is used as a drain cleaner.
(d) It is used in the preparation of sodium metal and many salts of the sodium.
## Sodium Carbonate (Washing Soda) $\mathrm{Na}_{2} \mathrm{CO}_{3} \cdot 10 \mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}$
It exists in various forms, namely anhydrous sodium carbonate $\mathrm{Na}_{2} \mathrm{CO}_{3}$ (soda-ash), monohydrate $\mathrm{Na}_{2} \mathrm{CO}_{3}$. $\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}$ (crystal carbonate), heptahydrate. $\mathrm{Na}_{2} \mathrm{CO}_{3} .7 \mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}$ and decahydrate $\mathrm{Na}_{2} \mathrm{CO}_{3} .10 \mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}$ (washing soda or sal soda)
## Methods of preparations
Sodium carbonate is generally prepared now a days by ammonia soda or Solvay process. The ingredients of this process are readily available and inexpensive. These are salt brine $(\mathrm{NaCl})$ (from sea), ammonia $\left(\mathrm{NH}_{3}\right)$ and lime stone $\mathrm{CaCO}_{3}$ (from mines).
Calcium chloride $\left(\mathrm{CaCl}_{2}\right)$ is an important by-product.
- The process was devised and first put to commercial use by Ernest Solvay, who built a plant in 1865 in Couillet, Belgium, and was improved in the 1870s by the German-born British Chemist Ludwig Mond.
- In this process, advantage is taken of low solubility of sodium bicarbonate whereby it gets precipitated in the reaction between sodium chloride and ammonium bicarbonate $\left[\mathrm{NH}_{4} \mathrm{HCO}_{3}\right]$
## Ammonia soda (Solvay) process
(i) For preparation of $\mathrm{NH}_{4} \mathrm{HCO}_{3}$, carbon dioxide is passed into a concentrated solution of ammonia when ammonium carbonate followed by ammonium bicarbonate is formed. The reactions can be represented by the following equations :
$$
\begin{gathered}
2 \mathrm{NH}_{3}+\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}+\mathrm{CO}_{2} \longrightarrow\left(\mathrm{NH}_{4}\right)_{2} \mathrm{CO}_{3} \\
\left(\mathrm{NH}_{4}\right)_{2} \mathrm{CO}_{3}+\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}+\mathrm{CO}_{2} \longrightarrow 2 \mathrm{NH}_{4} \mathrm{HCO}_{3}
\end{gathered}
$$
(ii) Addition of common salt $(\mathrm{NaCl})$ to the solution containing $\mathrm{NH}_{4}^{+}$and $\mathrm{HCO}_{3}^{-}$results in the precipitation of $\mathrm{NaHCO}_{3}$ which is least soluble. It is then filtered off.
$$
\mathrm{NH}_{4} \mathrm{HCO}_{3}+\mathrm{NaCl} \longrightarrow \mathrm{NH}_{4} \mathrm{Cl}+\mathrm{NaHCO}_{3} \downarrow
$$

(iii) Sodium bicarbonate is then heated to give $\mathrm{Na}_{2} \mathrm{CO}_{3}$,

2 \mathrm{NaHCO}_{3} \xrightarrow{\Delta} \mathrm{Na}_{2} \mathrm{CO}_{3}+\mathrm{CO}_{2}+\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}

- What is the difference between baking soda and washing soda?

Baking soda is $\mathrm{Na}_{2} \mathrm{CO}_{3}$ while washing soda is $\mathrm{NaHCO}_{3}$. Both baking soda and washing soda are alkaline (bases), but washing soda is stronger base. ## Calcium Sulphate, $\mathrm{CaSO}_{4}$
## Method of preparation
It occurs in nature as anhydrous $\mathrm{CaSO}_{4}$ or as dihydrate $\mathrm{CaSO}_{4} \cdot 2 \mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}$ (Gypsum). In India large quantities of gypsum are found in Punjab and Rajasthan.
It can be prepared by the action of conc. sulphuric acid on calcium chloride.
$$
\mathrm{CaCl}_{2}+\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{SO}_{4} \longrightarrow \mathrm{CaSO}_{4}+2 \mathrm{HCl}
$$
The dihydrate $\mathrm{CaSO}_{4} \cdot 2 \mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}$ is not soluble in water.
## Properties
Gypsum when heated to 390 K loses water to give $\mathrm{CaSO}_{4} \cdot \frac{1}{2} \mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}$. This is known as plaster of paris. When heated above 437 K , it becomes anhydrous $\mathrm{CaSO}_{4}$. ## Plaster of Paris $\mathrm{CaSO}_{4} \cdot \frac{1}{2} \mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}$
## Method of preparation :
It is prepared by heating gypsum ( $\mathrm{CaSO}_{4} \cdot 2 \mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}$ ) to 373 K .
$$
\mathrm{CaSO}_{4} \cdot 2 \mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O} \xrightarrow{373 \mathrm{~K}} \mathrm{CaSO}_{4} \cdot \frac{1}{2} \mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}+\frac{3}{2} \mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}
$$
The temperature should not be allowed to rise beyond 373 K as above this temperature whole of water of crystallization is lost.
## Properties
It is a white powder. When water is added to plaster of paris it evolves heat and quickly sets to a hard porous mass in about half an hour. The setting of plaster of Paris is accompanied by a slight expansion (about $1 \%$ ) in volume so that it fills the mould completely and takes a sharp impression. The process of setting occurs as follows:
$$
\underset{\text { Plaster of paris }}{\mathrm{CaSO}_{4} \cdot 1 / 2 \mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}} \xrightarrow[\text { setting }]{\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}} \underset{\substack{\text { Gyssum } \\ \text { orthorhombic } \\ \text { dihydrate }}}{\mathrm{CaSO}_{4} \cdot 2 \mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}} \xrightarrow[\text { hardening }]{\mathrm{CaSO}_{4} \cdot 2 \mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}}
$$
The first step is called the setting stage, and the second, the hardening stage. The setting of plaster of Paris is catalyzed by sodium chloride, while it is reduced by borax, or alum.
Uses
(a) It is used for producing moulds for industries like pottery, ceramics, etc.
(b) It is used for setting broken and fractured bones in the body.
(c) It is used for making statues, models and other decorative material.
(d) It is mixed with cement and so used in the building industry.

## Bleaching Powder, $\mathrm{CaOCl}_{2}$
## Method of preparation :
If chlorine is passed for a considerable time over solid slaked lime, the product formed is bleaching powder. It is also known as chloride of lime. Bleaching powder, a solid combination of chlorine and slaked lime, introduced in 1799 by the Scottish chemist,
Charles Tennant. Bleaching powder is represented as $\mathrm{CaOCl}_{2}$, though the actual composition is quite complex.
$$
\mathrm{Ca}(\mathrm{OH})_{2}+\mathrm{Cl}_{2} \longrightarrow \mathrm{CaOCl}_{2}+\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}
$$
In the laboratory bleaching powder is prepared by taking a gas jar full of chlorine and shaking a spatula measure of freshly prepared slaked lime into it. The colour of the chlorine disappears immediately. The product may be used to absorb chlorine from several more gas-jars before absorption is complete.
[Note : bleaching powder is actually a mixture of calcium hypochlorite $\left(\mathrm{Ca}(\mathrm{ClO})_{2}\right)$, calcium chloride $\mathrm{CaCl}_{2} \cdot \mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}$ with some slaked lime]
There are two types of calcium hypochlorite. These are (i) Dry form and (ii) Hydrated form
The hydrated form $\left(\mathrm{CaOCl}_{2} \mathrm{O}\right)$ is safer to handle. It was used as an transport substitute for free chlorine.
## Properties of bleaching powder
(a) It is a yellow white solid having a strong smell of chlorine.
(b) It is soluble in water. The lime present is always left behind as an insoluble salt.
(c) It has greater available chlorine than sodium hypochlorite, NaClO (liquid bleach). It contains about $36 \%$ of available chlorine.
[Note : Available chlorine is that which can be removed by dilute acids and even by carbonic acid $\left(\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{CO}_{3}\right)$ of the atmosphere.]
(d) Bleaching powder deteriorates if left in contact with the air and smells like chlorine because of action of $\mathrm{CO}_{2}$ of atmosphere i.e. $\mathrm{CaOCl}_{2}$ reacts with $\mathrm{CO}_{2}$ to form $\mathrm{CaCO}_{3}$ and release $\mathrm{Cl}_{2}$ and with HCl to form calcium chloride.
$$
\begin{aligned}
2 \mathrm{CaOCl}_{2}+2 \mathrm{CO}_{2} & \longrightarrow 2 \mathrm{CaCO}_{3}+2 \mathrm{Cl}_{2}+\mathrm{O}_{2} \\
\mathrm{CaOCl}_{2}+4 \mathrm{HCl} & \longrightarrow \mathrm{CaCl}_{2}+2 \mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}+\mathrm{Cl}_{2}
\end{aligned}
$$
Extreme care should be used in handling bleaching powder. It should always be kept in dry place, away from any organic material. Bleaching powder undergoes self-heating and rapid decomposition accompanied by release of toxic chlorine gas.
## Uses:
(a) It is widely used as a bleaching agent for bleaching clothes.
(b) It is used for disinfection of drinking water or swimming pool water. For use in outdoor swimming pools, $\mathrm{CaOCl}_{2}$ can be used as a sanitizer in combination with cyanuric acid stabilizers. Two stabilizers will reduce the loss of chlorine because of u.v. radiation.
(c) It is also used for bleaching cotton and linen.
(d) It is used in manufacture of chloroform $\left(\mathrm{CHCl}_{3}\right)$.
(e) It is used as an oxidising agent.
(f) It is also used in bathroom cleaners, household disinfectant sprays, mass and algae removers, and weed killers.
