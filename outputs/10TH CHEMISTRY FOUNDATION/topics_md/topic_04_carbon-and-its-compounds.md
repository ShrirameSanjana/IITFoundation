## TETRAVALENCY IN CARBON
A carbon atom has a total of six electrons occupying the first two shells, i.e., the K-shell has two electrons and the L-shell has four electrons. This distribution indicates that in the outermost shell there are one completely filled ' $s$ ' orbital and two half-filled ' $p$ ' orbitals, showing carbon to be a divalent atom. But in reality, carbon displays tetravalency in the combined state. Therefore, a carbon atom has four valence electrons. It could gain four electrons to form C anion or lose four electrons to form C cation. Both these conditions require large amount of energy. Thus it has a very little tendency to form ionic compounds. To overcome this problem carbon undergoes bonding by sharing its valence electron with other carbon atoms or with atoms of other elements. This allows it to be covalently bonded to one, two, three or four carbon atoms or atoms of other elements or groups of atoms (molecule).
This characteristic of carbon atom by virtue of which it form four covalent bonds is generally referred to as tetracovalency of carbon.
Not just carbon, but many other elements form molecules by sharing valence electrons. The shared electrons 'belong' to the outer shells of both the atoms and lead to both atoms attaining the noble gas configuration.

## COVALENT BOND
According to Lewis, covalent bond is formed by the sharing of same number of electron between two atoms to complete their octet".
If one electron is shared between two atoms then it forms single bond. Similarly double \& triple bonds are formed by the sharing of two and three electrons respectively. For example:-
Only the electrons of valence shell take part in sharing. The electrons of valence shell which do not take part in chemical bonding are called lone pair or non bonding electrons. For example, in oxygen six valence electrons of oxygen atom (2, 6) can take part in bonding. Out of six electrons two electrons, are shared by one oxygen atom thus after bond formation each oxygen possesses four non bonding electrons (or two lone pairs).
## Octet Rule
The octet rule requires all atoms in a molecule to have 8 valence electrons; either by sharing, losing or gaining electrons; to become stable. For covalent bonds, atoms tend to share their electrons with each other to satisfy the octet rule. It requires 8 electrons because that is the amount of electrons needed to fill its outer most orbital (electron configuration); also known as a noble gas configuration. Each atom wants to become as stable as the noble gases that have their outer valence shell filled. Although it is important to remember the "magic number", 8, yet there are many octet rule exceptions.
Example: There are many stable molecules in which central atom have more than $8 \mathrm{e}^{-}$in its valence shell e.g. $\mathrm{IF}_{7}$ ( I has $14 \mathrm{e}^{-}$in valence shell), $\mathrm{PCl}_{5}$ ( P has $10 \mathrm{e}^{-}$in valence shell), $\mathrm{SF}_{6}$ ( S has $12 \mathrm{e}^{-}$in valence shell) etc.
## General properties of covalent molecules
(a) Solubility : These compounds are usually insoluble or less soluble in water and in other polar solvents. Polar covalent molecules are more soluble in water in comparison to that of non polar covalent molecules.
(b) Low melting point and boiling point: The intermolecular forces of attractions between the molecules of covalent compounds are generally weak. As a result, lesser amount of energy is needed to overcome these intermolecular forces. Thus, these compounds usually have low melting point and boiling point than ionic compounds.
(c) Conducting nature: These are generally poor conductors of electricity in the fused or dissolved state due to unavailability of ions. or gases at room temperature. However, a few covalent compounds also exist in the solid state e.g. glucose, urea, thiourea \& sugar etc.
## Electron Dot (Lewis Dot) Structures
Lewis dot structures for many species can be written according to the following steps :
(i) Pick up the central atom of the species in question.
(ii) Recall the number of electrons in the outermost orbit (valence electrons) in each atom of the species.
(iii) Recall the valency of the central atom and other atoms too.
(iv) Write down the structural formula of the species on the basis of point no. (ii) and (iii).
(v) Supply the required number of electrons to each bond (1 pair of electrons for a single bond, 2 pairs for a double bond and 3 pairs for a triple bond).
(vi) Verify that each atom of the species has inert gas configuration, i.e., either 2 (in case of hydrogen) or 8 electrons in the outermost orbit. Let us apply these points on few examples.
For main group elements, the number of valence electrons which an atom contributes in a Lewis structure is the same as its group number in the periodic table.

These differences in the properties of diamond and graphite are due to the difference in their structures.
In diamond, each C atom is linked to its neighbours by four single covalent bonds. This leads to a three-dimensional network of covalent bonds. In graphite, the carbon atoms are arranged in flat parallel layers as regular hexagons. Each carbon in these layers is bonded to three others by covalent bonds. Graphite thus acquires some double bond character.
Each layer is bonded to adjacent layers by weak van der Waal's forces. Moreover, one electron is free at each carbon atom. This allows each layer to slide over the other easily. Due to this type of structure graphite is soft and slippery, and can act as a lubricant. Graphite is also a good conductor of electricity due to mobile electrons in it.
Graphite is also used as a industrial lubricant because it possesses high melting point. Thus no change in structure occur even if machineries are working at high temperature.

## CATENATION IN CARBON
Carbon atom is unique amongst all the elements that are found in nature. It can form long chain molecules. The ability of carbon to form a long chain is called catenation. Carbon forms strong bond because of its small size which enables the nucleus to held on to the shared pairs of electrons strongly. The chains are formed because carbon atoms form tetravalent bonds with other carbon atoms. This structure can be repeated endlessly without disturbing the stability of the bonds and the compounds formed. The chains can form branches, and sub-branches. The carbon atoms also form rings. The rings themselves can have more rings attached to them. Protein molecules and amino acids are long chain carbon molecules.
Figure : Variety of carbon chains formed when carbon atoms join together.
In addition, carbon atoms may be linked by single, double or triple bonds. Compounds of carbon, which has only single bonds between the carbon atoms are called saturated compounds. Compounds of carbon having double or triple bonds between their carbon atoms are called unsaturated compounds.

## COMPOUNDS OF CARBON
Compounds of carbon can broadly classified into two main categories, namely inorganic and organic compounds.
Inorganic compounds : These include compounds of carbon with metals and non-metals (other than hydrogen) such as oxygen, halogens etc. These do not have carbon-carbon bonds.
Organic compounds : Organic compounds are those chemical compounds in which one or more atoms of carbon are covalently linked to atoms of other elements, most commonly oxygen or nitrogen.

## Origin and Definition of Organic Compounds :
According to the earlier definition, organic chemistry was defined as the chemistry of substances found in the living matter (Berzelius in 1808). Further, Berzelius in 1815 also suggested that the organic compounds could be prepared only by living organisms under the influence of a mysterious force known as the vital force (life-force). The vital force theory remain unchallanged until 1828, when Friedrich Wohler, a German chemist, reported the synthesis of a typical organic compound urea by heating an inorganic compound, ammonium cyanate.
$$
\underset{\substack{\text { Ammonium } \\ \text { chloride }}}{\mathrm{NH}_{4} \mathrm{Cl}}+\underset{\substack{\text { Potassium } \\ \text { cyanate }}}{\mathrm{KCNO}} \longrightarrow \underset{\substack{\text { Ammonium } \\ \text { cyanate }}}{\mathrm{NH}_{4} \mathrm{CNO}+\mathrm{KCl}}
$$
The synthesis of urea from inorganic compounds gave a blow to the vital force theory and thus the distinction between organic and inorganic compounds based on vital force soon started fading away. The vital force theory was completely discarded after the synthesis of acetic acid from C, H and O (Kolbe, 1845) and methane (Berthelet, 1856). Subsequently, a large number of naturally occurring organic compounds were synthesized in the laboratory and the vital force theory was decisively disproved.
However, all the organic compounds were found to contain carbon as one of the elements. Thus the emphasis for the definition of organic compounds was shifted from origin to composition. Accordingly, organic chemistry has now been defined as the chemistry of carbon compounds.
On the basis of this definition, the study of compounds like $\mathrm{CO}, \mathrm{CO}_{2}, \mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{CO}_{3}$, carbonates, cyanides, cyanates, carbides, etc., should be included in organic chemistry, but due to their greater resemblance with inorganic compounds these are studied under inorganic chemistry.
Further, since all the organic compounds could be considered as derivatives of hydrocarbons, a more appropriate definition for the organic chemistry is the study of hydrocarbons and their derivatives.
## HYDROCARBONS
Compounds containing only carbon and hydrogen are known as hydrocarbons. Petroleum and coal are the two important natural sources of hydrocarbons. Petroleum is a major source of aliphatic hydrocarbons while coal is a source of aromatic hydrocarbons.
On the basis of structure and properties, hydrocarbons can be classified broadly into two classes.
## 1. Open Chain or Acyclic Hydrocarbons
As the name represents, these hydrocarbons contain open chain of carbon atoms in their molecules. These are also called aliphatic hydrocarbons. These are further classified into three types depending on the nature of carbon-carbon bonds in their molecules namely alkanes, alkenes and alkynes.
(i) Alkanes : These contain carbon-carbon single bonds e.g.
$$
\begin{array}{cc}
\mathrm{H}_{3} \mathrm{C}-\mathrm{CH}_{3} & \mathrm{H}_{3} \mathrm{C}-\mathrm{CH}_{2}-\mathrm{CH}_{2}-\mathrm{CH}_{3} \\
\text { Ethane } & \text { Butane }
\end{array}
$$
General formula of alkane is $\mathrm{C}_{\mathrm{n}} \mathrm{H}_{2 \mathrm{n}+2}$ and are commonly called paraffins.
(ii) Alkenes : These contain at least one carbon-carbon double bonds, e.g.
$$
\begin{array}{lc}
\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{C}=\mathrm{CH}_{2} & \mathrm{H}_{3} \mathrm{C}-\mathrm{CH}=\mathrm{CH}-\mathrm{CH}_{3} \\
\text { Ethene } & \text { Butene-2 }
\end{array}
$$
General formula of alkene is $\mathrm{C}_{n} \mathrm{H}_{2 n}$. They are commonly called olefins.

## HOMOLOGOUS SERIES
In chemistry, a homologous series is a series of organic compounds with a similar general formula, possessing similar chemical properties due to the presence of the same functional group, and shows a gradation in physical properties as a result of increase in molecular size and mass (see relative molecular mass). For example, ethane has a higher boiling point than methane since it has more van der Waal's forces(intermolecular forces) with neighbouring molecules. This is due to increase in the number of atoms making up the molecule.
Organic compounds in the same homologous series differ by a $\mathrm{CH}_{2}$ group. Alkanes (paraffins), alkenes (olefins), methoxyethane (ethers), and alkynes (acetylenes) form such series in which members differ in mass by 14 atomic mass units. For example, the alkane homologous series have methane $\left(\mathrm{CH}_{4}\right)$, ethane $\left(\mathrm{C}_{2} \mathrm{H}_{6}\right)$, propane $\left(\mathrm{C}_{3} \mathrm{H}_{8}\right)$, butane $\left(\mathrm{C}_{4} \mathrm{H}_{10}\right)$, and pentane $\left(\mathrm{C}_{5} \mathrm{H}_{12}\right)$, each member differing from the previous one by a $\mathrm{CH}_{2}$ group (or 14 atomic mass units).
Similarly, alcohol homologous series starts with methanol ( $\mathrm{CH}_{3} \mathrm{OH}$ ), ethanol ( $\mathrm{C}_{2} \mathrm{H}_{5} \mathrm{OH}$ ), propylalcohol ( $\boldsymbol{C}_{3} \boldsymbol{H}_{7} \mathbf{O H}$ ) etc.
- A homologation reaction is any chemical process converting one member of a homologous series to the next.

## NOMENCLATURE OF CARBON COMPOUNDS
In the early stages of the development, the various organic compounds were named on the basis of either the source of origin of the compounds or on some specific property of the compounds. For example, methane gas is found to get released in marshy places. Hence it is called marsh gas. These names are called trivial or common names.
In order to give names for the huge number of organic compounds used all over the world, a more systematic method was required. The International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry (IUPAC) developed a systematic approach to the nomenclature of organic compounds based on its structure and reflects the physical and chemical properties. This IUPAC system has three parts: root word, suffix and prefix.

Combustion usually occurs when a hydrocarbon reacts with oxygen to produce $\mathrm{CO}_{2}$ and $\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}$.
In more general sense, combustion involves a reaction between any combustible materials and an oxidizer to form an oxidized product. Combustion is an exothermic reaction, so it release heat. General reaction is:
Hydrocarbon + Oxygen ⟶ Carbondioxide + Water

## Preparation of ethanol by fermentation of sugar
Fermentation is a process whereby an organic compound is broken into smaller molecules by enzymes such as yeast. Ethanol is prepared by fermentation of molasses. Molasses, a by product of sugar industry, has traces of sugar. The fermentation is initiated by yeast, which secretes two enzymes called invertase and zymase. These enzymes act as catalyst for converting sugar into ethanol. The reaction is exothermic and carbon dioxide is released in the process.
$$
\begin{aligned}
& \underset{\text { Sucrose }}{\mathrm{C}_{12} \mathrm{H}_{22} \mathrm{O}_{11}+\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}} \xrightarrow{\text { Invertase }} \underset{\text { Glucose }}{\mathrm{C}_{6} \mathrm{H}_{12} \mathrm{O}_{6}}+\underset{\text { Fructose }}{\mathrm{C}_{6} \mathrm{H}_{12} \mathrm{O}_{6}} \\
& \underset{\text { Glucose or fructose }}{\mathrm{C}_{6} \mathrm{H}_{12} \mathrm{O}_{6}} \xrightarrow{\text { Zymase }} \underset{\text { Ethanol }}{2 \mathrm{C}_{2} \mathrm{H}_{5} \mathrm{OH}+2 \mathrm{CO}_{2}}
\end{aligned}
$$
- Presence of air will tend to oxidise the mixture whose resultant is vinegar (acetic acid or ethanoic acid) $\mathrm{CH}_{3} \mathrm{COOH}$. This is undesirable.
- Ethanol can also be made by fermentation of starchy materials such as barley, potatoes and maize. The starch is first converted to sugar by fermentation and then to ethanol.

## How Do Alcohols Affect Living Beings?
Superficially, ethanol evokes a distinctive heat-like sensation in the mouth and a stinging sensation on the skin. In the body it is metabolized to other substances, affecting the central nervous system in particular. The effect varies between individuals, and can be worse when applied in addition to certain drugs. When applied to open wounds (as for disinfection) it produces a strong stinging sensation. Pure or highly concentrated ethanol may permanently damage living tissue on contact. When large quantities of ethanol are consumed, it tends to slow metabolic processes and depresses the central nervous system. This results in lack of coordination, mental confusion, drowsiness, lowering of the normal inhibitions, and finally stupour.
The individual ethanol consuming may feel relaxed but does not realise that his sense of judgement, sense of timing, and muscular coordination have been seriously impaired. Ethanol within the human body is converted into acetaldehyde by alcohol dehydrogenase and then into acetic acid by acetaldehyde dehydrogenase. The product of the first step of this breakdown, acetaldehyde, is more toxic than ethanol. It has been shown to increase the risk of developing cirrhosis of the liver, multiple forms of cancer, and alcoholism. Death from ethyl alcohol consumption is possible when blood alcohol level reaches $0.4 \%$. A blood alcohol level of $0.5 \%$ or more is commonly fatal.

## ACETIC ACID (ETHANOIC ACID)
Acetic acid, also known as ethanoic acid, is an organic chemical compound best recognized for giving vinegar its sour taste and pungent smell. Its chemical formula is represented as $\mathrm{CH}_{3} \mathrm{COOH}$. The trivial name acetic acid is commonly used. Glacial acetic acid is a trivial name for water-free acetic acid, the name comes from the ice-like crystals formed slightly below room temperature at $16.7^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ (about $62^{\circ} \mathrm{F}$ ).
It is a weak acid because at standard temperature and pressure the dissociated acid exists in
Did You Know! equilibrium with the undissociated form in aqueous solutions, in contrast to strong acids, which are fully dissociated.
## Physical Properties
Pure, acetic acid (glacial acetic acid) is a colourless liquid that attracts water from the environment (hygroscopy), and freezes below $16.7^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ ( $62{ }^{\circ} \mathrm{F}$ ) to a colourless crystalline solid. Acetic acid is the second simplest carboxylic acids (first being formic acid, HCOOH ). Acetic acid is corrosive, and its vapour causes irritation to the eyes, a dry and burning nose, sore throat and congestion to lungs.
## Chemical Properties
Carboxylic acids $(-\mathrm{COOH})$ ionises in aqueous solution to give H , hence these are acidic in nature. Acetic acid is a weak, monoprotic acid. Acetic acid is corrosive to metals including iron, magnesium, and zinc, forming hydrogen gas and metal salts called acetates.

## SOAPS AND DETERGENTS
Soaps are sodium or potassium salts of higher fatty acids, produced from the alkaline hydrolysis of fats (higher fatty acids) in a chemical reaction. The reaction is called saponification. They are of biological origin. Each soap molecule has a long hydrocarbon chain, sometimes called its 'tail', with a carboxylate 'head'. In water, the sodium or potassium ions float free, leaving a negatively charged head.
- When the alkali is sodium hydroxide, a sodium soap is formed. Sodium soaps are "hard" soaps. When the alkali is potassium hydroxide, a potassium soap is formed. Potassium soaps are softer and are found in some liquid hand soaps and shaving creams.
- Soap is an excellent cleanser because of its ability to act as an emulsifying agent. An emulsifier is capable of dispersing one liquid into another immiscible liquid. This means that while oil (which attracts dirt) doesn't naturally mix with water, soap can suspends oil/dirt in such a way that it can be removed.
Detergents (syndets) are emulsifying agents that are very similar to soaps but they do not have a biological origin and they do not have a carboxylate group instead of it they have sulphonate group as they are sodium salt of long chain sulphonic acids. Synthetic detergents usually have a long hydrocarbon chain of $12-20$ carbon atoms. At the head of this chain there is a polar group that forms a strong hydrogen bond with water but does not form strong ionic bonds with the ions of hard water.
Before dealing with cleansing action of soap let us understand few points:
(i) The salts of fatty acids have an ionic polar head group, the carboxylate and a long non-polar hydrocarbon chain.

(iii) An aqueous solutions of this type of compound tend to form ordered aggregates such as micelles and bilayers.
(iv) Here the polar groups are exposed to the water and the hydrocarbon portions are buried within the core, away from the water.
## How Soap Cleans :
The things we call "dirt" are actually grease, oils, and fats which are insoluble in water. When cleaning, soap micelles come into contact with the "dirt" and because like dissolves like, a minute amount of "dirt" dissolves within the central region of the soap micelle. Nonpolar dirts are emulsified and washed away in polar wash water. This is why soaps are said to be good emulsifying agents.
## How Does Detergent Remove Grease From Clothing ?
Detergent molecules contain hydrophobic tails (hydrocarbon chain) which are water hating and will attach themselves to any substance which is non-polar (grease). The tails surround the grease with the polar heads in the aqueous phase, forming the structure shown. On agitation, the grease is pulled from the clothing allowing the grease to be completely surrounded and moved into the aqueous phase. This is then washed away leaving only clean clothes behind.

## Surface Tension/Surfactants :
The property of a liquid due to which its free surface tries to have minimum surface area and behaves as if it were under tension some what like a stretched elastic membrane is called surface tension. Chemicals that are able to reduce the surface tension of the solvent effectively are called surface active agents, or surfactants. Surfactants perform other important functions in cleaning, such as loosening, emulsifying (dispersing in water) and holding soil in suspension until it can be rinsed away. Surfactants can also provide alkalinity, which is useful in removing acidic soils. Surfactants are classified by their ionic (electrical charge) properties in water: anionic (negative charge), nonionic (no charge), cationic (positive charge) and amphoteric (either positive or negative charge). Soap is an anionic surfactant. Carboxymethyl cellulose (CMC) is a compound which is added to synthetic detergent to keep the dust particles suspended in water, and helps in cleansing process.

1. Temporary hardness: It is a type of water hardness caused by the presence of dissolved bicarbonate salts of calcium and magnesium. Temporary hardness can be removed by boiling. When temporary hard water is boiled, the bicarbonates of $\mathrm{Ca} \& \mathrm{Mg}$ undergo decomposition to form insoluble carbonates.
The insoluble carbonates of $\mathrm{Ca} \& \mathrm{Mg}$ can be removed by filtration and the water thus obtained is free from $\mathrm{Ca}^{2+} \& \mathrm{Mg}^{2+}$ ions and becomes soft.
Permanent hardness: This type of water hardness is due to the presence of Ca or Mg sulphates or chlorides. Permanent hardness cannot be removed by boiling. It can be removed by using water softeners.
