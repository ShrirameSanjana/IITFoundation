## CLASSIFICATION OF CHEMICAL REACTIONS BASED ON THEIR SPEED
In a chemical reaction, the bonds present in the reactants are broken and new bonds are formed to give the products. The speed of bond formation and bond breaking in different reactions is different. Chemical kinetics tells about the rate of a reaction. Depending upon the rate of reaction, by which the reactants are converted into products, reactions are classified in three types.
## (a) Fast or Instantaneous Reactions
Those reactions in which the reactants readily change into products within few seconds are fast reactions. Ionic reactions are fast reactions.
## Example :
Acid-base reactions complete in a fraction of second, e.g. Neutralisation of sodium hydroxide by hydrochloric acid.
$\underset{\substack{\text { Sodium } \\ \text { hydroxide }}}{\mathrm{NaOH}(\mathrm{aq})}+\underset{\substack{\text { Hydrochloric } \\ \text { acid }}}{\mathrm{HCl}(\mathrm{aq})} \longrightarrow \underset{\substack{\text { Sodium } \\ \text { chloride }}}{\mathrm{NaCl}(\mathrm{aq})}+\underset{\text { Water }}{\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}(\mathrm{l})}$
## (b) Moderate Reactions
Those chemical reactions which take few minutes or hours to complete are moderate reactions. (Neither very slow nor very fast)
## Example :
(i) $\mathrm{N}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{5} \xrightarrow{55^{\circ} \mathrm{C}} 2 \mathrm{NO}_{2}+\frac{1}{2} \mathrm{O}_{2}$
(ii) $\underset{\text { Sucrose }}{\mathrm{C}_{12} \mathrm{H}_{22} \mathrm{O}_{11}}+\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O} \longrightarrow \underset{\text { Glucose }}{\mathrm{C}_{6} \mathrm{H}_{12} \mathrm{O}_{6}}+\underset{\text { Fructose }}{\mathrm{C}_{6} \mathrm{H}_{12} \mathrm{O}_{6}}$
## (c) Slow Reactions
Those reactions which take very long time to complete are called slow reactions.
## Example :
(i) Rusting of iron takes place in days or months.
$$
2 \mathrm{Fe}+\frac{3}{2} \mathrm{O}_{2}+x \mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O} \longrightarrow \mathrm{Fe}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3} \cdot x \mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}
$$
(ii) Weathering of rocks and the reactions in earth's crust continue for several years.
Some reaction are slow. For example; reaction between potassium permaganate and oxalic acid or hydrogen peroxide solution proceed with slow rate. There are several reactions which are very slow and take very long time for completion.

## REACTION RATE AND ITS MEASUREMENT
Let us consider a simple hypothetical chemical reaction,
$$
\mathrm{X} \longrightarrow \mathrm{Y}
$$
The progress of the chemical reaction is followed by measuring the change in concentration of either reactant or product. Hence the rate of a reaction may be defined as the decrease in the concentration of reactant per unit time or increase in the concentration of product per unit time. It represents the average rate of reaction. So, mathematically the rate of reaction is expressed as given below.
$$
\begin{aligned}
\text { Average rate of reaction } & =\frac{\text { Decrease in concentration of reactant }}{\text { Time interval }} \\
& =\frac{\text { Amount of reactant consumed }}{\text { Time interval }} \\
& =-\frac{\Delta[\mathrm{X}]}{\Delta t}
\end{aligned}
$$
$$
\begin{aligned}
\text { Similarly, Average rate of reaction } & =\frac{\text { Increase in concentration of product }}{\text { Time interval }} \\
& =\frac{\text { Amount of product formed }}{\text { Time interval }} \\
& =+\frac{\Delta[\mathrm{Y}]}{\Delta t}
\end{aligned}
$$
Reaction rates are always positive. The negative sign along with the change in concentration of the reactant shows that the concentration of the reactant decreases with time while the plus sign along with the concentration of the product denotes that the concentration of the product increases with time.
The decrease in concentration of the reactant and increase in concentration of the product can graphically be represented in the following fig.
Fig. Variation of concentration with time.
Now let us extend this concept of expressing the rate of reaction to different kind of chemical reactions.
(a) Reactions Having Same Stoichiometric Coefficients of the Reactants and Products
For the reaction,
$$
\mathrm{PCl}_{5} \rightleftharpoons \mathrm{PCl}_{3}+\mathrm{Cl}_{2}
$$
Suppose during a time interval, $\Delta t$,
Decrease in concentration of $\mathrm{PCl}_{5}=\Delta\left[\mathrm{PCl}_{5}\right]$
Increase in concentration of $\mathrm{PCl}_{3}=\Delta\left[\mathrm{PCl}_{3}\right]$
Increase in concentration of $\mathrm{Cl}_{2}=\Delta\left[\mathrm{Cl}_{2}\right]$
Where square brackets show molar concentration, i.e, moles litre ${ }^{-1}$ of the substance.
Thus, rate of reaction is given by
Rate of reaction $=-\frac{\Delta\left[\mathrm{PCl}_{5}\right]}{\Delta t}=+\frac{\Delta\left[\mathrm{PCl}_{3}\right]}{\Delta t}=+\frac{\Delta\left[\mathrm{Cl}_{2}\right]}{\Delta t}$
Likewise, for the reaction
$$
\mathrm{NO}_{2}(\mathrm{~g})+\mathrm{CO}(\mathrm{~g}) \longrightarrow \mathrm{CO}_{2}(\mathrm{~g})+\mathrm{NO}(\mathrm{~g})
$$
Rate of reaction $=-\frac{\Delta\left[\mathrm{NO}_{2}\right]}{\Delta t}=-\frac{\Delta[\mathrm{CO}]}{\Delta t}=+\frac{\Delta\left[\mathrm{CO}_{2}\right]}{\Delta t}=+\frac{\Delta[\mathrm{NO}]}{\Delta t}$
(b) Reactions Having Different Stoichiometric Coefficients of Reactants and Products
Let us consider the reaction involving the thermal decomposition of nitrogen pentoxide ( $\mathrm{N}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{5}$ ) as shown below,
$$
2 \mathrm{~N}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{5}(\mathrm{~g}) \rightleftharpoons 4 \mathrm{NO}_{2}(\mathrm{~g})+\mathrm{O}_{2}(\mathrm{~g})
$$
then,
The rate of disappearance of $\mathrm{N}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{5}=-\frac{\Delta\left[\mathrm{N}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{5}\right]}{\Delta t}$
The rate of appearance of $\mathrm{NO}_{2}=-\frac{\Delta\left[\mathrm{N}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{5}\right]}{\Delta t}$
The rate of appearance of $\mathrm{O}_{2}=+\frac{\Delta\left[\mathrm{O}_{2}\right]}{\Delta t}$
From the given reaction, it is evident that for each mole of $\mathrm{O}_{2}$ formed, four moles of $\mathrm{NO}_{2}$ are formed and two moles of $\mathrm{N}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{5}$ are disappearing. Therefore, the rate of appearance of $\mathrm{NO}_{2}$ is four times the rate of appearance of $\mathrm{O}_{2}$. The rate of disappearance of $\mathrm{N}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{5}$ is twice the rate of appearance of $\mathrm{O}_{2}$.
So, the rate of reaction irrespective of the species selected is expressed in the following form,
Rate of reaction $=-\frac{\Delta\left[\mathrm{N}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{5}\right]}{2 \Delta t}=+\frac{\Delta\left[\mathrm{NO}_{2}\right]}{4 \Delta t}=+\frac{\Delta\left[\mathrm{O}_{2}\right]}{\Delta t}$
In brief, the rate of reaction can be equated for any species (either reactant or product) by just dividing the rate of change of concentration by the stoichiometric coefficient of reactant or product expressed by the reaction.
Similarly, for the reaction
$$
\mathrm{N}_{2}(\mathrm{~g})+3 \mathrm{H}_{2}(\mathrm{~g}) \rightleftharpoons 2 \mathrm{NH}_{3}(\mathrm{~g})
$$
the rate of reaction can be represented by
$$
-\frac{\Delta\left[\mathrm{N}_{2}\right]}{\Delta t}=-\frac{\Delta\left[\mathrm{H}_{2}\right]}{3 \Delta t}=+\frac{\Delta\left[\mathrm{NH}_{3}\right]}{2 \Delta t}
$$
So, we can sum up the important points to express the rate of reaction as follows :
(i) The rate of a reaction is expressed by the change in concentration divided by the time taken for the change.
(ii) The rate of reaction can be expessed in terms of the reactant or the product both. Since molar concentration of reactant decreases with time and that of the product increases with time, the change in molar concentration of reactant is negative whereas that of product is positive.
(iii) The different expressions for the rate can be made equivalent by dividing the rate expression by the stoichiometric coefficient shown by the balanced chemical equation.
## Units of The Rate of Reaction
The units of the rate of reaction depend on the unit used to express the concentration of the reactant or product.
(i) Concentration expressed in moles litre ${ }^{-1}$
The concentration is usually expressed in moles litre ${ }^{-1}$ and the time in seconds or minutes, Therefore, the unit of the rate of reaction is moles litre ${ }^{-1} \mathrm{sec}^{-1}$ or moles litre ${ }^{-1} \mathrm{~min}^{-1}$
(ii) Concentration expressed in terms of partial pressure.
When the concentration of either gaseous reactant or product or both is expressed in atmosphere then the unit of the rate of reaction is given as atmosphere per second ( $\mathrm{atm} \mathrm{sec}^{-1}$ )

## AVERAGE RATE AND INSTANTANEOUS RATE
Since the rate of reaction does not remain constant throughout the reaction time the average rate over the time, $\Delta t$ discussed in above expressions is used as the rate of reaction. However, remember that the average rate does not specify the true rate of reaction at a particular time. So, a new term instantaneous rate of reaction is used to specify the rate of reaction at a particular instant. Therefore, the instantaneous rate may be defined as the rate of change of concentration of any one of the reactants or products at a specific instant of time.
To get instantaneous rate, the time interval ( $\Delta t$ ) is made infinitesimally small ( $\Delta t \rightarrow 0$ ) so that the change in concentration during that time interval remains almost uniform, i.e., the concentration changes when $\Delta t$ approaches zero. Mathematically, instantaneous rate may be expressed by the expression $\frac{\mathrm{d} x}{\mathrm{~d} t}$ instead of $\frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t}$, where $\mathrm{d} t$ is infinitesimally small time change. For example, for a gaseous reaction
$$
2 \mathrm{SO}_{2}(\mathrm{~g})+\mathrm{O}_{2}(\mathrm{~g}) \longrightarrow 2 \mathrm{SO}_{3}(\mathrm{~g})
$$
Average rate is given as,
$$
-\frac{1}{2} \frac{\Delta\left[\mathrm{SO}_{2}\right]}{\Delta t}=-\frac{\Delta\left[\mathrm{O}_{2}\right]}{\Delta t}=+\frac{1}{2} \frac{\Delta\left[\mathrm{SO}_{3}\right]}{\Delta t}
$$
But the instantaneous rate,
$$
\text { As } t \rightarrow 0 \frac{\Delta\left[\mathrm{O}_{2}\right]}{\Delta t}=\frac{\mathrm{d}\left[\mathrm{O}_{2}\right]}{\mathrm{d} t}
$$
Hence the instantaneous rate expression for the reaction will be,
$$
-\frac{1}{2} \frac{\Delta\left[\mathrm{SO}_{2}\right]}{\Delta t}=-\frac{\Delta\left[\mathrm{O}_{2}\right]}{\Delta t}=+\frac{1}{2} \frac{\Delta\left[\mathrm{SO}_{3}\right]}{\Delta t}
$$
A clear distinction between average rate and instantaneous rate can be made by following example. Suppose you travelled 10 km distance in 15 min , then your speed is $40 \mathrm{~km} / \mathrm{h}$. It is average speed and it is not necessary that you travelled with this speed all the time. Instantaneous speed is your speed at a particular moment and it can be calculated by dividing 15 minutes into infinitely small time intervals.

## FACTORS AFFECTING RATE OF A REACTION
The rate of a chemical reaction is not a fixed quantity and varies with the experimental conditions. The rate of reaction depends on the following factors :
(a) Effect of Concentration
(b) Effect of Temperature
(c) Effect of Catalyst
(d) Effect of Light
(e) Effect of Surface Area
(a) Effect of Concentration on Reaction Rate
The rate of reaction increases as the concentration of reactant increases and vice versa.
Rate $\propto$ Concentration of reactant
Example: $\mathrm{Zn}(\mathrm{s})+\underset{\text { Zinc }}{\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{SO}_{4}(\mathrm{aq})} \longrightarrow \underset{\text { Zulphuric acid }}{\mathrm{ZnSO}_{4}(\mathrm{aq})}+\mathrm{H}_{2}(\mathrm{~g})$
On increasing the conc. of $\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{SO}_{4}$ the rate of evolution of $\mathrm{H}_{2}$ will increase.
Different reactants can affect the rate of a particular reaction to different extents.
$$
\begin{gathered}
2 \mathrm{NO}(\mathrm{~g}) \\
\text { Nitric oxide }
\end{gathered}+\mathrm{O}_{2}(\mathrm{~g}) \longrightarrow \begin{gathered}
2 \mathrm{NO}_{2}(\mathrm{~g}) \\
\text { Nitrogen dioxide }
\end{gathered}
$$
(i) The rate of this reaction becomes double when concentration of $\mathrm{O}_{2}$ is doubled.
(ii) The rate of this reaction becomes four times or quadruples when the concentration of NO is doubled.
Reason : For a reaction to occur, the reactant molecules must collide with each other. The probability of such collisions increases with increase in concentration of reactants.Thus, more is the number of collisions taking place per unit volume, more will be the rate of reaction.
$$
\text { i.e } \quad \text { Rate of reaction } \propto \text { [Reactants] }
$$
## Rate Law and Rate Constant
The mathematical expression which denotes the experimentally observed rate of a reaction in terms of the concentrations of the reacting species which influence the rate of reaction is termed as rate law.
Consider a general reaction
$$
a \mathrm{~A}+b \mathrm{~B}+c \mathrm{C} \longrightarrow \text { Products }
$$
Where $\mathrm{A}, \mathrm{B}$ and C are the reactants and $a, b$ and $c$ are stoichiometric coefficients of the balanced equation.
Rate of above reaction
$$
\begin{aligned}
\text { Rate } & =k[\mathrm{~A}]^{a}[\mathrm{~B}]^{b}[\mathrm{C}]^{c} \\
k & =\text { Rate constant }
\end{aligned}
$$
For example
Rate law expression :
$$
2 \mathrm{NO}_{2}(\mathrm{~g})+\mathrm{F}_{2}(\mathrm{~g}) \longrightarrow 2 \mathrm{NO}_{2} \mathrm{~F}(\mathrm{~g})
$$
$$
\text { Rate }=k\left[\mathrm{NO}_{2}\right]\left[\mathrm{F}_{2}\right]
$$
Rate law expression according to law of mass action; Rate $=k\left[\mathrm{NO}_{2}\right]^{2}\left[\mathrm{~F}_{2}\right]$
Did You Know?
$\boldsymbol{a}, \boldsymbol{b}$ and $\boldsymbol{c}$ are coefficients of reactants in reaction. It is not necessary that they represent true
powers of concentration of these reactants that should be determined experimentally.
If all the concentration of reactants are taken as unity i.e. $[\mathrm{A}]=[\mathrm{B}]=1$ mole/litre then Rate $=k$; where rate is termed as the specific reaction rate and $k$ is called as rate constant. Thus rate constant may be defined as the rate of reaction when the concentration of each reactant is taken as unity. Therefore, the rate constant is also referred as specific reaction rate.
## Order of a Reaction
Order of a reaction is an important parameter which is always determined experimentally. Let us consider a hypothetical reaction
$$
a \mathrm{X}+b \mathrm{Y}+c \mathrm{Z} \rightarrow \text { Product }
$$
Where $\mathrm{X}, \mathrm{Y}$ and Z are the reactants and $a, b$ and $c$ are the stoichiometric coefficients. Rate law expression for this reaction based on the experimental determination is
$$
\text { Rate }=k[\mathrm{X}]^{l}[\mathrm{Y}]^{m}[\mathrm{Z}]^{n}
$$
Where $k$ is the rate constant and $[\mathrm{X}],[\mathrm{Y}]$ and $[\mathrm{Z}]$ are molar concentrations of the reactants $\mathrm{X}, \mathrm{Y}$ and Z respectively. The exponents $l, m$ and $n$ are the order with respect to $\mathrm{X}, \mathrm{Y}$ and Z respectively.
The sum of exponents $l, m, n$ is known as the overall order of the reaction.
$$
\text { Order of reaction }=l+m+n
$$
These power or exponents ( $l, m$ or $n$ ) have no relation with the stoichiometric coefficients $a, b$ and $c$ of reactants. Following examples illsutrate the order of reaction.
| | Reaction | Rate law | Reaction order |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| 1. | $2 \mathrm{~N}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{5} \longrightarrow 4 \mathrm{NO}_{2}+\mathrm{O}_{2}$ | Rate $=k\left[\mathrm{~N}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{5}\right]$ | 1 |
| 2. | $\mathrm{H}_{2}+\mathrm{I}_{2} \longrightarrow 2 \mathrm{HI}$ | Rate $=k\left[\mathrm{H}_{2}\right]\left[\mathrm{l}_{2}\right]$ | $1+1=2$ |
| 3. | $2 \mathrm{NO}_{2} \longrightarrow 2 \mathrm{NO}+\mathrm{O}_{2}$ | Rate $=k\left[\mathrm{NO}_{2}\right]_{2}$ | 2 |
| 4. | $2 \mathrm{NO}+2 \mathrm{H}_{2} \longrightarrow \mathrm{~N}_{2}+2 \mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}$ | Rate $=k\left[\mathrm{H}_{2}\right][\mathrm{NO}]_{2}$ | $1+2=3$ |
| 5. | $2 \mathrm{NO}+\mathrm{O}_{2} \longrightarrow 2 \mathrm{NO}_{2}$ | Rate $=k[\mathrm{NO}]_{2}\left[\mathrm{O}_{2}\right]$ | $2+1=3$ |
| 6. | $\mathrm{CHCl}_{3}+\mathrm{Cl}_{2} \longrightarrow \mathrm{CCl}_{4}+\mathrm{HCl}$ | Rate $=k\left[\mathrm{CHCl}_{3}\right]\left[\mathrm{Cl}_{2}\right]^{1 / 2}$ | $1+1 / 2=1 \frac{1}{2}$ |
| 7. | $\mathrm{H}_{2}+\mathrm{Br}_{2} \longrightarrow 2 \mathrm{HBr}$ | Rate $=k\left[\mathrm{H}_{2}\right]\left[\mathrm{Br}_{2}\right]^{1 / 2}$ | $1+1 / 2=1 \frac{1}{2}$ |
Thus the order of a reaction can be defined as the sum of the powers of the concentration terms, as expressed in rate law.
## Molecularity of a Reaction
The molecularity of a reaction is defined as the total number of molecules of the reactants taking part in a reaction, i.e., the molecularity represents the number of reactant molecules undergoing collisions to bring about the chemical reaction. Thus in the reaction,
$$
\begin{gathered}
a \mathrm{~A}+b \mathrm{~B} \longrightarrow \text { Product. } \\
\text { Molecularity }=a+b
\end{gathered}
$$
The molecularity of the reaction may be $1,2,3$ etc. according to the participation of one, two or three reactant molecules in the reaction.

## (b) Effect of Temperature on Reaction Rate
Generally reactions take place much faster at a higher temperature. It means on increasing temperature rate of reaction increases.
$$
\text { Rate } \propto \text { Temperature }
$$
Reason : (i) As the temperature increases, the kinetic energy of the reactant molecules increases, hence, the number of collisions also increases.
(ii) At higher temperature the reactant molecules have greater chance of energy greater than the activation energy.
Note : Activation energy is the minimum amount of energy required to initiate the reaction.
Example : $\underset{\substack{\text { Calcium } \\ \text { carbonate }}}{\mathrm{CaCO}_{3}(\mathrm{~s})}+\underset{\substack{\text { Hydrochloric } \\ \text { acid }}}{2 \mathrm{HCl}(\mathrm{aq})} \longrightarrow \underset{\substack{\text { Calcium } \\ \text { chloride }}}{\mathrm{CaCl}_{2}(\mathrm{aq})}+\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}(\mathrm{l})+\mathrm{CO}_{2}(\mathrm{~g})$
At 273 K the evolution of $\mathrm{CO}_{2}$ is very slow, but at 323 K the evolution of $\mathrm{CO}_{2}$ is rapid.
This graph shows that at higher temperature rate of evolution of $\mathrm{CO}_{2}$ is also high.
The reactants do not change directly in the products, reactants first absorb a certain amount of energy above the average potential energy of the reactants so that they overcome energy barrier between reactants and products. Thus this minimum amount of energy required to overcome this energy barrier between reactant and product is called activation energy ( $E_{\mathrm{a}}$ ). For fast reactions, activation energies are low whereas for slow reactions, activation energies are high.
Activation energy $=$ [Threshold energy] - [Average energy of the reactants]
$$
E_{\mathrm{a}}=E_{\mathrm{t}}-E_{\mathrm{r}}
$$
$E_{\mathrm{a}}$-Activation energy
$E_{\mathrm{t}}$ - Threshold energy
$E_{\mathrm{r}}$ - Average energy of reactant
$E_{\mathrm{p}}$ - Energy of products
Note : The minimum amount of energy which the colliding reactant molecules must possess to react is known as threshold energy.

## (c) Effect of Catalyst
A catalyst is a foreign substance which is added to reaction mixture to change the rate of chemical reaction (either increases or decreases) without itself undergoing any change. A catalyst is recovered unchanged in mass and composition at the end of the reaction.
e.g.
$$
2 \mathrm{KClO}_{3} \xrightarrow{\mathrm{MnO}_{2}} 2 \mathrm{KCl}+3 \mathrm{O}_{2}
$$
Here, $\mathrm{MnO}_{2}$ is acting as a catalyst to increase the rate of reaction.

## ENERGY CHANGES IN CHEMICAL REACTIONS
It is common observation that when chemical reactions occur, they are accompanied by energy changes. Depending upon the evolution or absorption of energy, the chemical reactions can be classified into three types : exothermic, endothermic and photochemical reactions.
## (a) Exothermic Reactions
The chemical reactions which proceed with the evolution of heat energy are called exothermic reactions.
The heat energy produced during the reaction is indicated by writing $+q$ on the product side. In general, exothermic reactions may be represented as :
$$
\mathrm{A}+\mathrm{B} \longrightarrow \mathrm{C}+\mathrm{D}+q \text { (heat energy) }
$$
The heat evolved is expressed in the units of Joules (J) or kilo Joules (kJ).
Example : When one mole of carbon (coal) is burnt in oxygen, there is an evolution of 393.5 kJ of energy. It can be expressed as :
$$
\mathrm{C}(\mathrm{~s})+\mathrm{O}_{2}(\mathrm{~g}) \longrightarrow \mathrm{CO}_{2}(\mathrm{~g})+393.5 \mathrm{~kJ}
$$
(i) All combustion reactions are exothermic reactions.
(ii) The number of exothermic reactions are more than endothermic reactions.
(iii) During exothermic reaction, a part of the potential energy possessed by the reactants is released. Hence, products of an exothermic reaction have less potential energy than the reactants.
## (b) Endothermic Reactions
The chemical reactions which proceed with the absorption of heat energy are called endothermic reactions.
The heat energy absorbed during the reaction can be indicated by writing $+q$ with the reactants or it can be indicated by writing $-q$ (or the actual numerical value) with the products. In general, an endothermic reaction can be represented as,
$$
\mathrm{A}+\mathrm{B}+q(\text { heat }) \longrightarrow \mathrm{C}+\mathrm{D}
$$
or
$$
\mathrm{A}+\mathrm{B} \longrightarrow \mathrm{C}+\mathrm{D}-q \text { (heat), where } q \text { is the heat absorbed }
$$
Example : Formation of nitric oxide from nitrogen and oxygen proceeds with the absorption of 180.5 kJ of heat. It can be represented as :
$$
\mathrm{N}_{2}(\mathrm{~g})+\mathrm{O}_{2}(\mathrm{~g}) \longrightarrow 2 \mathrm{NO}(\mathrm{~g})-180.5 \mathrm{~kJ}
$$
(i) Decomposition reactions are generally endothermic.
(ii) The number of endothermic reactions is much less than the exothermic reactions.
(iii) During endothermic reactions, reactants gain energy. Hence, products of an endothermic reaction have more potential energy than the reactants.

## Explanation for the energy changes during chemical reactions :
A chemical reaction involves the rearrangement of atoms. During the reaction certain bonds are broken while certain new bonds are formed between the atoms. Energy is absorbed for breaking the bonds and released during formation of bonds. If the energy required to break the bonds is more than the energy during formation of bonds then there is net absorption of energy and the reaction is endothermic. On the other hand, if the energy released during formation of bonds is more than the energy absorbed for breaking the bonds, then there is net release of energy and the reaction is exothermic.
## (c) Photochemical Reactions
The reactions which are either slow in dark or do not proceed, but takes place in the presence of radiations like sunlight or ultraviolet light. Such reactions are called photochemical reactions.
## Importance of photochemical reactions
(i) Photosynthesis is very common example of reaction occurring in plants by the light.
(ii) Light is used in the synthesis and manufacture of many organic compounds. This is called photochemical synthesis. For example, formaldehyde is synthesised from CO and $\mathrm{H}_{2}$ in the presence of Hg using light.
(iii) Polymerisation is generally initiated by light.
(iv) Photography is based upon photochemistry.
(v) Photo etching is used in electronic industry.
(vi) Printing technology also makes use of photochemical processes.
(vii) Sterilisation of water.
- Damaging effects of photochemistry-The most common damaging effect of photochemistry is the effect of sunlight on the dyes used for colouring of fabrics and other materials. Due to the effect of sunlight, the coloured fabrics or materials fade away.
- Some substances absorb radiation when exposed to it and immediately re-emit the absorbed energy. It is called fluorescence e.g., fluorite ( $\mathrm{CaF}_{2}$ ). Emission of light during a chemical reaction is known as chemiluminescence e.g., glowing of phosphorus in dark (due to oxidation).
- It may be noted that generally ultra-violet or visible radiations are used for carrying out photochemical reactions because their photons possess energies approximately of the order of 420 kJ per mol which is comparable to most of the bond energies. Thus, ultra-violet radiation energy is capable of breaking the bonds. On the other hand, infra-red radiations are generally not used because their photons possess energy of the order of 60 kJ per mol which is quite less for breaking of bonds.
## More
Photosensitization : Some substances do not react themselves even when exposed to sunlight. However, these can be made to react by the addition of a small quantity of a foreign substance. The role of the foreign substance is to absorb light energy and subsequently transfer it to one of the reactants. Such substance which can absorb light energy and thus induce a photochemical change in the reactants without itself taking part in the reaction is called a photosensitizer and the phenomenon is termed as photosensitization.
