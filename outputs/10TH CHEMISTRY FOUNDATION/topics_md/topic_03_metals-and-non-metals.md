## Physical properties of metals
- Metallic lusture
- Usually solid (except Hg which is a liquid)
- Show ductility and malleability.
- Good conductor of heat and electricity.
## Chemical properties of metals
Chemical properties of metals
- Form basic oxides with oxygen (Al and Zn forms amphoteric oxides)
- Form metal oxide and $\mathrm{H}_{2}$ gas with $\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}$
- Na and K reacts violently with cold water.
- Mg reacts only with hot water.
- $\mathrm{Al}, \mathrm{Zn}$ and Fe react with steam.
- Form salts and $\mathrm{H}_{2}$ gas with acids.
- Reactive metals displaces less reactive metals from their solution.

- Metal loses the valence electrons while non-metal accepts these electron to form ionic bond.
- Properties of ionic compounds :

- Soluble in water, insoluble in kerosene, petrol etc.
- Conduct electricity in molten state and not in solid state.

## Physical Properties of Metals
Some of the physical properties of metals are listed below.
(a) They have a shining surface called lustre when freshly prepared.
(b) They are ductile (they can be drawn into wires) and malleable (they can be beaten into thin sheets, due to this property metals can be rolled into sheets)
(c) They usually have a high melting points.
(d) They are usually hard and solid at room temperature.
(e) Metals are generally good conductors of heat, electricity and sound.
(f) Metals have high tensile strength and density.
Alkali and alkaline earth metals are extremely reactive, and are rarely encountered in their elemental or metallic form. Some metals like sodium are so soft that they can be cut even with knife. Some metals are magnetic in nature such as iron, cobalt and nickel. Steel is also magnetic in nature since it is an alloy with major composition of iron. Whereas, other metals are not magnetic.

Noble metals : Noble metals are those metals which are resistant to corrosion or oxidation, unlike most base metals. They are referred to noble metals, often due to their inertness. Examples include tantalum, gold, platinum, and rhodium. Precious metals: A precious metal is a rare metallic chemical element of high economic value. Chemically, the precious metals are less reactive than most elements, they have high lustre and high electrical conductivity. Historically, precious metals were important as currency, but are now regarded mainly as investment and industrial commodities. The best-known precious metals are gold and silver. While both have industrial uses, they are better known for their uses in art, jewellery, and coinage. Other precious metals include the platinum group metals: ruthenium, rhodium, palladium, osmium, iridium, and platinum, of which platinum is the most widely traded.
Base metals : In chemistry, the term 'base metal' is used informally to refer to a metal that oxidizes or corrodes relatively easily, and reacts variably with dilute hydrochloric acid $(\mathrm{HCl})$ to form hydrogen. Examples include iron, nickel, lead and zinc. Copper is considered as a base metal because it oxidizes relatively easily, although it does not react with HCl .
Ferrous metals : The term "ferrous" is derived from the latin word meaning "containing iron". This can include pure iron, such as wrought iron, or an alloy such as steel. Ferrous metals are often magnetic in nature.

Alloy : An alloy is a mixture of two or more elements in solid state in which the major component is a metal. Most pure metals are either too soft, brittle or chemically reactive for practical use. Combining different ratios of metals in alloys modify the properties of pure metals to produce desirable characteristics. The aim of making alloys is generally to make them less brittle, harder, resistant to corrosion, or have a more desirable color and lustre. Examples of alloys are steel (iron and carbon), brass (copper and zinc), bronze (copper and tin), and duralumin (aluminium and copper). Alloys specially designed for highly demanding applications, such as jet engines, may contain more than ten elements. Stainless steel ( $\mathrm{Fe}+\mathrm{C}+\mathrm{Cr}+\mathrm{Ni}$ ) is used where resistance to corrosion is important. Al and Mg alloys are used for strength and light applications. Nickel based super alloys are used in high temperature appliances such as turbochargers, pressure vessels and heat exchangers. Generally, electrical conductivity of an alloy is less than that of pure metal.
Alloys have wide domestic and industrial applications, as alloy formation enhances the applicability of metals in various fields.

## Chemical Properties of Metals
## 1. Metal burns in air :
Many metals burn in the oxygen of air to produce a metal oxide
Metal + Oxygen → Metal oxide
The more reactive a metal is, the more violent the reaction when it is burnt in oxygen or air.
Example : Magnesium + Oxygen → Magnesium oxide
$$
2 \mathrm{Mg}(\mathrm{~s})+\mathrm{O}_{2}(\mathrm{~g}) \rightarrow 2 \mathrm{MgO}(\mathrm{~s})
$$
Many metals tarnish in air. The metal slowly reacts with the oxygen of the air forming a dull layer of the metal oxide on the surface of the metal.
Very unreactive metals like gold and silver do not tarnish, they stay shiny.
The reactivity series :
$\left.\begin{array}{l}\mathrm{K}-\text { Potassium } \\ \mathrm{Na}-\text { Sodium } \\ \mathrm{Ca}-\text { Calcium } \\ \mathrm{Mg}-\text { Magnesium }\end{array}\right]$ Burn very easily with a bright flame $\left.\begin{array}{ll}\text { Al-Aluminium } & \left.\begin{array}{l}\text { Zn-Zinc } \\ \text { Fe-Iron } \\ \text { Pb-Lead } \\ \text { Cu-Copper }\end{array}\right] \text { React slowly with air when heated } \\ \end{array} \quad \begin{array}{l}\text { Ag-Silver } \\ \text { Au-Gold }\end{array}\right]$ No reaction
Metal oxides are usually basic in nature but there are some metal oxides which show both acidic and basic behaviour. i.e. they react with both acids and bases. These are called amphoteric oxides. One of the most common example of an amphoteric oxide is $\mathrm{Al}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}$. It reacts in the following manner with acids and bases -
$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{Al}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}+6 \mathrm{HCl} & \longrightarrow 2 \mathrm{AlCl}_{3}+3 \mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O} \\
\mathrm{Al}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}+2 \mathrm{NaOH} & \longrightarrow 2 \mathrm{NaAlO}_{2}+3 \mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}
\end{aligned}
$$
Zinc, tin, lead and beryllium can also form amphoteric oxides.
Most metal oxides are insoluble in water but some of these dissolve in water to form alkali. For example, sodium oxide and potassium oxide dissolve in water to produce alkali as follows,
$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{Na}_{2} \mathrm{O}(\mathrm{~s})+\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}(\mathrm{l}) & \longrightarrow 2 \mathrm{NaOH}(\mathrm{aq}) \\
\mathrm{K}_{2} \mathrm{O}(\mathrm{~s})+\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}(\mathrm{l}) & \longrightarrow 2 \mathrm{KOH}(\mathrm{aq})
\end{aligned}
$$
All metals do not react with oxygen at same rate, some metals are more reactive than others. This is because very reactive metals lose electrons easily. Metals such as sodium are very reactive and explosive in air. Hence, to protect them and to prevent accidental fires, they are kept immersed in kerosene oil. Whereas, metals such as gold are very unreactive, and therefore do not corrode or tarnish in air.

2. Reaction of metal with water :
Metals react with water and produce a metal oxide and hydrogen gas. Metal oxides that are soluble in water dissolve in it to form metal hydroxides. But all metals do not react with water.
$$
\begin{aligned}
& \text { Metal + Water } \text { ⟶ Metal oxide + Hydrogen } \\
& \text { Metal oxide + Water } \text { ⟶ Metal hydroxide }
\end{aligned}
$$
Metals like potassium and sodium react violently with cold water. In case of sodium and potassium, the reaction is so violent and exothermic that the evolved hydrogen immediately catches fire.
$$
\begin{aligned}
2 \mathrm{~K}(\mathrm{~s})+2 \mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}(\mathrm{l}) & \longrightarrow 2 \mathrm{KOH}(\mathrm{aq})+\mathrm{H}_{2}(\mathrm{~g})+\text { Heat energy } \\
2 \mathrm{Na}(\mathrm{~s})+2 \mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}(\mathrm{l}) & \longrightarrow 2 \mathrm{NaOH}(\mathrm{aq})+\mathrm{H}_{2}(\mathrm{~g})+\text { Heat energy }
\end{aligned}
$$
The reaction of calcium with water is less violent. The heat evolved is not sufficient for the hydrogen to catch fire.
$$
\mathrm{Ca}(\mathrm{~s})+2 \mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}(\mathrm{l}) \longrightarrow \mathrm{Ca}(\mathrm{OH})_{2}(\mathrm{aq})+\mathrm{H}_{2}(\mathrm{~g})
$$
Calcium starts floating because the bubbles of hydrogen gas formed stick to the surface of the metal.
Magnesium does not react with cold water. It reacts with hot water to form magnesium hydroxide and hydrogen. It also starts floating due to the bubbles of hydrogen gas sticking to its surface.
Metals like aluminium, iron and zinc do not react either with cold or hot water. But they react with steam to form the metal oxide and hydrogen.
Eg. Magnesium + Steam ⟶ Magnesium oxide + Hydrogen
$$
\begin{aligned}
& \mathrm{Mg}(\mathrm{~s})+\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}(\mathrm{~g}) \longrightarrow \mathrm{MgO}(\mathrm{~s})+\mathrm{H}_{2}(\mathrm{~g}) \\
& 2 \mathrm{Al}(\mathrm{~s})+3 \mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}(\mathrm{~g}) \longrightarrow \mathrm{Al}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}(\mathrm{~s})+3 \mathrm{H}_{2}(\mathrm{~g}) \\
& 3 \mathrm{Fe}(\mathrm{~s})+4 \mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}(\mathrm{~g}) \longrightarrow \mathrm{Fe}_{3} \mathrm{O}_{4}(\mathrm{~s})+4 \mathrm{H}_{2}(\mathrm{~g})
\end{aligned}
$$
Metals such as lead, copper, silver and gold do not react with water at all.
Freshly cut sodium has a metallic shine. However, it reacts with water (present in air) to form NaOH which forms a layer on the exposed metallic surface. The reaction so strongly exothermic that the piece of sodium melts (melting point of sodium: $97.8{ }^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ ). The heat transferred to the local environment ignites the hydrogen that is produced. The hydrogen burns with a bright flame. This is why elemental sodium is stored under kerosene oil or a similar substance.
$$
\text { Sodium reacts with water : } \quad 2 \mathrm{Na}+2 \mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O} \longrightarrow 2 \mathrm{NaOH}+\mathrm{H}_{2}
$$
The phenolphthalein solution indicator shows that sodium hydroxide is produced. The solution changes to deep red.

## 3. Reaction of metal with acids :
When an acid reacts with metal, a salt and hydrogen are produced:
For example :
$$
\text { Acid + Metal } \longrightarrow \text { Salt + Hydrogen }
$$
Sulfuric acid + Calcium ⟶ Calcium sulfate + Hydrogen
The salt that is produced depends upon the reactivity of metals with acids. Potassium, sodium, lithium and calcium all react violently with dilute sulphuric acid and dilute hydrochloric acid. It is dangerous to put these metals into an acid. The reaction is similar to the reaction with water, forming the metal salt (either sulphate or chloride) and hydrogen gas.
For example :
$$
\begin{aligned}
& \text { Sodium }+ \text { Hydrochloric acid } \longrightarrow \text { Sodium chloride }+ \text { Hydrogen } \\
& 2 \mathrm{Na}(\mathrm{~s})+2 \mathrm{HCl}(\mathrm{aq}) \quad \longrightarrow 2 \mathrm{NaCl}(\mathrm{aq}) \quad+\quad \mathrm{H}_{2}(\mathrm{~g})
\end{aligned}
$$
Magnesium, aluminium, zinc, iron, tin and lead react safely with dilute acid. Magnesium is the fastest and lead is the slowest among the six.
The reaction of zinc with sulphuric acid is often used to make a small amount of hydrogen in the laboratory.
$$
\mathrm{Zn}(\mathrm{~s})+\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{SO}_{4}(\mathrm{aq}) \longrightarrow \mathrm{ZnSO}_{4}(\mathrm{aq})+\mathrm{H}_{2}(\mathrm{~g}) \uparrow
$$
The reaction is slow at room temperature but its rate can be increased by the addition of a small amount of copper(II) sulphate. Zinc displaces copper metal, which acts as a catalyst.
Hydrogen gas is not evolved when a metal reacts with nitric acid. It is because $\mathrm{HNO}_{3}$ is a strong oxidising agent. It oxidises the $H_{2}$ produced to water and itself gets reduced to any of the nitrogen oxides ( $\mathrm{N}_{2} \mathrm{O}, \mathrm{NO}, \mathrm{NO}_{2}$ ). But magnesium ( Mg ) and manganese (Mn) react with very dilute $\mathrm{HNO}_{3}$ to evolve $\mathrm{H}_{2}$ gas.
Metals react with dil HCl to form corresponding chloride salt and hydrogen which is evolved as bubbles. The rate of formation of bubbles was the fastest in case of magnesium and the reaction is exothermic. The reactivity decreases in the order $\mathrm{Mg}>\mathrm{Al}>\mathrm{Zn}>\mathrm{Fe}$. In the case of copper, no bubbles were seen and the temperature also remains unchanged. This shows that copper does not react with dilute HCl .

## 4. Reaction of metals with nitric acid :
Dilute nitric acid is added to zinc, aluminium and copper. Immediately thereafter, zinc and aluminum show no reaction. Copper, on the other hand, reacts immediately and produces brown $\mathrm{NO}_{2}$ according to the following reaction.
$$
\mathrm{Cu}+2 \mathrm{HNO}_{3}+2 \mathrm{H}^{+} \longrightarrow \mathrm{Cu}^{2+}+2 \mathrm{NO}_{2}+2 \mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}
$$
Aluminum and zinc do not react even with concentrated nitric acid since, a thick and hard to dissolve, oxidative layer builds (passive oxidation) on the metal, that protects the metal against further assaults.
Acids are often used to dissolve metals or organic material. However, certain material are more difficult to dissolve. They require more potent and specialized acid solutions like aqua regia.
Aqua regia consists of 3 parts HCl to 1 part $\mathrm{HNO}_{3}$, it is an excellent oxidant, can dissolve gold and platinum, it produces yellow fumes to produce nitrosyl chloride, NOCl, chlorine gas, $\mathrm{Cl}_{2}$, and water, $\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}$.
Acid Piranha consists $\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{SO}_{4}$ and $\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{2}$ in the ratios, $3: 1$ to $7: 1$, it is also an excellent oxidant and removes trace amount of organic residues.
For preparing piranha always add peroxide to sulfuric acid, never vice versa. This is a selfheating solution that may even boil when mixed.
## 5. Reaction of metal with solution of other metal salts:
A metal will displace (take the place of) a less reactive metal in a metal salt solution.
For example,
$$
\begin{aligned}
& \text { Iron + Copper(II) sulphate } \longrightarrow \text { Iron sulphate + Copper. } \\
& \mathrm{Fe}(\mathrm{~s})+\mathrm{CuSO}_{4}(\mathrm{aq}) \quad \longrightarrow \mathrm{FeSO}_{4}(\mathrm{aq})+\mathrm{Cu}(\mathrm{~s}) \downarrow
\end{aligned}
$$
Copper(II) sulphate is blue, iron sulphate is pale green in colour.
During the reaction the blue solution loses its colour and the iron metal is seen to turn pink-brown as the displaced copper becomes deposited on it.
If a less reactive metal is added to a metal salt solution, there will be no reaction - nothing will happen.
For example, iron is less reactive than magnesium.
Iron + Magnesium sulphate ⟶ No reaction
In these displacement reactions the metals are competing for the non-metal anion. Reactions using chlorides or nitrates can also be used.
The order of the metals in the reactivity series can be worked out by using these type of reactions.
For example, tin would be seen to displace lead from lead chloride but would not react with iron(II) chloride.
$$
\begin{aligned}
& \text { Tin }+ \text { Lead chloride } \longrightarrow \text { Tin chloride }+ \text { Lead } \\
& \mathrm{Sn}(\mathrm{~s})+\mathrm{PbCl}_{2}(\mathrm{aq}) \longrightarrow \mathrm{SnCl}_{2}(\mathrm{aq})+\mathrm{Pb}(\mathrm{~s}) \\
& \text { Tin }+ \text { Iron }(\mathrm{II}) \text { chloride } \longrightarrow \text { No reaction } \\
& \mathrm{Sn}(\mathrm{~s})+\mathrm{FeCl}_{2}(\mathrm{aq}) \longrightarrow \text { No reaction }
\end{aligned}
$$
Therefore, tin must be above lead but below iron in the reactivity series.
Copper would be seen to displace silver from silver nitrate but would not react with lead nitrate.
$$
\begin{aligned}
& \text { Copper }+ \text { Silver nitrate } \longrightarrow \text { Copper nitrate }+ \text { Silver } \\
& \mathrm{Cu}(\mathrm{~s})+2 \mathrm{AgNO}_{3}(\mathrm{aq}) \longrightarrow \mathrm{Cu}\left(\mathrm{NO}_{3}\right)_{2}(\mathrm{aq})+2 \mathrm{Ag}(\mathrm{~s}) \\
& \text { Copper }+ \text { Lead nitrate } \longrightarrow \text { No reaction } \\
& \mathrm{Cu}(\mathrm{~s})+\mathrm{Pb}\left(\mathrm{NO}_{3}\right)_{2}(\mathrm{aq}) \longrightarrow \text { No reaction }
\end{aligned}
$$
Therefore, copper must be above silver but below lead in the reactivity series.

CASE III : Few metals like Na, Mg, Al and Zn reacts easily with dilute HCl whereas copper and silver do not react with dilute HCl or even with dilute $\mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{SO}_{4}$. Why?
All those metals which are more reactive viz. lose electron more easily than hydrogen, displaces hydrogen from dilute acids to produce hydrogen gas. This happens because the more reactive metal gives electron easily and these electrons reduce the hydrogen ion of the acids to $\mathrm{H}_{2}$ gas. So, $\mathrm{Na}, \mathrm{Mg}, \mathrm{Al}$ or Zn gives electrons easily required for the reduction while Cu and Ag do not give electrons for the reduction. Therefore, Cu and Ag do not react with acids.

Extraction of ore is a complex process which comprises various steps. So before studying the topic of extraction we should understand the general principles of metallurgy. Although we will study all these principles in detail in class XIIth, for your better understanding a summary is given here.
## GENERAL PRINCIPLES OF METALLURGY
Metallurgy is an art of extracting metals from the ores. The ores are generally associated with a number of impurities and earthy matter. The extraction of metals from ores involves various steps as discussed below :
## 1. Grinding or Pulverisation
The large pieces of ores are broken into small pieces with the help of hammer. These pieces are crushed with crusher and finally powdered in stamp mills. This powdered form of ore is used for further processes.
## 2. Concentration
The ores, obtained from ore mine contain various types of impurities. The removal of various types of impurities from ores is known as concentration. The process by which ore is concentrated depends on the type of ore and gangue. This concentration is carried out by using one or more of the following methods :
(a) Gravity separation or hydraulic washing : This method is based on the difference in the density of ore and the density of impurities. The ore is crushed and finely powdered, sieved and then levigated in a stream of water. The lighter impurities are carried away by the stream of water and heavier ore particles settle down. Ores of tin, lead and iron are concentrated by this process.
## Gravity separation method
(b) Froth floatation process : This process is used in the concentration of sulphide ores like galena ( PbS ), zinc blende (ZnS) etc. The well powdered ore (taken in a tank) is mixed with water to which an oil (pine oil or eucalyptus oil) has been added. The whole mass is agitated by a blast of air sent through pipe as shown in figure. The froth (containing impurities) floats on the surface of the water, while heavy ore particles settle down. The froth along with the ore is removed from the surface of the water.
Froth floatation process for the concentration of sulphide ores.
Froth stabilizers are those chemicals which stabilise the froth e.g. cresols and aniline.
## Collectors :
These compounds attach themselves by polar groups to grains of minerals which then become water repellent and thus pass on into the froth. Examples are ethyl xanthate and potassium ethyl xanthate.
## Activators and depressants :
These compounds activate or depress the floating property of one of the components of the ore and thus help in the separation of different minerals present in the same ore. Copper sulphate is an example of activator, while sodium and potassium cyanides are the examples of depressants.

(c) Electromagnetic separation : This method is used when either the ore or the gangue is magnetic in nature. For example, tin stone contains $\mathrm{Fe}_{3} \mathrm{O}_{4}$ as impurity. The powdered ore is dropped on a moving belt passing over two magnetic rollers. The magnetic impurities (like $\mathrm{Fe}_{3} \mathrm{O}_{4}$ ) get collected in a heap nearer the magnetic rollers, while the ore (free from impurity) is collected in a heap, away from the magnetic rollers.
Some metals are attracted to magnet and can behave as a magnet. Thus Iron, cobalt and nickel are described as magnetic materials.
There are two types of permanent magnets those from "hard" magnetic materials and those from "soft" magnetic materials. Hard magnetic materials tend to stay magnetized over a long period while soft magnetic metals can be magnetized but lose their magnetism quickly.
(d) Leaching : It is a chemical method used for concentration of ore in which crude powdered ore is allowed to react with suitable reagent. Ore reacts with added reagent to form soluble complex while impurities remain undissolved. Insoluble impurities are separated by filtration and ore is reprecipitated from soluble complex.
Note : This process is used for concentration of Al, Ag, Au ores etc.
## 3. Extraction of Metals
Metals are extracted from their concentrated ore by the reduction of metal oxides. The ore is converted to its oxide by one of following methods.
(a) Calcination : Calcination is the process of heating the ore in the presence of limited supply of air to a temperature insufficient to melt it. During calcination, moisture and volatile impurities are removed and the whole mass becomes porous. Here carbonates are converted into oxides by giving out carbon dioxide. Apart from carbonate ores, hydrated ores are also subjected to calcination for the removal of water of crystallization. Some examples are given below:
(i) The metal zinc and calcium are obtained from calamine $\left(\mathrm{ZnCO}_{3}\right)$ and lime stone $\left(\mathrm{CaCO}_{3}\right)$ respectively. When these ores are calcined, they are converted into oxides.
$$
\begin{aligned}
& \mathrm{ZnCO}_{3} \longrightarrow \mathrm{ZnO}+\mathrm{CO}_{2} \\
& \mathrm{CaCO}_{3} \longrightarrow \mathrm{CaO}+\mathrm{CO}_{2}
\end{aligned}
$$
(ii) Haemetite $\left(\mathrm{Fe}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}\right)$ is an ore of iron. Concentrated ore when calcined in a shallow kiln -
(1) Moisture and carbon dioxide are removed.
(2) Ferrous oxide present is oxidised to ferric oxide.
(3) Sulphur is removed as sulphur dioxide gas.
(4) The whole mass becomes porous.
(b) Roasting: It is a process which involves heating of ore in presence of excess of air. In this process, moisture and volatile impurities are removed. The ore itself is converted into oxides.
## Examples :
(i) Zinc blende ( ZnS ), when roasted, is converted into zinc oxide.
$$
2 \mathrm{ZnS}+3 \mathrm{O}_{2} \longrightarrow 2 \mathrm{ZnO}+2 \mathrm{SO}_{2}
$$
(ii) Copper metal is extracted from copper pyrite ore. When this ore is roasted :
(1) Most of the sulphur is removed as sulphur dioxide.
(2) Arsenic and antimony are volatilised.
(3) Copper pyrite ore is converted into sulphides of iron and copper
$$
2 \mathrm{CuFeS}_{2}+\mathrm{O}_{2} \longrightarrow 2 \mathrm{FeS}+\mathrm{Cu}_{2} \mathrm{~S}+\mathrm{SO}_{2}
$$
Partial oxidation of FeS and $\mathrm{Cu}_{2} \mathrm{~S}$ also takes place
$$
\begin{aligned}
2 \mathrm{FeS}+3 \mathrm{O}_{2} & \longrightarrow 2 \mathrm{FeO}+2 \mathrm{SO}_{2} \\
2 \mathrm{Cu}_{2} \mathrm{~S}+3 \mathrm{O}_{2} & \longrightarrow 2 \mathrm{Cu}_{2} \mathrm{O}+2 \mathrm{SO}_{2}
\end{aligned}
$$
Both calcination and roasting may be carried out in same reverberatory furnace. In calcination, air holes are partially or completely closed, while in roasting they are open.

## 4. Reduction of Metal Oxides
(a) Smelting : Calcined or roasted ore is mixed with carbon or any other suitable reducing agent and flux, and heated strongly. Metal oxides are reduced to metal. Smelting is opposite of roasting which involves an oxidizing reaction.
Examples : When tin stone $\left(\mathrm{SnO}_{2}\right)$ is heated strongly with carbon, tin metal is obtained.
$$
\mathrm{SnO}_{2}+2 \mathrm{C} \longrightarrow \mathrm{Sn}+2 \mathrm{CO}
$$
The zinc oxide obtained by calcination of zinc blende, when heated strongly with carbon gives zinc metal.
$$
\mathrm{ZnO}+\mathrm{C} \longrightarrow \mathrm{Zn}+\mathrm{CO}
$$
Similarly, lead metal is obtained by the reduction of lead oxide.
$$
\mathrm{PbO}+\mathrm{C} \longrightarrow \mathrm{~Pb}+\mathrm{CO}
$$

- Carbon is good reducing agent for oxides but not for sulphides.
- Due to cheapness and high availability, carbon is widely used as a reducing agent. The process is known as carbon reduction. The metals which are less electropositive, do not form carbides with carbon, they are reduced by this method e.g. oxides of Ph, Fe, Zn, Sn etc.
- Below $710^{\circ} \mathrm{C}, \mathrm{CO}$ is better reducing agent than carbon and reverse is true at temperature higher than $710^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$.
Flux :
Ores contain some fusible impurities. These impurities are removed by adding certain substances. These substances are called flux. The flux to be used depends on the nature of impurities. If acidic impurities (such as $\mathrm{SiO}_{2}$ ) are present, basic flux (e.g., $\mathrm{CaO}, \mathrm{FeO}, \mathrm{MgO}$ etc.) are mixed and if the impurities present are of basic nature the acidic flux are used.
Slag :
Fusible compounds, formed by the combination of flux and impurities are known as slag. For example, if ferrous oxide is present as impurity in a metal it may be removed by adding silica as a flux. Ferrous oxide is removed as $\mathrm{FeSiO}_{3}$ (slag).
$$
\mathrm{FeO}+\mathrm{SiO}_{2} \longrightarrow \mathrm{FeSiO}_{3}
$$
Generally, slags possess melting points lower than the metals. The slags are generally lighter and hence floats on the surface of molten metals from where it can be skimmed off from time to time.
(b) Aluminothermic process :
There are certain metals whose oxides can not be reduced by carbon. These metals are extracted by aluminothermic process. The metal oxides are thoroughly mixed with aluminium powder and a little $\mathrm{BaO}_{2}$ in a crucible. The reaction is brought about by introducing a lighted magnesium ribbon. Since the reaction is exothermic, temperature reaches to about $3000^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$. Metals like chromium and manganese can be extracted by this method.
$$
\begin{gathered}
\mathrm{Cr}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}+2 \mathrm{Al} \longrightarrow \mathrm{Al}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}+2 \mathrm{Cr} \\
3 \mathrm{MnO}_{2}+4 \mathrm{Al} \longrightarrow 2 \mathrm{Al}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}+3 \mathrm{Mn}
\end{gathered}
$$
The reaction between iron (II) oxide and aluminium is called thermite reaction. Commercially, it is used in welding of iron tracks. It was discovered by German chemist Dr. Hans Goldschmidt. In this reaction, iron oxide (rust) and aluminium metal powder undergo a redox (oxidation-reduction) reaction to form molten iron metal.
$$
\mathrm{Fe}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}(\mathrm{~s})+2 \mathrm{Al}(\mathrm{~s}) \longrightarrow \mathrm{Al}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}(\mathrm{~s})+2 \mathrm{Fe}(\mathrm{l})
$$
In fact, thermite reaction has been used for underwater welding, joining railway tracks or cracked machine parts.
(c) Precipitation method :
Those metals which are more reactive tend to displace less reactive metals. If an iron rod is placed in a copper sulphate solution the copper metal is precipitated and iron goes in solution. Zinc is more reactive than silver and gold in reactivity series and hence it precipitates these metals from their salt solutions. This method is used in the extraction of silver and gold.
## 5. Refining of Metals
The metals obtained by anyone of the above methods are not pure. The metals are refined by the following methods :
(a) By liquation : This method is used for the easily fusible metals like tin etc. Impure metal is placed on the inclined bed of a furnace and heated. The metal melts and flows out leaving behind the impurities.
For example, purification of Sn containing impurities of high m.p. like $\mathrm{Fe}, \mathrm{Cu}, \mathrm{W}$. It is also used for purification of $\mathrm{Pb}, \mathrm{Bi}$ etc.
Purification of tin metal by liquation process
(b) By distillation : Volatile metals like Zn and Hg etc. can be purified by this method. When these metals are heated, their vapours are formed leaving behind the impurities. The vapours are condensed to get pure metals.
(c) By oxidation : If the impurities present in the metals can be easily oxidised, these are removed by this method. Molten metal is stirred thoroughly in the presence of air. The impurities float on the surface of the molten mass in the form of a scum which can be easily separated.
(d) Electrolytic refining : The impure metal is taken as anode and the cathode consists of a pure metal. Cathodes and anodes are placed in a soluble salt of the metal taken as an electrolyte. Pure metal is gradually deposited on the cathode and impurities either settle down or dissolved in the solution and collected as anode mud. Large number of metals like copper, silver, gold and zinc are purified by this method. The metal of highest purity are obtained by this method.
(e) Thermal decomposition method : In these methods, the metal is converted into suitable volatile compound which is decomposed at high temperature to give pure metal.
(i) Mond's process or carbonyl process (Purification of Ni) :
This method is used for the purification of nickel. Impure nickel is allowed to react with carbon monooxide at $60-80^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$. It gives $\mathrm{Ni}(\mathrm{CO})_{4}$, which is a volatile compound. Vapours of $\mathrm{Ni}(\mathrm{CO})_{4}$ decomposes at about $180^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ giving pure Ni ( $\approx 99.9 \%$ purity).
(ii) van-Arkel process :
It is also known as iodide refining method. This method is used for obtaining metals of very high purity. Common metals which are refined by this method are Ti, Zr, Hf, Si etc. In this method, metal is converted into a volatile compound (e.g., iodides) which is decomposed to obtain pure metal. Impurities are left behind as they do not react with $\mathrm{I}_{2}$.
$$
\text { Impure metal }+\mathrm{I}_{2} \longrightarrow \text { Metal iodide } \xrightarrow{\text { Very high temp. }} \text { Pure metal }+\mathrm{I}_{2}
$$
## EXTRACTION OF METALS ON THE BASIS OF REACTIVITY SERIES
The reaction of metals or their position in activity series can also give a great idea about extraction of metals.
## Extraction of Metals Placed Low in the Activity Series
Metals that are low in the activity series are very unreactive. The oxides of these metals can be reduced to metals by heating alone. For example, mercury is obtained from its ore, cinnabar (HgS), by the process of heating.
## Reduction using heat :
$$
\begin{gathered}
2 \mathrm{HgS}(\mathrm{~s})+3 \mathrm{O}_{2}(\mathrm{~g}) \xrightarrow{\text { Heat }} 2 \mathrm{HgO}(\mathrm{~s})+2 \mathrm{SO}_{2}(\mathrm{~g}) \\
2 \mathrm{HgO}(\mathrm{~s}) \xrightarrow{\text { Mercuric oxide }}
\end{gathered}
$$
Copper can also be obtained in a similar manner from its sulphide ore $\left(\mathrm{Cu}_{2} \mathrm{~S}\right)$.
When concentrated cinnabar ore (HgS) is roasted the ore is converted to oxide. The reduction of cinnabar ore to oxide is a combination of roasting and reduction.
## Extraction of Metals Placed in the Middle of the Activity Series
Metals such as iron, zinc, lead, copper, etc., are present in the middle of the reactivity series. These are moderately reactive metals and are usually present as sulphides or carbonates. It is easier to reduce an oxide ore as compared to its sulphides and carbonates. If the ore is not an oxide ore, it is first converted to the oxide by the process of calcination or roasting.
Roasting : Sulphide ores are converted into oxides by heating strongly in presence of excess air, to form the corresponding oxides. Sulphur impurities escape as $\mathrm{SO}_{2}$ gas. For example,
$$
\begin{aligned}
& 2 \mathrm{ZnS}(\mathrm{~s})+3 \mathrm{O}_{2}(\mathrm{~g}) \longrightarrow 2 \mathrm{ZnO}(\mathrm{~s})+2 \mathrm{SO}_{2}(\mathrm{~g}) \\
& 2 \mathrm{PbS}(\mathrm{~s})+3 \mathrm{O}_{2}(\mathrm{~g}) \longrightarrow 2 \mathrm{PbO}(\mathrm{~s})+2 \mathrm{SO}_{2}(\mathrm{~g}) \\
& 4 \mathrm{FeS}(\mathrm{~s})+7 \mathrm{O}_{2}(\mathrm{~g}) \longrightarrow 2 \mathrm{Fe}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}(\mathrm{~s})+4 \mathrm{SO}_{2}(\mathrm{~g})
\end{aligned}
$$
Calcination : In calcination, ore is heated to a high temperature in absence of air. Usually, carbonate ores or ores containing water are calcined to drive out carbonate and moisture impurities.
$$
2 \mathrm{ZnCO}_{3}(\mathrm{~s}) \longrightarrow 2 \mathrm{ZnO}(\mathrm{~s})+\mathrm{CO}_{2}(\mathrm{~g})
$$
The zinc oxide is then reduced to zinc by a suitable reducing agent such as carbon.
$$
\mathrm{ZnO}(\mathrm{~s})+\mathrm{C}(\mathrm{~s}) \longrightarrow \mathrm{Zn}(\mathrm{~s})+\mathrm{CO}(\mathrm{~g})
$$
Besides using carbon to reduce metal oxides to metals, highly reactive metals like sodium, calcium, aluminium etc. are also used as reducing agents because they can displace the metal of lower reactivity from their compounds, e.g. when manganese dioxide is heated with aluminium powder, the following reaction takes place.
$$
3 \mathrm{MnO}_{2}(\mathrm{~s})+4 \mathrm{Al}(\mathrm{~s}) \longrightarrow 3 \mathrm{Mn}(\mathrm{~s})+2 \mathrm{Al}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}(\mathrm{~s})+\text { Heat }
$$
A great amount of heat is evolved in such reactions, hence metals are produced in the molten state.
Another example of this type of the reaction is the thermite reaction in which iron oxide reacts with aluminium to give iron and aluminium oxide evolving large amount of heat
$$
\mathrm{Fe}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}(\mathrm{~s})+2 \mathrm{Al}(\mathrm{~s}) \longrightarrow 2 \mathrm{Fe}(\mathrm{~s})+\mathrm{Al}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}(\mathrm{~s})+\text { Heat }
$$
## Extraction of Metals Placed at the Top of the Activity Series
Metals such as sodium, magnesium, calcium, aluminium present on the top of the reactivity series are very reactive and cannot be obtained from their compounds by heating with carbon. This is because these metals have more affinity for oxygen than for carbon. Such highly reactive metals are obtained by electrolytic reduction of fused chlorides, e.g. sodium from sodium chloride in a "Downs cell". Similarly, calcium is obtained by the reduction of fused calcium chloride.
At cathode :
$$
\mathrm{Na}^{+}+\mathrm{e}^{-} \longrightarrow \mathrm{Na}
$$
At anode :
$$
2 \mathrm{Cl}^{-} \longrightarrow \mathrm{Cl}_{2}+2 \mathrm{e}^{-}
$$

## Prevention of Corrosion
Iron and steel (alloy of iron) are most easily protected by paint which provides a barrier between the metal and air/water. Moving parts of machines can be protected by a water repellent oil or grease layer. Covering the surface with enamel and lacquers is another method.

## NON-METALS

## Chemical Properties of Non-metals
Non-metals are more reactive with metals than with other non-metals. Generally, non-metals react with each other at a high temperature.

## HOW DO METALS AND NON-METALS REACT
In earlier classes we learnt that, noble gases have a completely filled valence shell and hence show little chemical activity. We can, therefore, explain the reactivity of elements as a tendency to attain a completely filled valence shell. Let us have a look at the electronic configuration of noble gases and some metals and non-metals.
A sodium atom has one electron in its outermost shell. If it loses the electron from its M shell then its L shell now becomes the outermost shell and has a stable octet. The nucleus of this atom still has 11 protons but the number of electrons has become 10 , so there is a net positive charge giving us a sodium cation $\mathrm{Na}^{+}$. On the other hand, chlorine atom has seven electrons in its outermost shell and requires one more electron to complete its octet. When sodium and chlorine react with each other, the electron is lost by sodium atom which could be taken up by chlorine atom. After gaining an electron, the chlorine atom gets a unit negative charge, because its nucleus has 17 protons and 18 electrons in its $\mathrm{K}, \mathrm{L}$ and M shells. This gives us a chloride anion

Sodium and chloride ions, being oppositely charged, attract to each other and are held by strong electrostatic forces of attraction to exist as sodium chloride ( NaCl ).

## Properties of Ionic Compounds
(a) Physical nature : Ionic compounds are solids and are somewhat hard because of the strong forces of attraction between the positive and negative ions. These compounds are generally brittle and break into pieces when pressure is applied.
(b) Melting and Boiling points : Ionic compounds have high melting and boiling points . This is because a considerable amount of energy is required to break the strong inter-ionic attraction.

Magnesium oxide, MgO has higher melting point than sodium chloride which is around $2800^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$. Because it stays solid at such high temperatures, magnesium oxide remains unconductive, and is used for hightemperature electrical insulation.
(c) Solubility : Electrovalent compounds are generally soluble in water (polar solvent) and insoluble in solvents such as kerosene, petrol, (nonpolar solvents) etc.
(d) Conduction of electricity : The conduction of electricity through an aqueous solution involves the movement of charged particles. A solution of an ionic compound in water contains ions, which move to the opposite electrodes when electricity is passed through the solution. Ionic compounds in the solid state do not conduct electricity because movement of ions in the solid state is not possible due to their rigid structure. But ionic compounds conduct electricity in the molten state. This is possible in the molten state since the elecrostatic forces of attraction between the oppositely charged ions overcomes due to the heat. Thus, the ions move freely and conduct electricity.

## METALLOIDS
Metalloids are those chemical elements that exhibit properties of both metals and non-metals. They behave as non-metals physically and chemically and show electrical conductivity like metals. However, they are not good conductors of electricity like metals and are known as semiconductors.
The concepts of metalloid and semiconductor should not be confused. Metalloid refers to the properties of certain elements in relation to the periodic table. Semiconductor refers to the physical properties of materials (including alloys, compounds) and there is only partial overlap between the two.
The following elements are generally considered as metalloids :
- Boron (B)
- Germanium (Ge)
- Antimony (Sb)
- Silicon (Si)
- Arsenic (As)
- Tellurium (Te)
- Polonium (Po)
Metalloids characteristics include ability to form amphoteric oxides and ability to behave as semiconductors and semimetals. The metalloids are placed between metals and non-metals in the periodic table. There are seven metalloids in the periodic table that are placed in Group 13, 14, 15, 16 and 17. They form a zig zag step line in the periodic table. They are separated from the metals and non-metals by a line called the 'amphoteric line'. Metalloids like boron, germanium, arsenic are used as dopants in semiconductor chips. Metalloids are usually brittle in nature and behave as electrical insulators at room temperature.
## Uses of Metalloids
- Boron is found in boric acid - a mild antiseptic and insect killer.
- Silicon is used in computers and other electronics and it is used to make rubbery cookware that does not stick to the food. Silicon is also used in making solar energy devices.
- Germanium is used in fiber optics.
- Arsenic can be used with gallium in semiconductors, but it is generally used as an insecticide.
- Antimony is used in batteries, antifriction alloys, cable sheathing, matches, medicines, antiprotozoan drugs, plumbing, soldering (some "lead-free" solders contain $5 \% \mathrm{Sb}$ ), and a couple of other things.
- Tellurium is used in ceramics, semiconductors, and blasting caps.
- Polonium is a very rare natural element. It is found in uranimum ores but it is uneconomical to extract it. These are used in antistatic devices and for research purposes.
Note : Boron, germanium and arsenic, all are used as a dopant in semiconductor chips.

Some metals are chemically very reactive, whereas others are less reactive or unreactive. On the basis of vigourness of reactions of various metals with oxygen, water and acids, as well as displacement reactions, the metals have been arranged in a group or series according to their chemical reactivity.
