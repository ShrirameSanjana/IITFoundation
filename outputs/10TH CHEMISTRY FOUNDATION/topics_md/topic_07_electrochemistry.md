## ELECTROCHEMISTRY
Electrochemistry deals with the study of electrical properties of solutions of electrolytes and with the interrelation of chemical phenomenon and electrical energy. It is the study of production of electricity from energy released during spontaneous chemical reactions and the use of electrical energy to bring about non-spontaneous chemical transformations.
Electricity has an important place in modern society. What constitutes an electric current? How does it flow? An electric current is a flow of particles (electrons) flowing through wires and components.
Substances which allow the electricity to pass through them are called conductors, whereas those which do not allow electricity to pass through them are called insulators.
Conductors are classified into two types based on the nature of decomposition: electronic and electrolytic.
Electronic conductors: The conductors which conduct electricity without causing decomposition of substance are electronic conductors. The flow of electricity is due to flow of electrons in the conductor.
Examples : Metals, graphite, and certain minerals.
Electrolytic conductors: The conductors which decompose when current is passed through them are electronic conductors. In these conductors flow of electricity is due to flow of ions.
Examples : Aqueous solutions of acids, bases, and salt or even fused salts.
Compounds which conduct electricity in solution or in the fused state are called electrolytes and those which do not conduct electricity are called non-electrolytes.
Aqueous solutions of electrolyte is a better conductor than fused electrolyte. Concentration of
free ions in aqueous solution is greater than in fused state, because an electrolyte dissociates to a greater extent in its aqueous solution than in fused state. Therefore, conductivity of aqueous solution of electrolyte is greater than that of fused electrolyte.
## ELECTROLYSIS
Electrolysis is the process of conduction of electricity through a chemical compound in an aqueous solutions or in the molten state accompanied by a chemical change. The process of electrolysis is carried out in an electrolytic cell.
An electrolytic cell has two electrodes immersed in a solution placed in a container. The electrode connected to the negative pole is called cathode and the one connected to the positive pole is called anode.
An aqueous solution of electrolyte contains both positive and negative ions. When electricity is passed, positive ions migrate towards the cathode and negative ions move towards the anode. A compound is converted into its respective elements at cathode by accepting electrons, and at anode by donating electrons.
## Properties of Electrolysis
(i) The phenomenon of electrolysis appears only at the electrodes.
(ii) The electrolyte as a whole remains neutral during the process of electrolysis as equal number of charges are neutralised at the electrodes.
(iii) The deposition of different ions at the electrodes takes place only when electricity passes.
(iv) If two or more positive and negative ions are present in the solution, the least active ion is discharged first at the respective electrode.

The process of electrolysis has various applications.

## CELLS
The process of eletrolysis requires chemical reaction to occur, which is carried out by using electrical energy. Similarly, electrical energy can also be generated by undergoing chemical reaction in an electrochemical cell.
Cell is a system or arrangement in which two electrodes are fitted in the same electrolyte or in two different electrolytes which are joined by a salt bridge. Cells are of two types :
(a) Electrolytic cell
(b) Galvanic or voltaic cell
## Electrolytic Cell
It is a device in which a non-spontaneous chemical reaction is carried out by using electricity or in which conversion of electrical energy into chemical energy is done.
## Galvanic or Voltaic Cell
It is a device in which chemical energy liberated during a chemical reaction is converted into electrical energy, i.e., electricity can be obtained with the help of oxidation and reduction reaction.
The chemical reaction responsible for production of electricity takes place in two separate compartments. Each compartment consists of a suitable electrolyte solution and a metallic conductor. The metallic conductor acts as an electrode. The compartments containing the electrode and the solution of the electrolyte are called half-cells. When the two compartments are connected by a salt bridge and electrodes are joined by a wire through galvanometer, the electricity begins to flow. This is the simple form of voltaic cell.

## Cell Notation
By seeing the cell notation we can easily understand reactions undergoing in electrochemical cell. The first half of the notation represents anode reaction, whereas the second half represents cathode reaction.
In the notation, the anode or oxidation half cell is always written on the left. The cathode or reduction half cell is written on the right. The two electrodes are connected by means of a salt bridge, denoted by two vertical lines.

## Standard Electrode Potential ( $E^{\circ}$ )
If the concentration of ions is unity, temperature is $25{ }^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ and pressure is 1 atm (standard conditions), the potential of the electrode is called standard electrode potential.
It is represented by $E^{\circ}$.
## Electromotive Force of Cell or Cell Voltage
The difference in the electrode potentials of the two electrodes of the cell is termed as electromotive force (EMF) or cell voltage.
$$
\begin{gathered}
\mathrm{E}_{\text {cell }}^{\circ}=\mathrm{E}_{\text {red }}^{\circ}(\text { cathode })-\mathrm{E}_{\text {red }}^{\circ}(\text { anode }) \\
\text { or } \\
\mathrm{E}_{\text {cell }}^{\circ}=\mathrm{E}_{\text {oxi. }}^{\circ}(\text { anode })-\mathrm{E}_{\text {oxi. }}^{\circ}(\text { cathode }) \\
\text { or } \\
\mathrm{E}_{\text {cell }}^{\circ}=\mathrm{E}_{\text {oxi. }}^{\circ}(\text { anode })+\mathrm{E}_{\text {red }}^{\circ}(\text { cathode })
\end{gathered}
$$

## Applications of Electrochemical Series
(a) Reactivity of metals :
(i) Alkali metals and alkaline earth metals react with cold water and evolve hydrogen and readily dissolve in acids.
(ii) Metals like $\mathrm{Fe}, \mathrm{Pb}, \mathrm{Sn}, \mathrm{Ni}, \mathrm{Co}$, etc. do not react with cold water but react with steam to evolve hydrogen.
(iii) Metals like $\mathrm{Cu}, \mathrm{Ag}$, and Au which lie below hydrogen are less reactive and do not evolve hydrogen from water.
(b) Electropositive character of metals : Electropositive character of metals decreases from top to bottom.
(c) Displacement reactions : The metal having low SRP will displace the metal from its salt solution which has higher value of SRP.
(d) Reducing power of metals : Reducing nature decreases from top to bottom in the electrochemical series.
(e) Oxidizing nature of non-metals : Oxidizing nature increases from top to bottom in the electrochemical series.
(f) Products of electrolysis : The ion which is a stronger oxidizing agent is discharged first at cathode.
(g) Corrosion of metals : The corrosion tendency increases from top to bottom.
Platinum and gold do not occur in combined state unlike other metals. They do not possess
Did You Know! tendency to form positive ions due to the high negative value of oxidation potential. Hence, they occur in free state in nature.

## Important Points about this Series
(a) Metals near the top of the series are strongly electropositive (or weakly electronegative). They lose electrons readily to give cations.
(b) Weakly electronegative metals in the upper part can displace more electronegative metals below them from their salts. For example, iron displaces copper from $\mathrm{CuSO}_{4}$ solution, Cu displaces silver from silver salt solution, silver displaces gold from gold salt solution as in photography during gold toning process.
(c) Hydroxides of metals in the upper part of the series are strongly basic and their salts do not undergo hydrolysis. On the other hand, hydroxides of the metals in the lower part of the series are weakly basic and their salts undergo hydrolysis.
(d) Metals lying above hydrogen are easily rusted. Those situated below are not rusted.
(e) Metals above hydrogen displace hydrogen from dilute acids and water.
(f) Iron and other metals above it decompose steam and liberate hydrogen.
(g) Oxides of iron and other metals below it can be reduced easily.
(h) Oxides of manganese and other metals above it are reduced when heated in a current of hydrogen.
(i) Oxides of mercury and other metals below it are decomposed on heating.

## Secondary Cell
In these cells, electrode reactions can be reversed by an external electric energy source, i.e., these cells can be recharged by passing electric current in opposite direction. Common examples are lead storage battery and nickel-cadmium storage cell.
## Lead Storage Battery
It consists of a lead anode and a grid of lead packed with lead dioxide as cathode. A solution of sulphuric acid is used as an electrolyte.
When the cell discharges, it operates as a voltaic cell.
Anode :
$$
\mathrm{Pb}(\mathrm{~s})+\mathrm{SO}_{4}^{2-}(\mathrm{aq}) \longrightarrow \mathrm{PbSO}_{4}(\mathrm{~s})+2 \mathrm{e}^{-}

Cathode :
$$

## CORROSION
Corrosion is basically an electrochemical phenomenon. The rusting of iron, the tarnishing of silver, development of a green coating on copper and bronze are some of the examples of corrosion.
In corrosion, a metal is oxidised by loss of electrons to oxygen and forms metal oxide. Corrosion of iron (which is commonly known as rusting) occurs in presence of water and oxygen (air).
Chemically, rust is hydrated form of ferric oxide $\mathrm{Fe}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3} . x \mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}$.
## Electrochemical Theory of Rusting
According to this theory, flow of electric current between separate anode and cathode bars is the reason why corrosion occurs. The ferrous ions formed by action of moisture or water move through water and come at the surface of iron object where these are further oxidised to ferric state by atmospheric oxygen and constitute rust which is hydrated iron (III) oxide.
$$
\mathrm{Fe}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3}+x \mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O} \rightarrow \mathrm{Fe}_{2} \mathrm{O}_{3} \cdot x \mathrm{H}_{2} \mathrm{O}
$$
## Factors Which Promote Corrosion
(a) More active metals are readily corroded.
(b) Presence of impurities in metals enhances the chances of corrosion. Pure metals do not corrode e.g. pure iron does not rust.
(c) Air and moisture accelerate corrosion. Presence of gases like $\mathrm{SO}_{2}$ and $\mathrm{CO}_{2}$ in air catalyses the process of corrosion. Iron when placed in vacuum does not rust.
(d) Corrosion (e.g. rusting of iron) takes place rapidly at bends, scratches, nicks and cuts in the metal.
(e) Electrolytes, if present, also increase the rate of corrosion.
